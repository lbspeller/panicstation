# What to do if… water appears around a shower screen or tub edge and seems to be getting worse quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the shower or tub immediately and shut off the water supply using the fixture valves or the home's main shutoff. If water is near electrical outlets or dripping through the ceiling below, evacuate the immediate area and disable power at the breaker panel only if you can do so safely and dry.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t continue running the water to "trace" the leak; hidden subflooring and ceiling voids can saturate in seconds.
- Don’t touch light switches, outlets, or the breaker panel if you are wet, standing in water, or if water is visibly near wiring.
- Don’t use electrical appliances like vacuums or fans while standing on a wet bathroom floor.
- Don’t apply new caulk or sealant over an active leak, as this traps moisture inside the wall and accelerates rot.
- Don’t ignore a "minor" pool if it is growing; water migration behind walls often precedes visible structural failure.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safe pause.** Exit the wet area, dry your hands and feet thoroughly, and put on rubber-soled shoes before taking further action.
2. **Isolate the water supply.** 
   - Check for **fixture shutoff valves** located behind an access panel or inside a vanity cabinet and turn them clockwise to close.
   - If fixture valves are unavailable or stuck, locate the **main water shutoff valve** for the property (usually in the basement, garage, or near the water meter). 
   - For **lever-style ball valves**, turn the handle 90 degrees so it is perpendicular to the pipe. 
   - For **wheel-style gate valves**, turn the handle clockwise until it is tight.
3. **De-energize the area if water is near electricity.**
   - If water is reaching outlets, baseboard heaters, or the ceiling below, go to your **electrical breaker panel**.
   - If the floor near the panel is dry, shut off the specific circuit for the bathroom or the **main breaker** if the circuits are not clearly labeled.
   - If the floor near the breaker panel is wet, **do not touch it**; call a qualified electrician or your power utility for an emergency disconnect.
4. **Contain the surface spread.**
   - Place heavy towels along the tub or shower edge and at the bathroom threshold to prevent water from reaching hallways or carpeted areas.
   - If you are in a multi-story building, notify the occupants below or building management immediately to check for ceiling damage.
5. **Begin the drying process.** 
   - Once the water is off and the area is electrically safe, remove wet rugs and mats.
   - Use a wet/dry vacuum only if the power is safely isolated and the device is plugged into a GFCI-protected outlet in a dry room.
   - Aim to have all porous materials (drywall, insulation, carpeting) **dry within 24–48 hours** to prevent mold colonization.
6. **Notify necessary parties.**
   - **Renters:** Contact your landlord or property manager immediately; document that you have shut off the water to mitigate damage.
   - **Owners:** Call an emergency plumber if you cannot isolate the leak or if the water is coming from behind the wall.
7. **Capture evidence.** Take clear photos and video of the water pooling, any gaps in the seals, and the condition of the ceiling in the room below before any cleanup begins.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause (e.g., door sweep failure vs. internal pipe leak) before shutting off the water.
- You do not need to begin removing tiles or drywall today unless directed by a professional.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent repair or contractor in the first few minutes.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when water spreads quickly. Stopping the flow of water and isolating electricity are the most effective ways to prevent a manageable leak from becoming a structural emergency. Taking these steps now protects the property and your safety regardless of the final repair cost.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Final diagnosis of the leak source and professional moisture mapping to check for hidden rot or mold usually requires a licensed plumber or restoration specialist.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional plumbing or electrical services. Always prioritize electrical safety; if water has infiltrated the walls or electrical system, do not restore power until the system has been inspected by a qualified professional.
