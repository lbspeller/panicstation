# What to do if… your fireplace or stove backdrafts smoke into the room and you’re unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
Stop adding fuel to the fire immediately and evacuate everyone to fresh air. Treat visible smoke or a sounding carbon monoxide (CO) alarm as a health emergency and do not re-enter until a professional confirms the area is safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep the fire burning in an attempt to "warm the flue" or "push the smoke out."
- Do not ignore a CO alarm even if you cannot see or smell smoke.
- Do not use kitchen exhaust fans, bathroom fans, or clothes dryers, as these increase negative pressure and pull more smoke into the room.
- Do not attempt to climb onto the roof to inspect the chimney while the system is hot or the room is smoky.
- Do not close the damper while there are still active embers or smoke.
- Do not assume the air is safe just because the smoke has cleared visually.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the source of the smoke safely.** Stop adding wood or fuel immediately. If using a wood stove, close the air intakes to dampen the fire. If using a gas fireplace, **turn the unit off** using the wall switch or remote.
2. **Evacuate the building immediately.** Move all occupants to fresh air outside. If you can do so without delaying your exit, open one window or door in the room to provide a pressure relief path for the smoke.
3. **Call 911 if anyone shows symptoms of exposure.** Look for headaches, dizziness, nausea, confusion, or shortness of breath.
4. **Treat any CO alarm as a life-safety event.** If the alarm is sounding, **call 911 from outside**. Do not re-enter the home until emergency responders have cleared the building with calibrated gas detection equipment.
5. **Report suspected gas leaks to the utility company.** If you smell rotten eggs or sulfur near a gas fireplace, **call your gas provider's emergency line** from a safe distance outside.
6. **Keep exhaust systems turned off.** Ensure the clothes dryer and all ventilation fans remain off to prevent the house from "sucking" more air and smoke down the chimney.
7. **Schedule a professional inspection before the next use.**
   - **Wood systems:** Contact a **CSIA-certified chimney sweep** to perform a Level 2 inspection to check for blockages, creosote, or structural flue failure.
   - **Gas systems:** Contact an **NFI-certified (National Fireplace Institute)** technician to inspect the venting, gas pressure, and burner assembly.
8. **Watch for signs of an active chimney fire.** If you hear a loud roaring sound like a jet engine or see sparks flying from the chimney top, **call 911 immediately** and stay away from the structure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause (such as a "cold hearth," animal nest, or wind loading) while the room is smoky.
- You do not need to clean soot or ash from furniture immediately; wait until the air has been cleared and the appliance is cold.
- You do not need to decide on permanent fixes like chimney caps or liners until a certified pro provides a written inspection report.

## Important reassurance
Backdrafting is a common mechanical failure often caused by simple pressure changes or temporary blockages. Leaving the room and seeking fresh air is the standard safety protocol and does not mean you have permanently lost use of your fireplace.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active smoking event. It does not replace the technical diagnostic tools required by a certified inspector to identify draft issues.

## Important note
Carbon monoxide is an odorless, colorless gas produced by incomplete combustion. Visible smoke always contains CO and particulate matter. Do not use the appliance again until it has been inspected and repaired by a qualified professional.
