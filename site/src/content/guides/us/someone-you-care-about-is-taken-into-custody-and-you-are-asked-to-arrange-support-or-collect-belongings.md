# What to do if… someone you care about is taken into custody and you are asked to arrange support or collect belongings (USA)

## Short answer
Identify the specific facility holding the person and obtain their **Booking ID or Inmate Number** immediately. Call the facility to confirm their "Property Release" protocol, as most jails require the person in custody to sign an authorization form before you can collect any personal effects.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t travel to a jail or police station without confirming the person is actually there and that the property window is currently open.
- Don’t assume all property is together; items taken at the scene of the arrest (evidence) are often stored at a different police headquarters than personal items taken during booking (jail property).
- Don’t discuss the details of the alleged crime or case on jail phone lines or in the lobby, as these areas are frequently recorded and monitored.
- Don’t attempt to bring "care packages," outside food, or unapproved clothing to the facility, as these will be rejected as contraband.
- Don’t delay pickup if the person has been transferred to state prison; many local jails discard or destroy unclaimed property within 30 days of a transfer.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the person and their official identification number.** 
   **Use the county sheriff’s "Inmate Lookup" or call the arresting agency to confirm which facility is holding them.** 
   - Ask for the full legal name used for booking. 
   - Write down the Booking Number or Inmate ID; you will need this for every interaction.

2. **Verify the specific property release requirements for that facility.** 
   **Call the jail’s property clerk and ask exactly how the person in custody can authorize you to collect their items.** 
   - Ask: "Has the inmate signed a Property Release Form naming me?" 
   - Ask: "Do I need a notarized letter or just the facility's internal form?"
   - Confirm the specific window of time (e.g., 8:00 AM to 10:00 AM) when property can be picked up.

3. **Distinguish between "Personal Property" and "Evidence" immediately.** 
   **Ask the clerk if any items (like phones or vehicles) are being held as evidence rather than general booking property.** 
   - Personal property is usually released via the jail clerk.
   - Evidence usually requires a written release from the lead Detective or the District Attorney’s office. 
   - If an item is evidence, get the Case Number and the name of the investigating officer.

4. **Prepare your identification and the "pickup kit."** 
   **Ensure you have a valid, government-issued photo ID (driver’s license, state ID, or passport) before you leave.** 
   - Bring the person’s full name, date of birth, and Booking ID written down. 
   - Bring a second form of ID (like a social security card or birth certificate) if the facility requires "two forms of identification."
   - Bring a secure bag or envelope to hold loose items like keys, jewelry, and wallets.

5. **Follow the protocol for medications carefully.** 
   **If you are asked to bring essential medications to the jail, confirm they are in the original pharmacy bottles with a legible, matching label.** 
   - Many facilities will not accept loose pills or non-prescription supplements. 
   - If they refuse the physical medication, provide the pharmacy name and doctor’s contact info to the jail’s medical unit so they can verify the prescription.

6. **Secure external property at the arrest site.** 
   **If you are collecting items from a home or vehicle not at the jail, keep a strict inventory of what you take.** 
   - Focus only on essentials: house keys, wallets, legal documents, and eyeglasses. 
   - If the police are still on-site, ask for a "Property Receipt" for anything they have seized.

7. **Establish a method for future communication.** 
   **Ask the facility which service provider they use for inmate phone calls and commissary deposits (e.g., GTL, Securus, or Access Corrections).** 
   - Note the facility’s "Site ID" or "Facility Code" if required by the app. 
   - Do not send money until you have confirmed the person has completed the initial booking process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a long-term legal strategy or hire a private attorney in the first hour.
- You do not need to find a permanent storage solution for all their household goods immediately.
- You do not need to notify everyone in the person’s social circle until you have clear information on their status.
- You do not need to decide on bail/bond until you know the exact charges and the scheduled court date.

## Important reassurance
Navigating a jail’s bureaucracy is confusing and often involves long wait times and repetitive questions. This frustration is a normal part of the process and does not mean you are doing something wrong. By focusing on the paperwork and following the facility's specific rules, you are providing the most effective support possible right now.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for logistics and property collection only. It does not cover legal defense, bail negotiations, or long-term incarceration planning, which may require professional legal counsel.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Jail policies are subject to change without notice and vary significantly between city, county, and federal jurisdictions. Always follow the direct instructions provided by facility staff at the time of your request.
