# What to do if… family members are removing belongings from a home after a death and you feel it is too soon (USA)
## Short answer
Ask for a pause: “Please leave everything here today while we make a basic record of what is in the home.” Do not start removing items yourself. Call 911 for an emergency or immediate danger; for a non-emergency property concern, contact the local law-enforcement agency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically fight over items, block exits, or grab things from someone’s hands.
- Do not use threats of police action or court action as leverage during an argument.
- Do not remove items yourself to make things feel fair.
- Do not agree to, assist with, or join in donating, selling, or discarding disputed items while the situation is unsettled.
- Do not change locks, take keys, or exclude someone who may lawfully occupy the home unless you are authorized to do so or have local legal advice.
- Do not hide, use, or dispose of financial records, identity documents, medications, or personal devices.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a pause in one sentence.** Say: “I am overwhelmed. Please leave everything here today while we make a basic record and work out who is authorized to decide what happens next.”
2. **Step away if the situation is escalating.** Move to a safer, calmer place. Call 911 for an emergency or immediate danger. For a non-emergency property concern, contact the local law-enforcement agency.
3. **Ask a calm support person to join you.** A trusted friend, neighbor, clergy member, or relative who is not part of the disagreement may help keep the pause clear and reduce confrontation.
4. **Make a simple record without arguing over each item.** From a safe position, take a video walk-through of the rooms and photos of areas where items have already been moved. Note any boxes or items that leave, who took them if known, and the time.
5. **Note important records and high-risk items.** Make a list of items such as wills, trust papers, keys, identity documents, vehicle titles, insurance records, cash, jewelry, and electronics. Place records in an agreed secure location only if everyone involved agrees or the person authorized to act approves. Do not remove medications needed by an occupant.
6. **Do not approach or handle weapons during a conflict.** Leave the area and call 911 if a weapon creates an immediate safety risk.
7. **Work out who may be authorized to make decisions.** A will may name an executor, but that person may need court appointment or authorization before acting for the estate. A trustee may control property held in a trust. Some property may be jointly owned or may pass outside probate. Ask a local probate lawyer or the appropriate court for state-specific guidance if authority is unclear.
8. **Use a temporary written rule for today.** Suggest: “Nothing disputed leaves today while authority is unclear.” If an item must move for immediate safety, photograph it, list it, and record where it went.
9. **If items continue to leave, document rather than confront.** Say once: “I am asking for a pause. I am going to get local probate guidance.” Keep a written list and save relevant messages.
10. **Ask what local non-emergency options are available if needed.** For a non-emergency property concern, contact the local law-enforcement agency and ask what options are available locally. Use 911 for an emergency or immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who receives each item.
- You do not need to settle inheritance arguments, arrange donations, plan a sale, or clear the home today.
- You do not need to resolve every question about ownership or fairness before asking for a pause.
- Detailed probate steps can wait until the immediate removals and confrontation have stopped.

## Important reassurance
Wanting a pause after a death is understandable. A short pause can reduce conflict, preserve information about what is in the home, and avoid decisions that cannot easily be reversed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probate rules, authority, ownership, and access rights vary by state and by the facts. Later decisions may need guidance from a local probate lawyer, the appropriate court, or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Prioritize immediate safety over property. Call 911 for an emergency or immediate danger. For state-specific questions about authority or estate property, seek local legal guidance.
