# What to do if… you receive an urgent message about a potential supervision breach and a deadline to respond today (USA)

## Short answer
Do not use links, payment routes, or contact details from the message until you verify it. Contact your supervising officer or the supervision office through contact information you already trust, then respond today through the official channel the office confirms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or sign into a website from the message before you verify who sent it.
- Do not pay gift cards, payment apps, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, prepaid cards, or “fees” to clear a violation or avoid arrest.
- Do not call back a number from the message unless you already know it is your officer’s or office’s real number.
- Do not send your Social Security number, ID photos, passwords, banking details, or one-time verification codes because of a “today” deadline.
- Do not reply in panic with detailed explanations, admissions, or guesses about what happened.
- Do not ignore it completely if you might really be under supervision; verify it using a trusted route.

## What to do now
1. **Save the message before doing anything else.** Screenshot the message, sender number or email, links shown, attachments shown, and the time you received it. Do not delete it yet.
2. **Identify the supervision type from your own paperwork.** Check whether this is probation, parole, supervised release, pretrial supervision, diversion, or another program. Use the office name, officer name, case number, or appointment paperwork you already have.
3. **Verify through contact details you already trust.** Use the phone number, email, portal, appointment card, release paperwork, court paperwork, or office main number you have used before. If you do not have one, search for the official court, probation, parole, or pretrial services office for your county, state, or federal district, then use the main number on that official site.
4. **Ask one narrow verification question.** Say that you received a message claiming a possible breach, give the date and time it arrived, and ask whether it came from that office. Do not explain the alleged violation until you know you are speaking to the real office.
5. **If the office confirms it is real, respond briefly through the confirmed official channel.** You can say: “I received the message today. Please confirm what you need me to do, where I should report if needed, and by what time.” Keep it factual and short.
6. **If you are told to report in person today, confirm the details first.** Ask for the exact address, the officer’s name, what time to arrive, and what to bring. Use your own transport plan and tell a trusted person where you are going if that helps you stay calm.
7. **If the contact includes a money demand, treat that as a serious scam warning sign.** Courts and law enforcement impersonation scams often pressure people with arrest threats, fake warrants, spoofed numbers, or demands for gift cards, payment apps, cryptocurrency, prepaid cards, wire transfers, or sensitive personal information.
8. **If you already paid or shared information, act on that now.** Contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, gift-card company, crypto platform, or phone provider as relevant. Save receipts, transaction IDs, messages, and screenshots.
9. **Report suspected scam contact through official routes.** Use the FTC for fraud reports. Use the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center if the contact involved online messages, links, email, websites, account access, or cyber-enabled fraud.
10. **If police contact or questioning is involved, try to speak with your lawyer before answering questions about an alleged violation.** If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your innocence in the first reply.
- You do not need to explain every missed call, appointment, payment, travel issue, or misunderstanding until the sender is verified.
- You do not need to decide today whether the allegation is fair, whether to challenge it, or what the long-term outcome should be.
- You do not need to warn everyone, change your number, or delete accounts before you know whether the contact is real.

## Important reassurance
An urgent breach message can feel frightening, especially if you are already under supervision. Pausing to verify through a trusted official route is not ignoring it; it is the safest way to avoid a scam while still taking a real supervision message seriously.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the contact, avoid scam traps, preserve the message, and respond safely today if it is genuine. Later decisions about the allegation, reporting, hearings, warrants, conditions, or legal strategy may need help from a lawyer or the supervision office.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, law enforcement, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you cannot reach your supervising officer quickly, keep trying through the office main line and document your attempts, including the date, time, number called, and who you spoke with.
