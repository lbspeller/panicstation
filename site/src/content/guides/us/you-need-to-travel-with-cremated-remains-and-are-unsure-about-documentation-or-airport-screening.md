# What to do if… you need to travel with cremated remains and are unsure about documentation or airport screening (USA)

## Short answer
Travel as though you may be asked for proof of death and cremation, keep the paperwork with you, and use a container that TSA can screen clearly. Then check the airline and, for any international route, the destination and transit country rules separately from TSA screening.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rely on a dense urn as the only travel container if it may stop TSA from screening the contents.
- Don’t pack ashes in checked luggage just to avoid questions unless the airline allows it and you accept the risk of delay, loss, or damage.
- Don’t assume TSA screening rules answer customs, public health, airline, destination, or transit-country requirements.
- Don’t open or transfer ashes at the airport unless an official or airline gives you a clear reason and you have a calm place to do it.
- Don’t scatter ashes at the destination until you have checked the local rules for that place.

## What to do now
1. **Put the strongest available documents together.**
   Keep these in your carry-on, not inside checked luggage:
   - A death certificate, certified copy, or other official proof of death if you have it.
   - A cremation certificate, cremation permit, funeral home letter, or crematory paperwork if you have it.
   - Any airline, funeral home, consulate, or destination-country instructions you have already received.
   - Paper copies, plus phone photos or scans as backup.

2. **Use a screening-friendly travel container.**
   If the ashes are in a dense urn, ask the funeral home or crematory whether they can provide a sealed temporary travel container made from a lighter material, such as plastic or wood, or another lighter-weight container they recommend for air travel. Put that sealed container inside a protective outer bag or box.

3. **Check the airline’s cremated-remains policy before you leave for the airport.**
   Confirm whether the airline allows ashes in carry-on, checked baggage, or both. Ask whether it requires specific paperwork, packaging, or advance notice. Save the policy page or written response on your phone and, if possible, print one copy.

4. **If the trip is domestic within the United States, focus first on TSA and the airline.**
   TSA’s role is security screening. The airline controls what it will carry and how it wants the remains packaged. A screenable container and accessible paperwork are the two most useful things to fix before travel.

5. **If the trip crosses a U.S. border, check each border separately.**
   Make a simple route list: departure country, any transit country, and arrival country. For each one, check whether cremated remains need a death certificate, cremation certificate, translation, consular document, customs declaration, or import or export permission. Fully cremated remains generally do not need a CDC import permit to enter the United States, but customs, airlines, or foreign authorities may still ask for documents.

6. **At the TSA checkpoint, name the item calmly and early.**
   Say: “I’m traveling with cremated remains.” Keep the container in your carry-on where you can reach it. Show documents only if asked, and give yourself extra time so a screening question does not force a rushed decision.

7. **If travel is imminent and you cannot confirm every rule, choose the safest practical default.**
   Carry all available documents, use the most screenable sealed container you can get, keep the ashes with you if the airline permits carry-on, and avoid tight connections on international routes if you can still change them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a permanent urn before travelling; a secure temporary travel container may be enough for the journey.
- You do not need to decide scattering, burial, or memorial details right now.
- You do not need to solve every future paperwork question at once; start with the flight you are taking, the airline carrying it, and any border you will cross.

## Important reassurance
This is a stressful thing to manage, especially while grieving. The most controllable problems are the paperwork you carry and whether the container can be screened, so handling those first is a sensible way to reduce the risk of a difficult airport moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps for travel screening and border-readiness only. International routes, deaths abroad, consular documents, and destination rules may need help from the airline, funeral home, consulate, customs authority, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, customs, funeral, travel, or professional advice. TSA procedures, airline policies, and border requirements can change and can differ by route, country, airport, and transit stop.
