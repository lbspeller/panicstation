# What to do if… you have sudden watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics and feel increasingly unwell (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your prescribing physician or an urgent care clinic immediately for a medical evaluation to rule out Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) or severe dehydration. If you experience fainting, confusion, or a hard/swollen abdomen, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take antimotility agents (antidiarrheals) such as loperamide (Imodium) or diphenoxylate-atropine (Lomotil) unless explicitly directed by a doctor, as these can trap toxins in the colon and cause life-threatening complications.
- Do not stop or change your prescribed antibiotic dose without medical consultation, but seek that consultation immediately before your next scheduled dose.
- Do not rely on alcohol-based hand sanitizers, as they do not kill C. diff spores; use soap and water only.
- Do not consume high-sugar drinks, full-strength fruit juices, or caffeinated beverages, as these can worsen intestinal fluid loss.
- Do not wait for symptoms to resolve on their own if you are experiencing a fever, severe cramping, or dizziness.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers for 911 or the Emergency Room.**
   - Seek emergency care if you experience any of the following:
   - Fainting, collapse, or severe confusion.
   - Visible blood or mucus in the stool.
   - High fever (over 102°F) or shaking chills.
   - Severe, persistent abdominal pain or a rigid, bloated belly.
   - Inability to keep any fluids down due to vomiting.
   - Signs of severe dehydration (no urination for 8+ hours, extreme lethargy, or a racing heart).

2. **Contact your prescribing physician or urgent care immediately if stable.**
   - **Call the doctor who prescribed the antibiotic and ask for the on-call clinician.**
   - If they are unavailable, **visit an urgent care center** for a same-day evaluation.
   - Report that you have "sudden, worsening watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics and am concerned about C. diff."

3. **Provide specific clinical details to the healthcare provider.**
   - Have the following information ready:
   - The name of the antibiotic and when you took the first and last dose.
   - The number of watery bowel movements in the last 24 hours.
   - Presence of fever, abdominal tenderness, or nausea.
   - Any recent hospital stays, healthcare facility visits, or if you are over age 65.

4. **Initiate rehydration using low-osmolarity solutions.**
   - **Take small, frequent sips of an Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)** such as Pedialyte, CeraLyte, or a generic pharmacy equivalent.
   - Aim for 50–100 mL of fluid every few minutes rather than large gulps.
   - If you feel dizzy when standing up, sit or lie down immediately to prevent falls and escalate your care level to the ER.

5. **Implement strict infection control at home.**
   - **Wash hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds** after every bathroom visit and before handling food.
   - Use a separate bathroom from other household members if available.
   - Clean high-touch surfaces (toilet handles, faucets, doorknobs) with a bleach-based disinfectant, as standard household cleaners may not kill spores.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a probiotic or supplement right now; wait for clinical guidance to avoid interfering with tests.
- You do not need to perform a deep-clean of the entire house today; focus only on the bathroom and high-touch surfaces.
- You do not need to determine the exact strain of bacteria yourself; a laboratory stool test is the only way to confirm the cause.

## Important reassurance
Antibiotic-associated diarrhea is a recognized clinical event, and feeling overwhelmed by sudden symptoms is a common reaction. While watery diarrhea can be serious, seeking professional medical assessment today is the most effective way to stabilize the situation and prevent further dehydration.

## Scope note
This guidance is for initial stabilization and urgent triage only. The risk of C. diff infection can persist for up to 90 days after finishing a course of antibiotics.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice or a diagnosis. Always follow the specific instructions of your healthcare provider. If you feel your condition is life-threatening, contact emergency services immediately.
