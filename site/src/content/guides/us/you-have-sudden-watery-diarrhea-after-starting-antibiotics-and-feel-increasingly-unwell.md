# What to do if… you have sudden watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics and feel increasingly unwell (USA)

## Short answer
Contact a healthcare professional now, such as your prescriber’s on-call line, urgent care, or the ER. Worsening watery diarrhea after antibiotics can be caused by C. diff and can lead to dehydration; if you feel severely ill or unstable, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take anti-diarrhea slow-down medicines such as loperamide or Imodium unless a clinician says it is safe.
- Do not ignore fainting, confusion, severe weakness, very little or no urination, severe belly pain, a swollen or hard abdomen, blood in stool, or repeated vomiting.
- Do not decide on your own whether to stop or continue the antibiotic if you are getting worse; contact the prescriber urgently, or use urgent care, the ER, or 911 if needed.
- Do not rely on hand sanitizer alone if C. diff is possible; wash hands with soap and water.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s antibiotics.

## What to do now
1. **Choose the safest urgent route.**
   - Call 911 now if you have fainting or collapse, confusion, severe weakness, very little or no urination, severe belly pain, a swollen or hard abdomen, or repeated vomiting with inability to keep fluids down.
   - If it is not that severe, call your antibiotic prescriber’s office now and ask for the on-call clinician.
   - If you cannot reach them promptly, go to urgent care or the ER today.

2. **Use clear words when you call or arrive.**
   - Say: “I have watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics, I feel increasingly unwell, and I am concerned about C. diff.”
   - Give the antibiotic name, when you started it, and when you took the last dose.
   - Say how many watery stools you have had in the last 24 hours.
   - Mention fever, belly cramps or pain, blood in stool, vomiting, dizziness when standing, recent hospital or nursing home exposure, age 65 or older, pregnancy, immune suppression, or serious ongoing medical conditions.

3. **Start rehydration while arranging care.**
   - Take small frequent sips of fluid.
   - Use an oral rehydration solution from a pharmacy if available, or an electrolyte drink if that is what you have.
   - If you cannot keep fluids down, feel faint, become confused, have a racing heartbeat, or stop urinating normally, escalate to the ER or 911.

4. **Reduce spread at home until you are evaluated.**
   - Wash hands with soap and water after using the bathroom and before food.
   - Use a separate bathroom if possible.
   - If you share a bathroom, wipe high-touch surfaces after use, especially the toilet flush handle, seat, sink handles, and doorknobs.
   - If C. diff is possible or confirmed, use bleach products as directed on the label for bathroom and high-touch surfaces.

5. **Be ready for same-day medical next steps.**
   - A clinician may ask for a stool sample to check for C. diff.
   - Keep the antibiotic container or prescription label nearby.
   - Bring or photograph your medication list, especially acid reducers, laxatives, water pills, anti-inflammatory pain medicines, immune-suppressing medicines, and any recent antibiotics.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know the exact cause before asking for medical help.
- You do not need to decide about probiotics, special diets, or supplements right now.
- You do not need to do deep cleaning now; focus on handwashing, bathroom surfaces, and getting assessed.
- You do not need to make a long-term plan about antibiotics today; the immediate issue is whether you need urgent assessment and fluids or treatment.

## Important reassurance
Many people get some diarrhea with antibiotics, and not every case is C. diff. Worsening watery diarrhea with feeling increasingly unwell is still a valid reason to get prompt help, because dehydration and C. diff are easier to deal with when they are assessed early.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Diagnosis, testing, antibiotic decisions, and treatment choices need a healthcare professional who can assess your symptoms and risk factors.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, treatment, or a substitute for professional care. If you feel severely unwell, are getting worse quickly, or cannot keep fluids down, call 911 or go to the ER.
