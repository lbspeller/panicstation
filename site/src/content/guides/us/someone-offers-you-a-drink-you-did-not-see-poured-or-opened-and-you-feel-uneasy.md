# What to do if… someone offers you a drink you did not see poured or opened and you feel uneasy (USA)
## Short answer
Do not drink it. Set it down, move toward other people or staff, and arrange an exit that you control.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drink it to avoid awkwardness, pressure, or conflict.
- Do not keep negotiating while staying close to the person.
- Do not let the person separate you from friends, staff, or other people.
- Do not rely on the person who is pressuring you for transport.
- Do not tell yourself that you need proof before moving away.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction simply.** Say, “No thanks.” Set the drink down and step away. You do not need to explain or debate your decision.
2. **Move toward other people and staff.** Go to the bar, a staffed area, security, or a well-lit place with other people. If you are with someone you trust, say, “I feel uneasy. Please stay with me.”
3. **Ask staff or security for a specific action.** Say, “Someone is pressuring me to drink. I need help getting away from them.” Ask to wait in a staff-visible area, get help creating distance, or be walked to your transport.
4. **Arrange an exit that you control.** Contact a trusted person, order your own ride, or ask staff to call a taxi. Keep your phone, keys, and belongings with you.
5. **Get urgent help if you feel suddenly unwell.** Tell staff or a trusted person immediately if you feel unusually dizzy, confused, very sleepy, sick, or much more intoxicated than expected. Call 911, or ask someone to call, if you are in immediate danger or there is an emergency such as trouble breathing, collapse, a seizure, or being unable to stay awake.
6. **Seek medical care promptly if you think the drink may have been tampered with.** Call 911 for an emergency, or go to a hospital emergency department. Tell the medical team, “I am worried I may have been drugged.”
7. **Ask for specialist care if there may have been sexual contact you did not consent to.** You can ask whether a sexual assault forensic exam, called a SAFE, is available and whether a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner, called a SANE, or another trained examiner can help. You do not need to decide whether to speak with police before asking for care. Reporting rules can vary, so ask the medical team or an advocate what applies where you are.
8. **Contact specialist support if that would help.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7 at 800-656-4673 and through online chat on its hotline page. The hotline page explains privacy details, including limits that may apply if identifying information is shared.
9. **Keep options open only if it feels manageable.** If keeping options open feels manageable, ask a medical professional or advocate what, if anything, to preserve rather than collecting evidence yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to speak with police.
- You do not have to keep socializing or finish the night.
- You do not have to explain yourself beyond saying that you are leaving.
- You do not have to work out exactly what was in the drink before asking for help.
- You do not have to decide any longer-term next steps tonight.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy is enough reason to decline the drink and move away. You do not need proof, a perfect explanation, or anyone else’s permission to prioritize your safety.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: moving toward safety, getting help, and keeping your choices open. Later decisions may need support from a medical professional, advocate, or other appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or there is a medical emergency, call 911.
