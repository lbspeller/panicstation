# What to do if… someone in authority hints that an opportunity depends on sexual favours (USA)

## Short answer
Create a safe pause and avoid private one-to-one contact with them where possible. You do not have to agree, reply immediately, or decide whether to report anything right now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to agree, reply, or keep the conversation going to protect the opportunity. Anything you have already said does not remove your choices.
- Avoid meeting them alone in a private place where possible.
- Do not put yourself at risk to collect more messages, get a clearer statement, or confirm what they meant.
- Do not confront them alone if you feel unsafe.
- Do not rush into quitting, withdrawing from a program, or moving out unless leaving is needed for your immediate safety.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance.** End the interaction with a simple line such as “I have to go” or “Please send the details by email,” then move to a public or safer place.
2. **Reduce private contact.** Where contact cannot be avoided, use a public setting or a normal work, school, or housing channel. Ask a trusted person to be present if that would help.
3. **Get support without committing to a report.** When you can, tell one safe person or contact confidential specialist support. RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers confidential support for sexual assault and harassment.
4. **Ask before sharing identifying details with an institution.** Workplace, school, and housing offices may have different confidentiality or reporting obligations. You can ask, “Are you a confidential resource?” and “What happens if I share names?”
5. **Keep your options open only if it is safe.** If you may want options later, keep existing messages and make a brief note of what happened somewhere you control; do not put yourself at risk to collect anything more.
6. **Treat immediate danger as an emergency.** Get somewhere safer and call **911** if you are being threatened or need immediate police, fire, or ambulance assistance.

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to decide right now whether to file a complaint, contact police, or take legal action.
- You do **not** need perfect proof or the perfect words before asking for support.
- You do **not** have to confront the person or keep communicating to clarify what they meant.
- You do **not** have to decide today whether to leave a job, program, or housing situation unless your immediate safety requires it.

## Important reassurance
Pressure from someone who has power over an opportunity can make it hard to think or respond clearly. Feeling frozen, confused, polite, or conflicted does not mean you caused the situation or gave up your choices. You deserve support and a safer pause.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about workplace, school, housing, reporting, or legal options may need confidential specialist support or independent professional advice.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
