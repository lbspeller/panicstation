# What to do if… you are asked to train someone and you suspect they are replacing you (USA)

## Short answer
Ask calmly for the training scope, timeline, and whether your role is changing, then confirm the answer in writing. Do not quit, refuse abruptly, or send an accusatory message while you are upset.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accuse the trainee or your manager of replacing you without facts.
- Do not sabotage the training, delete work, block routine access, or withhold information you are expected to provide.
- Do not take confidential files, customer lists, proprietary materials, or other company data for personal safekeeping.
- Do not send confidential workplace information to a personal email account or personal device.
- Do not sign a separation agreement, release, resignation letter, or unfamiliar document merely because you are asked to decide during the meeting. Ask for a copy and check the stated deadline.
- Do not make a sudden resignation decision while you are trying to understand the request.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the training request in writing.**  
   Ask what topics you are expected to cover, who the trainee is, when the training should happen, and which of your current tasks should be reprioritized.

2. **Ask the key question plainly.**  
   Say: “Is my role changing or being eliminated, or is this cross-training for coverage?” If the answer is verbal, send a short follow-up email stating your understanding.

3. **Make a personal facts-only timeline.**  
   Note the date of the request, who attended meetings, what was said, and any changes to your duties. Keep only documents you are permitted to keep, such as your own offer letter, job description, handbook, and performance reviews. Do not copy confidential company information into your notes.

4. **Ask for a workable training plan.**  
   Confirm the subjects, calendar time, deadlines, and which manager will decide priorities if the training conflicts with your usual work.

5. **If a role change or separation is confirmed, ask for the details in writing.**  
   Ask about the effective date, final paycheck timing, treatment of unused paid time off, benefits end date, severance if any, and the deadline for reviewing any document. These details can depend on state law, employer policy, and the documents involved.

6. **If you receive a separation agreement or release, slow the decision down.**  
   Take a copy, check the stated deadline, and do not rely on verbal promises. Consider speaking with an employment attorney in your state before signing, especially if the agreement is unclear or asks you to release claims.

7. **If you suspect discrimination, or retaliation connected to raising a discrimination concern, record the facts and check the appropriate route promptly.**  
   Note dates, comments, comparisons, and the employer process described in your handbook. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission filing time limits can apply. Federal employees and applicants for federal jobs have a different complaint process.

8. **If your employment ends, contact the state unemployment program promptly.**  
   Unemployment programs are state-run. Generally, file in the state where you worked and ask the state agency for guidance if you worked remotely, worked in more than one state, or now live elsewhere.

9. **If a broader layoff appears to be happening, check official notice information.**  
   Federal WARN rules can apply to some qualified plant closings and mass layoffs. If multiple roles are being cut, check the federal WARN information and ask the relevant state labor department whether state notice rules may apply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to interpret every clue or predict the final outcome.
- You do not need to build a full job-search plan during the first conversation.
- You do not need to negotiate a severance package before there is a written proposal.
- You do not need to confront the trainee.

## Important reassurance
A request to train someone can feel alarming, but the request alone does not confirm that you are being replaced. Written clarification, a facts-only record, and a calm response help preserve your options while you find out what is actually happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the situation becomes a termination, layoff, discrimination issue, retaliation concern, or severance negotiation, later decisions may need specialist help based on your state and the documents involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rules vary by state and by situation.
