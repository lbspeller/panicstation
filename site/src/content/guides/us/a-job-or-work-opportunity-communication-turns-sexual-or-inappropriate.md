# What to do if… a job or work opportunity communication turns sexual or inappropriate (USA)
## Short answer
Pause the contact and get to a safer moment. You do not owe the person an immediate reply, an explanation, or a meeting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree under pressure to meet alone, get into their car, or go to a private location to discuss the opportunity.
- Do not send intimate images, unusual verification photos, or personal documents that would not normally be needed for hiring.
- Do not use links, attachments, or contact details sent by the person to check whether the opportunity is legitimate.
- Do not keep negotiating while the communication is sexual, threatening, or pressuring.
- Do not feel that you must confront the person, make a report, or decide what the interaction means right now.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** End the call or close the chat. If you are with the person, move to a public place or closer to other people when you can do so safely. Call **911** if you are in immediate danger or need emergency assistance.
2. **Stop engaging for now.** You can leave the messages unanswered. If it feels safer to send a reply, keep it brief, such as: “I am ending this conversation.”
3. **Keep your options open.** If it feels safe, keep the messages somewhere private without contacting the person again.
4. **Check the opportunity independently.** Find the employer or organisation’s official website yourself and use its main contact route to ask whether the role and person are genuine. Do not use contact details provided in the sexual messages.
5. **Reduce further contact.** Mute or block the person when that feels safest, and review privacy settings if they have found your personal accounts.
6. **Use confidential support if you need it.** If this involved sexual assault or abuse, or you are unsure whether it did, RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers confidential support. Call **800.656.HOPE (4673)**, use online chat, or text **HOPE** to **64673**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report the person to an employer, platform, police department, or any other body.
- You do not need to write a full account, confront the person, or try to obtain an admission.
- You do not need to decide now whether to continue with the opportunity.
- If you later want to explore an employment-rights route, the EEOC pages below explain possible federal routes and filing deadlines. Coverage depends on the employer and situation, and federal job applicants use a different complaint process.

## Important reassurance
People may freeze, appease, or go along temporarily when someone uses power or an opportunity to apply sexual pressure. That does not make the situation your fault, and it does not mean you have agreed to further contact.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need support from a specialist advocate or an appropriately qualified professional, especially if there are threats, stalking, impersonation, or image-based abuse.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws, processes, and available options can depend on the state, employer, role, and situation.
