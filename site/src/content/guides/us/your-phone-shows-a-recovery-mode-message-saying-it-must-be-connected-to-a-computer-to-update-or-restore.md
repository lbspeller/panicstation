# What to do if… your phone shows a recovery mode message saying it must be connected to a computer to update or restore (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately assume the risk of a "SIM swap" or "port-out" scam. Connect to Wi-Fi, verify if your physical SIM is loose, and if service does not return within minutes, call your carrier from another phone to check for unauthorized transfers.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the message even if it disappears; a flickering "No SIM" alert can indicate a failing card or a deliberate hijacking attempt.
- Don't click links in "service restoration" texts or emails, as these are often phishing attempts to steal your account credentials.
- Don't factory-reset your device yet, as this may delete eSIM profiles and security logs needed to prove the fraud to your carrier.
- Don't share any one-time passcodes (OTP) with anyone claiming to be from "carrier support" who called you first.

## What to do now
1. **Find a secure location with stable Wi-Fi and a second device.** **You need an internet connection to access carrier accounts and a second phone (friend, landline, or VoIP app) to contact support while your primary line is dead.**
   - Do not rely on your own phone's cellular data, as the "No SIM" error indicates that connection is severed.

2. **Perform a rapid physical and software reset (3 minutes maximum).** **Toggle Airplane Mode on for 15 seconds then off, and restart your device completely.**
   - If you have a physical SIM card, power off the phone, remove the tray with a SIM tool or paperclip, and ensure the card is flush and free of dust.
   - For eSIM users, go to Settings > Cellular (iOS) or Settings > Network (Android) to ensure the line is still listed and toggled "On."

3. **Check for a manual carrier settings update.** **Go to Settings > General > About (iPhone) or Settings > About Phone (Android) and wait for 30 seconds to see if a "Carrier Settings Update" prompt appears.**
   - Installing an update can often resolve legitimate "Invalid SIM" or "No SIM" errors caused by network changes.

4. **Call your wireless carrier's official fraud department immediately.** **Use a different phone to call your carrier and ask: "Has there been a SIM change, eSIM reissue, or port-out request on my line in the last 24 hours?"**
   - If they confirm an unauthorized change, demand they reverse the port/swap and place a "Port-Out Lock" or "Number Lock" on the account.
   - Request an official "FCC Fraud Report" or a written incident number to document that the change was unauthorized.

5. **Secure your primary email and financial accounts in order of risk.** **Change your email password immediately and "Sign out of all other sessions" to prevent a hijacker from using your email to reset other passwords.**
   - Contact your bank and credit card issuers to place a "fraud alert" on your accounts.
   - Switch your two-factor authentication (2FA) from SMS/Text codes to an Authenticator App or Physical Security Key where possible.

6. **File an official report if fraud is confirmed.** **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan and file a report with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).**
   - If the carrier is uncooperative or the fraud resulted from their security failure, file an FCC Consumer Complaint online to trigger a formal investigation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy a new phone or a new SIM card until you have confirmed with your carrier whether the issue is hardware or account-based.
- You can wait to notify your entire contact list; focus only on your carrier, banks, and email providers for the first hour.
- You don't need to change your actual phone number unless the carrier is unable to recover your original one.

## Important reassurance
A "No SIM" error is frequently just a piece of dust or a minor software glitch. However, because your phone number is the "key" to your digital identity, treating it as a security emergency for the first 15 minutes is a safe and proactive measure that prevents significant financial loss if it is a scam.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a sudden loss of cellular service and suspected number hijacking. It does not provide long-term identity theft recovery or technical repair for damaged hardware.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information for a technical and security crisis. It is not legal, financial, or professional security advice. Terms of service and fraud protection features vary by carrier (Verizon, AT&T, T-Mobile, etc.); always use official carrier contact methods found on your billing statements.
