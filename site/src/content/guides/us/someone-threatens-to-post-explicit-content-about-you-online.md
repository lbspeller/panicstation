# What to do if… someone threatens to post explicit content about you online (USA)

## Short answer
Save the threat, secure the accounts and handles they named, and stop engaging after you have preserved the messages. If there is immediate physical danger, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay, send gift cards or cryptocurrency, send more images, or give them access to your accounts, devices, or financial information.
- Do not click links, open files, or install apps they send.
- Do not delete the messages before saving the account details, threat, demand, and timestamps.
- Do not post a public call-out while panicked; it can expose more identifiers or draw attention to the threat.
- Do not treat the claim as harmless just because you are unsure whether the content exists. Treat it as potentially real while you secure your accounts.
- Do not download or forward intimate content involving anyone who was under 18.
- Do not assume a protection tool can remove content from the whole internet.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are in immediate physical danger, are being followed, or the threat includes violence, call 911. If you may hurt yourself, call or text 988. Otherwise, move to a place where you can take the next steps without replying under pressure.
2. **Save the threat before blocking.** Take screenshots showing the sender’s account name, handle, profile URL if available, the exact threat, any demand, the date and time, and any references to your name, handles, contacts, or workplace. Keep the screenshots somewhere safe. Do not download or redistribute intimate content involving anyone who was under 18. For adult content, keep only the minimum evidence you safely need.
3. **Stop engaging after you have saved the evidence.** Mute, restrict, or block the account. Do not bargain, argue, or send a final explanation.
4. **Secure the accounts and handles they named.**
   - Change passwords if you suspect anyone else may have access.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Review active sessions and sign out of devices you do not recognise.
   - Remove suspicious connected apps.
   - Make profiles private where possible.
   - Restrict tagging, mentions, comments, and direct messages from non-contacts where available.
   - Remove public phone numbers and email addresses, and limit account discovery by phone number or email where available.
5. **Report the threatening account on each platform involved.** Use the closest available category, such as blackmail, sexual exploitation, non-consensual intimate imagery, impersonation, or harassment. Save any confirmation number or email.
6. **If content has already been posted, request removal from the platform.** The FTC says covered platforms must provide a removal-request process and remove qualifying intimate content and known identical copies within 48 hours of a qualifying request. If a covered platform has no working process or does not remove the content within 48 hours, you can report the platform through the FTC form.
7. **Choose any support or reporting route that feels useful.**
   - For specialist support with image-based abuse, use the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative Safety Center.
   - For cyber-enabled extortion or scams involving an adult, you can file a complaint with IC3.
   - You can also report the person to local police, the FBI tip form, or an FBI field office.
   - If you were under 18 when the content was created, or the threatened content involves a minor, use NCMEC’s CyberTipline.
8. **Use the age-appropriate protection tool if you have safe access to the content.**
   - If the image or video was taken when you were under 18, use NCMEC’s Take It Down service from the device on which it was originally taken when available. Do not download or ask someone to send the content just to submit it. Use the CyberTipline if you do not have the original device.
   - If you were over 18 in the image or video, the content is of you, and you have access to it, consider StopNCII. It creates a digital fingerprint on your device so participating platforms can look for matching content. It does not remove content from the whole internet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to go public or explain the situation to everyone you know.
- You do not need to confront the sender or work out their real identity tonight.
- You do not need to search the whole internet for copies right now.
- You do not need to decide on legal action right now.
- You do not need to make every account change at once. Start with the handles the sender named.

## Important reassurance
Threats like this are designed to create panic and urgency. Feeling frozen, ashamed, sick, or frantic does not mean you have done anything wrong. Saving the messages, securing the named accounts, and stopping engagement are enough for the first pause.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate harm and keep your options open. Later decisions about removal, safety planning, or legal routes may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Reporting is your choice unless immediate danger means you need emergency help.
