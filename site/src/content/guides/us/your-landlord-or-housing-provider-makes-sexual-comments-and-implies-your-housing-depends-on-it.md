# What to do if… your landlord or housing provider makes sexual comments and implies your housing depends on it (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and avoid any non-essential private contact. Where safe and practical, use written communication and choose one support route when you are ready.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet them alone or continue a non-essential private conversation while you feel unsafe.
- Do not send a rushed reply while you are overwhelmed.
- Do not agree to sexual or romantic contact because you feel pressured about your housing.
- Do not make a rushed decision about rent, moving, or confronting the person before getting advice, unless leaving temporarily is needed for immediate safety.
- Do not post publicly while overwhelmed if doing so could increase unwanted contact or risk.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call **911**. If you do not feel safe staying alone, go to a safer place or ask a trusted person to stay with you or remain on the phone.

2. **Reduce private contact.**  
   Where safe and practical, ask for tenancy-related communication in writing. A brief message is enough: “Please send tenancy-related messages in writing.”

3. **Keep one optional record.**  
   If you may want advice or make a report later, save existing messages and voicemails somewhere the housing provider cannot access.

4. **Plan around repairs, inspections, or visits.**  
   Ask for a scheduled time where possible. For any unavoidable contact, consider having a trusted person with you or on the phone.

5. **Choose one support route when you are ready.**  
   You do not need to contact every service. You can:
   - report housing discrimination to **HUD** online or call **1-800-669-9777**;
   - call the **U.S. Department of Justice Sexual Harassment in Housing Initiative** at **1-844-380-6178** or use the DOJ online form;
   - ask a local civil legal-aid organization about your options.

6. **Treat new housing threats as time-sensitive.**  
   If you receive a lockout threat, utility shutoff threat, unexpected entry request, or eviction notice, prioritize immediate safety and ask local legal aid promptly about next steps. Call **911** if there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, confront the person, or take legal action.
- You do not need a perfect written account before asking for help.
- You do not need to contact several organizations at once.
- You do not need to respond immediately to a non-urgent message.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by taking the situation seriously. You can slow the interaction down, reduce private contact, and choose the support that feels safest.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, housing changes, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
