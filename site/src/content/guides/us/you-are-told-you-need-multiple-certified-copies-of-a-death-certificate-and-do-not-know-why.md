# What to do if… you are told you need multiple certified copies of a death certificate and do not know why (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the specific documentation requirements for each financial and government institution involved before ordering. Most legal and financial entities require an original certified copy with an official seal to transfer assets or release benefits.

## Do not do these things
- Do not order informational copies for legal or financial use as they generally lack the necessary authority for asset transfer.
- Do not mail your only certified copy without confirming the recipient will return it.
- Do not assume a standard photocopy is sufficient for banks, life insurance companies, or the Social Security Administration.
- Do not pay third-party "fast track" document services before checking the official state vital records website for standard fees.
- Do not rely on digital scans or photos for institutions that explicitly require physical documents with a raised or multi-colored seal.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific agencies and institutions tied to the deceased person.** 
   - Compile a list of all bank accounts, credit unions, life insurance policies, retirement accounts (401k/IRA), and pension providers.
   - Include government entities such as the Social Security Administration (SSA), Department of Veterans Affairs (VA), and the Internal Revenue Service (IRS).
2. **Contact the top three priority institutions to confirm their proof-of-death policy.**
   - **Ask if they require an original certified copy or if they accept a high-resolution scan via a secure portal.**
   - **Ask if they keep the original document or if they will review and return it.**
   - **Ask if they require a "Long Form" certificate (listing cause of death) or if a "Short Form" (fact of death) is sufficient.**
3. **Verify if the Social Security Administration (SSA) has already received a report.**
   - **Contact the SSA at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) or a local office to check the status of the death report.**
   - **Confirm whether a certificate is needed for immediate reporting or only later for survivor benefit applications.**
4. **Locate the correct Vital Records office or authorized partner for ordering.**
   - **Identify the state and county where the death occurred.**
   - **Consult the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) "Where to Write for Vital Records" directory to find the official state agency.**
   - **Confirm the specific identity verification requirements for "Authorized" copies, which typically include providing a government-issued photo ID.**
5. **Request a specific number of "Certified Authorized" copies based on your verified list.**
   - **Order one copy for each institution that confirmed they must keep an original.**
   - **Order one additional copy to keep in a secure physical location as a permanent record.**
   - **Request these through the funeral home if they are still assisting with arrangements, as they can often facilitate the initial batch.**
6. **Gather your own identification documents before placing the order.**
   - Prepare a valid driver’s license, state ID, or passport.
   - If your name does not match the name on the record (e.g., due to marriage), have your marriage certificate or court order available to prove your relationship.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the final number of certificates for secondary accounts like utilities, subscriptions, or social media today.
- You do not need to finalize the probate filing if you are still waiting for the initial certificate to be issued by the state.
- You do not need to decide which beneficiaries receive which assets until the proof-of-death is accepted by the holding institution.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for institutions to have conflicting requirements for proof-of-death. Most families find that while the process is repetitive, institutions are following federal or state fraud prevention protocols. Difficulty in obtaining these documents is an administrative hurdle and does not mean you are doing anything incorrectly.

## Scope note
This guide covers the initial stabilization of document needs and the acquisition of official death records in the United States. It does not provide legal advice on estate distribution or tax obligations.

## Important note
Procedures for ordering death certificates and the fees involved vary significantly by state and county. Requirements for what constitutes a "certified copy" can change; always confirm the current standards with the specific institution requesting the document.
