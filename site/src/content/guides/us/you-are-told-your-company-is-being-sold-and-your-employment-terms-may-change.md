# What to do if… you are told your company is being sold and your employment terms may change (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before resigning or signing new terms. Ask for the proposed changes and any deadline in writing, then save personal copies of the employment records you are entitled to keep.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign in a panic. Resigning can affect your practical and legal options.
- Do not agree to new terms, waivers, releases, arbitration terms, or restrictions during the same meeting if you have not had time to read them.
- Do not assume the sale guarantees that your job will end or that your current terms will stay the same.
- Do not treat workplace rumors as confirmed information.
- Do not copy confidential company, customer, or coworker information.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the basic facts in writing.** Ask for the expected sale date, whether continued employment is expected, and any proposed changes to your role, work location, schedule, pay, commissions, bonuses, paid time off, and benefits.
2. **Save your personal employment records.** Keep copies of documents you are entitled to retain, such as your offer letter or contract, recent pay stubs, commission plan, paid-time-off balance, benefit summaries, and recent written communications about the sale. Do not copy protected company data.
3. **Ask for the documents and the deadline.** If you receive new terms, say that you need a copy and the date by which a response is requested. Note whether the packet includes a waiver, release, arbitration term, or restriction.
4. **Check the contact route for notices.** Confirm the personal email address, mailing address, and phone number HR has for you. Keep a personal record of the HR contact details you may need later.
5. **Contact your union representative if you are represented.** A buyer may have bargaining obligations in some circumstances. Ask your representative what to review before agreeing to any individual change.
6. **Ask whether layoffs, relocation, or closure are planned.** Ask whether WARN or another applicable advance-notice requirement may apply and request any available written notice.
7. **Check your health-coverage dates.** Ask for the final date of coverage under the current plan, the start date of any new plan, and information about COBRA and other available coverage options.
8. **Keep a short written log.** Record the date, who gave you information, the documents you received, and any response deadlines.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will stay long-term or begin a job search.
- You do not need to resolve every possible change before you have the written details.
- You do not need to argue about rumors in a meeting.
- You do not need to make a same-meeting decision without first asking for the documents and the deadline.

## Important reassurance
A sale announcement can feel destabilizing because important details may still be unclear. For now, you only need to avoid rushed decisions, get the proposed changes in writing, and keep your own records organized.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the period immediately after the announcement. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if you receive a new employment packet, a layoff notice, a severance agreement, or a waiver.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment and benefit rules can depend on your state, your documents, your workplace, and the facts of the sale.
