# What to do if… your employer tells you to relocate or change your work location (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree, resign, or make moving commitments in the moment. Ask for the work-location change in writing and review the documents that may be relevant before you respond.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign on the spot or stop attending work without communicating.
- Do not sign a relocation agreement, transfer letter, or acceptance form before reading it and keeping a copy.
- Do not accept relocation money or reimbursements before checking for repayment terms.
- Do not assume the change is permanent, non-negotiable, or governed by the same rules in every state.
- Do not break a lease, sell property, or make family-moving commitments before you confirm the details.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the change in writing.** Request the new work address, start date, expected schedule, whether the change is temporary or permanent, when a response is requested, and what the employer expects if you cannot relocate or commute.
2. **Save your own employment documents.** Keep your offer letter, any employment agreement, relevant handbook or policy sections, remote-work documents, relocation terms, and written notices about the change. Do not remove confidential company or client information.
3. **Ask for relocation-package details before accepting money.** Ask what expenses are covered, whether repayment may be required, and whether any deadlines apply.
4. **Send a brief holding response.** For example: “I received the notice and am reviewing the details and my ability to comply. Please confirm the new location, start date, and whether alternatives are available.”
5. **Ask about workable alternatives.** These may include a nearer site, a temporary arrangement, a phased change, different hours, hybrid work, remote work, or travel reimbursement.
6. **If you are union-represented, contact your union representative before responding.** Ask whether your collective bargaining agreement or bargaining process is relevant to the location change.
7. **If a medical condition affects your ability to work at the new location, tell your employer that the condition is creating a work-related barrier and ask to discuss an adjustment.** Keep a written record. Depending on the job and circumstances, options may include a schedule change, telework, hybrid work, or another effective adjustment.
8. **Ask whether the announcement is connected to a plant closing or mass layoff.** If it is, ask whether a federal or state WARN notice applies.
9. **Keep a factual record.** Save notices and note dates, names, and what was said. After a meeting, send a brief email confirming your understanding.
10. **If the job may end, ask the employer to confirm the outcome in writing.** Keep your description accurate and factual. Unemployment eligibility depends on state rules and the circumstances, so check the relevant state unemployment program promptly if your employment ends.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move your household.
- You do not need to make long-term housing, school, or family arrangements before the requirement and timeline are clear.
- You do not need to settle every legal or financial question in the first conversation.
- You do not need to resolve unemployment benefits in the first conversation, but check the relevant state program promptly if your employment ends.

## Important reassurance
A sudden location change can feel like an ultimatum. You can slow the pace, ask for the details in writing, and take the next step without making an irreversible decision today.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your state, your employment documents, any union agreement, the reason for the change, and whether a disability-related adjustment may be relevant.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. A union representative or qualified employment professional in your state may be able to help with a specific situation.
