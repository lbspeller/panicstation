# What to do if… you are removed from key duties at work without explanation and think you are being pushed out (USA)

## Short answer
Do not resign in panic. As soon as you can, make a factual record of what changed and ask your manager to confirm your current duties and expectations in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign impulsively or send a farewell message while you are upset.
- Do not accuse people of illegal conduct in a heated email; keep communications neutral and factual.
- Do not take confidential files, client information, customer data, or proprietary material for evidence.
- Do not forward workplace data to personal email or cloud storage unless you are clearly permitted to do so.
- Do not rely only on a verbal discussion; send a short factual recap so there is a record of your understanding.
- Do not sign a new job description, performance plan, or agreement in panic. Ask whether you can have time to read and review it before responding.

## What to do now
1. **Write a dated timeline and a before-and-after duties list.**  
   Record duties removed, meetings you were dropped from, access changes, who communicated each change, and any written messages.

2. **Send a calm confirming email to your manager.**  
   Ask them to confirm your current responsibilities, priorities, evaluation criteria, and when the changes took effect. If the change was discussed verbally, include your understanding and ask them to correct anything you missed.

3. **Ask whether other parts of your role are changing.**  
   Ask whether the change affects your reporting line, title, pay, bonus eligibility, schedule, goals, or performance measures.

4. **Save permitted personal employment records.**  
   Keep copies of records you are allowed to retain, such as your offer letter, job descriptions, performance reviews, pay stubs, and written recognition. Follow workplace rules and do not take confidential or proprietary material.

5. **Ask whether the change is performance-related.**  
   Ask whether there are performance concerns and, if so, what they are and what process is planned. After a meeting, send a brief factual recap email.

6. **Use the appropriate internal channel if you are not getting clarity.**  
   Contact HR, your union representative if you have one, or the stated internal complaint route. Describe what changed, when it changed, and what you want clarified.

7. **Check timing promptly if discrimination or EEO retaliation may be involved.**  
   EEOC charge deadlines can be limited. For many EEOC charges, the deadline is generally 180 calendar days from the discriminatory event and may extend to 300 calendar days where a state or local agency enforces a law prohibiting discrimination on the same basis. Age-discrimination rules can differ. Federal employees and applicants generally have a different process and generally must contact an agency EEO Counselor within 45 days. An internal grievance or complaint process generally does not pause an EEOC deadline. Different events can have separate deadlines.

8. **Note whether the change followed concerns raised with coworkers.**  
   Some activity with coworkers about pay or working conditions may be protected under the National Labor Relations Act. The NLRA does not cover public-sector employees, independent contractors, or supervisors in most circumstances. Keep a factual note of what was raised, when, and who was involved.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to file with an agency, take legal action, or leave your job.
- You do not need to decide your employer’s motive before you ask for written clarification.
- You do not need to settle the whole situation in one message.
- You do not need to tell everyone at work while you are still working out what changed.

## Important reassurance
A sudden reduction in responsibilities can be upsetting and disorienting. A calm factual record and a written request for clarification can help you preserve options without forcing an immediate decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your state, your role, your workplace policies, and whether specialist help is needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules, deadlines, and available routes can vary by state and situation.
