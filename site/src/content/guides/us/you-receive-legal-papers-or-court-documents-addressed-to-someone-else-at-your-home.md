# What to do if… you receive legal papers or court documents addressed to someone else at your home (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign or agree that you are accepting papers for the person named. If the papers came by mail and are clearly not addressed to you, keep a simple record and return the unopened mail through USPS marked “Not at this address.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not open and read mail clearly addressed only to someone else. If you opened it by mistake, stop reading and do not use or share the contents.
- Do not call, pay, click links, or give personal information using contact details from papers you have not verified.
- Do not sign anything saying you accept service for the person named.
- Do not say the person lives there, stays there, or receives mail there if that is not true.
- Do not invite a process server, collector, or anyone claiming authority inside your home, or step outside, unless you choose to.
- Do not throw the papers away if the same mistake keeps happening.

## What to do now
1. **Work out how the papers arrived.** If they came in the mail, keep the envelope with the papers. If a person handed them over or left them at the door, note the date, time, name on the papers, and any company, sheriff’s office, court, or case number shown.

2. **For mailed items not addressed to you, return them through USPS.** On the envelope, write “Not at this address.” Put it back for your mail carrier or take it to a Post Office. Do not write on the legal papers inside the envelope.

3. **If a process server, sheriff’s deputy, or delivery person comes to the door, keep it brief.** Say: “That person does not live here. I am not accepting papers for them.” Do not sign anything. If it feels safe, ask for a business card or write down the server’s name, company, phone number, and any case number.

4. **If a court name and case number are visible, consider one careful notification.** Use an official court website or official phone directory, not only the number printed on the papers. Tell the clerk: “I live at this address. The person named in case number [case number] does not live here. I am not a party to this case. Please note that this address may be wrong.” Keep a note of the date, time, and who you contacted.

5. **If any paper is addressed to you, treat it as your own urgent issue.** Do not return it as someone else’s mail. Contact the issuing court, agency, or local legal help using independently verified contact details, because deadlines may matter even when a document contains mistakes.

6. **If you suspect mail theft, tampering, fraud, or identity misuse connected to the mail, report it through an official route.** Use the U.S. Postal Inspection Service for mail-related crimes. Use USPS complaint routes for repeated delivery problems.

## What can wait
- You do not need to understand the whole lawsuit, debt, eviction, subpoena, or court process today.
- You do not need to find, warn, or contact the person named on the papers.
- You do not need to decide whether the papers were legally served unless someone claims you are responsible.
- You do not need a lawyer immediately unless your name appears, your property is mentioned, deliveries continue, or someone threatens action against you.

## Important reassurance
Receiving legal papers for someone else at your address is often an address, records, or previous-resident problem. Your immediate job is to avoid accidentally accepting responsibility, make a clear record, and point the sender or court toward the address mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate risk and stop avoidable escalation. Later decisions may need help from a local attorney, legal aid office, court self-help center, or housing adviser, depending on the document and your state.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Rules for service of process, mail handling, court notice, and deadlines vary by state, court, and case type.
