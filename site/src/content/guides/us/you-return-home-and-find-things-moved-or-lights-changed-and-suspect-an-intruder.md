# What to do if… you return home and find things moved or lights changed and suspect an intruder (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go inside to check. Move to a safer spot nearby and call 911 if you think someone may be inside, a break-in may be happening now, or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter “quickly to check” or shout into the home.
- Do not confront anyone or try to search the home yourself.
- Do not touch doorknobs, windows, curtains, or items that may have been handled.
- Do not start cleaning or putting things back.
- Do not post the situation, your address, or your location on social media.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance and cover.** Move away from doors and ground-floor windows. Go to a trusted neighbor, a nearby business, or a locked car at a safe distance if that is the safest available place.
2. **Look only from where you are.** Do not approach the home. From a safer spot, notice whether there is an open door or window, broken glass, movement inside, an unfamiliar person, or an unfamiliar vehicle.
3. **Call 911 if there may be immediate danger.** Tell the call-taker:
   - your address and that you are outside
   - what changed, such as lights or curtains
   - whether you think someone could still be inside
   - whether anyone with permission could explain it
   - any suspicious person or vehicle, including direction of travel if they leave
4. **Follow the 911 call-taker’s instructions.** Stay on the line if asked. If you cannot safely speak, texting 911 may be available in some areas, but a voice call is usually preferred when you can safely make one.
5. **If you are sure there is no immediate threat, contact local law enforcement through the local non-emergency number.** Search for your city or county law enforcement non-emergency number. If you are unsure whether there is danger now, use 911 and explain what you are seeing.
6. **Check cameras or alarm apps only from where you are safe.** Do not go back inside because an app looks quiet. If footage shows a person, a door ajar, a vehicle, or a time stamp, tell the dispatcher or responding officers.
7. **Wait for responding officers before entering.** Ask them when it is safe to go inside. If you have a monitored alarm system, you can tell the monitoring company that you are outside and that police have been called.
8. **After officers say it is safe, preserve what you can.** Take a few photos of disturbed areas before touching them. Avoid handling entry points, moved curtains, moved lights, drawers, or items that look as if someone handled them.
9. **Write down the basics while they are fresh.** Note what changed, when you left, when you returned, who had permission to enter, and anything missing, especially keys, ID, wallets, bank cards, devices, or medication.
10. **Ask for the case or report number.** Ask how to get a copy of the report if you need it for an insurer, landlord, bank, or card issuer.
11. **If cards, checks, ID, or account devices are missing, act on those next.** From a safe place, contact the bank, credit union, card issuer, or relevant account provider as soon as you can.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely a burglary.
- You do not need to do a full room-by-room inventory immediately.
- You do not need to start cleaning, repairing, or replacing locks before the home is safe and basic photos are taken, unless officers or urgent safety needs require it.
- You do not need to settle an insurance claim tonight. Once the immediate danger is over, check your policy or contact your insurer about what they need and when.
- You do not need to plan long-term security changes right now.

## Important reassurance
Noticing something “off” at home can trigger a strong fear response, even if there is later a harmless explanation. Staying outside, keeping distance, and asking for help is a reasonable first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reduce immediate risk, avoid confronting anyone, and avoid destroying possible evidence. Later decisions about repairs, insurance, landlord notices, account security, or longer-term safety may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, insurance, or other professional advice. If you believe someone may be inside or you feel unsafe, prioritize immediate safety and call 911.
