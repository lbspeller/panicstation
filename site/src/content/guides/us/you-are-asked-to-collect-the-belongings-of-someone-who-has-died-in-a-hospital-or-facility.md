# What to do if… you are asked to collect the belongings of someone who has died in a hospital or facility (USA)

## Short answer
Call the facility before traveling. Ask which office has the belongings, who can collect them, what identification or documents are required, and whether an inventory or written handover record is available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not turn up without checking the release process first.
- Do not argue with staff if they cannot release the items to you; ask what document or contact is needed.
- Pause before using the person’s cards or accounts, even for expenses connected with the pickup. Authority can depend on the account and circumstances.
- Do not discard, distribute, or hand over important items immediately.
- Do not assume every item is stored in the same place.
- Do not assume items can be released immediately if a medical examiner, coroner, or law-enforcement agency is involved.

## What to do now
1. **Call the facility’s main number and ask which office handles belongings.**  
   The office name varies. It may be a nursing unit, property office, security office, patient-relations office, or decedent-affairs office. Ask for the direct number and opening hours.

2. **Ask what you need to bring before you travel.**  
   Ask:
   - who is allowed to collect the items,
   - what identification is required,
   - whether any authorization document is required,
   - whether an appointment is needed,
   - where to go when you arrive.

3. **Ask where the items are currently held.**  
   Ask whether anything:
   - is on the unit,
   - is in a safe or security office,
   - went with the person to a funeral home,
   - went to a medical examiner or coroner,
   - was transferred to a law-enforcement agency.

4. **Ask for an inventory or written handover record if one is available.**  
   Check the items before leaving and make a simple list yourself. Look for the phone, wallet or purse, cash, cards, keys, jewelry, glasses, hearing aids, dentures, clothing, and documents. Ask whether any government-issued identification documents are being handled separately.

5. **Ask what to do if something is missing.**  
   Get the name or office number for the next contact. Ask whether the missing item may be in separate storage or with a funeral home, medical examiner, coroner, or law-enforcement agency.

6. **Set medications aside and ask before taking equipment.**  
   Do not give leftover medication to anyone else. Ask staff whether medication should stay with the facility. If medication is released to you, keep it separate until you can follow official disposal guidance. Ask whether any wheelchair, oxygen equipment, or other item belongs to the facility or another provider.

7. **Ask how long the facility will hold anything you cannot collect today.**  
   Ask whether there is a pickup deadline and what happens to unclaimed items.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what to keep, donate, or distribute.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements during the pickup.
- You do not need to start extra estate paperwork solely for this pickup until the releasing office tells you what it requires.
- You do not need to deal with bank accounts, cards, subscriptions, or other account changes while collecting the items.
- You do not need to dispose of released medication during the pickup. Keep it separate and deal with it safely later.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask staff to slow down and explain the handover process. A short written list can reduce confusion when the situation feels overwhelming.

## Scope note
These are first steps for collecting belongings and keeping a clear record. Questions about estate authority, missing property, or later account changes may need help from an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, facility policy, and whether a medical examiner, coroner, funeral home, or law-enforcement agency is involved.
