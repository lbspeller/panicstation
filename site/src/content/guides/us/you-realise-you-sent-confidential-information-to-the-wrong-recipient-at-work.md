# What to do if… you realise you sent confidential information to the wrong recipient at work (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately cease all deletion or modification of data described in the notice and wait for IT or Legal to confirm that automated retention policies for your account have been suspended under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37(e).

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete, archive, or "clean up" any emails, chat logs, or files, even if they appear redundant or irrelevant.
- Do not edit, rename, or save-as existing files, as this alters critical metadata such as "Last Modified" dates.
- Do not use "disappearing message" or "ephemeral" settings in apps like Slack, Teams, or Signal for work-related topics.
- Do not forward work documents to personal email accounts or upload them to personal cloud storage (e.g., personal Dropbox or iCloud).
- Do not attempt to "self-collect" or export data to a USB drive unless specifically instructed by your legal department.
- Do not discuss the contents of the hold notice with colleagues or in group chats unless authorized to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Suspend all manual and automated deletion routines immediately.**  
   Stop emptying your "Deleted Items" or "Trash" folders and disable any personal mailbox cleanup rules or auto-archive scripts you have created.

2. **Acknowledge the notice through the official channel provided.**  
   If the notice was sent via a platform like Microsoft Purview or a dedicated legal hold software, use the "Acknowledge" button or link provided to document your compliance.

3. **Identify all potential data locations without moving the files.**  
   Create a mental or private list of where you store relevant work:
   - Primary email (Outlook, Gmail)
   - Collaboration tools (Teams, Slack, Zoom chats)
   - Cloud storage (OneDrive, Google Drive, SharePoint)
   - Local storage (Desktop, Downloads, Documents folders)
   - Physical records (Notebooks, printed memos)

4. **Confirm with IT or Legal that a "Platform Hold" is active.**  
   Ask the designated contact if a system-level hold (such as Microsoft Purview eDiscovery or Google Vault) has been applied to your account to ensure automated company-wide retention policies are overridden.

5. **Disclose the existence of work data on personal devices if applicable.**  
   If you have used a personal phone for work texts or a personal computer for work files, inform the legal contact today; do not delete these items, but do not move them to company systems until instructed on the proper forensic procedure.

6. **Flag upcoming "data-loss events" to your legal team.**  
   Notify the contact person if you are scheduled for a laptop refresh, if your mailbox is near its storage quota, or if you are planning to leave the company in the near future.

7. **Maintain "In-Place" preservation for all versions of documents.**  
   Leave all drafts and previous versions of files exactly where they are; modern discovery tools are designed to capture "in-place" data without the user moving it.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to review every document to determine its relevance; the legal team or specialized software will perform that filtering later.
- You do **not** need to categorize or organize the files into new folders.
- You do **not** need to explain why certain documents exist unless specifically interviewed by counsel.
- You do **not** need to worry about routine work unrelated to the hold; you can continue normal business operations as long as you do not delete data related to the specific matter.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a legal hold notice is a standard procedural step in American corporate law and does not necessarily imply wrongdoing by you or the company. It is a protective measure required by federal rules to ensure that if a dispute proceeds, the facts remain available for all parties.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only to prevent "spoliation" (the loss or destruction of evidence). It does not cover the "collection" or "production" phases of discovery, which require specific technical protocols.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Failure to comply with a legal hold can result in "adverse inference" instructions from a judge or workplace disciplinary action. If the hold conflicts with your daily duties or involves highly sensitive personal information, contact your employer's legal department or your own private counsel for specific guidance.
