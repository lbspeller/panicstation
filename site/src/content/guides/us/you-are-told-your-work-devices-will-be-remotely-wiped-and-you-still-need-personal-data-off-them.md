# What to do if… you are told your work devices will be remotely wiped and you still need personal data off them (USA)

## Short answer
Document the posting immediately and request a private meeting with your manager to clarify if the listing affects your current position. Do not resign or send confrontational messages until you have received a direct explanation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit immediately — resigning may disqualify you from unemployment benefits or severance pay.
- Do not record meetings secretly in "all-party consent" states (CA, FL, IL, MD, MA, MI, MT, NH, NV, PA, WA) as this can be a felony.
- Do not download, email, or print confidential company data or client lists to personal devices.
- Do not confront coworkers or post details of the situation on social media.
- Do not sign any "voluntary resignation" or "severance release" documents during the first meeting.
- Do not assume the listing is definitely your replacement; it may be an expansion, backfill for a different department, or a template error.

## What to do now
1. **Capture the full job advertisement safely.** Take a screenshot or save a PDF of the listing using a personal device, ensuring the date, URL, and job details (salary range, duties, location) are visible.
2. **Review your state-specific employment rights.** Determine if you are in a "good cause" jurisdiction or an "at-will" state.
   - If you are in Montana, your employer generally needs a valid reason to fire you after your probationary period ends.
   - In all other states, you are likely "at-will," meaning you can be let go for any reason that is not illegal (discrimination, retaliation, or whistleblowing).
3. **Compare the ad to your official job description.** Identify specific overlaps in reporting lines, budget authority, or specialized tasks.
4. **Request a clarification meeting with your manager or HR.** State clearly: "I came across a job posting for [Title] that matches my current duties. Can you clarify if this listing relates to my role or a new position?"
5. **Request a minimum review period for any termination paperwork.** If told you are being replaced, do not sign immediately. 
   - Federal law (OWBPA) requires workers age 40+ to be given 21 days (individual) or 45 days (group) to review severance agreements that waive age-discrimination claims.
   - New York law (2026) requires a 21-day review period for all severance agreements regardless of age.
6. **Follow up the meeting with a neutral summary email.** Send a brief recap to the manager: "Per our meeting today, I understand the posting I saw is for [reason given] and that my current role is [impacted/not impacted]."
7. **Check the WARN Act public database for your state.** If your company is large, check if a "Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification" has been filed, which requires 60 to 90 days' notice for mass layoffs.
8. **Contact federal agencies if you suspect illegal retaliation.** If you believe the replacement is due to a protected activity (reporting safety issues, discussing wages, or discrimination), contact the relevant body:
   - **EEOC (Discrimination):** 1-800-669-4000.
   - **NLRB (Protected Concerted Activity):** 1-844-762-NLRB.
   - **OSHA (Safety Whistleblowing):** 1-800-321-OSHA.
9. **Maintain your current work output.** Continue performing all duties to standard to prevent the employer from citing "poor performance" or "misconduct" as a reason for immediate termination.

## What can wait
- You do not need to update your resume or start applications in the next few minutes; wait for the clarification meeting.
- You do not need to decide whether to hire an attorney before you know if your employment is actually ending.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your colleagues or professional network today.
- You do not need to clear out your desk or return company property until a termination date is finalized.

## Important reassurance
Finding a job posting that looks like your own is a common source of workplace shock and confusion. This reaction is a normal response to a perceived threat to your livelihood. Taking a moment to pause and gather facts before speaking often results in more options and better protections for your future.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Laws regarding "at-will" exceptions and severance review periods change frequently; specific legal advice should be sought if an adverse action occurs.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including recording consent and severance requirements, vary significantly by state. If you believe you are being targeted for an illegal reason, consult a qualified employment attorney in your jurisdiction.
