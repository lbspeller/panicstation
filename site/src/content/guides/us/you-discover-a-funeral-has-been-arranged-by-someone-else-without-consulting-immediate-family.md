# What to do if… you discover a funeral has been arranged by someone else without consulting immediate family (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the funeral home immediately to declare a formal dispute and request a **dispute hold** to pause all irreversible actions—especially cremation—until the legal **Right of Disposition** is verified under your state’s specific priority hierarchy.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that being "immediate family" grants automatic authority; legal priority is strictly defined by state law (typically: Designated Agent, Spouse, Adult Children, then Parents).
- Do not sign any authorization forms or "release of remains" documents until you have verified who holds the legal Right of Disposition.
- Do not rely on verbal promises from the funeral home; request all confirmation of a "hold" or "pause" in writing or via email.
- Do not ignore the clock; cremation and burial can often happen within 48 to 72 hours of the death.
- Do not attempt to pay the funeral home to "buy back" control; payment does not establish legal authority and may complicate the dispute.

## What to do now
1. **Call the funeral home and request an immediate dispute hold on all remains.**  
   **State clearly that there is a contested Right of Disposition and that you are an equal or higher-priority relative under state law.**  
   - Ask for a written confirmation that all irreversible steps (cremation, embalming, burial, or transport) are paused.
   - Request a copy of the General Price List (GPL) to monitor any "sheltering" or "refrigeration" fees added during the delay.

2. **Identify the "Authorizing Agent" the funeral home is currently following.**  
   **Ask the funeral director specifically who signed the contract and what legal relationship they claimed to the deceased.**  
   - Ask if a "Designated Agent for Disposition" form was provided.
   - Determine if the funeral home is acting on the instructions of a single child, a distant relative, or a non-family member.

3. **Locate any written "Appointment of Agent for Disposition" or similar document.**  
   **Search the decedent's immediate physical effects, safe, or digital files for a specific form naming a funeral representative.**  
   - Look for a "Funeral Planning Declaration" or a "Disposition Directive."
   - Note that in many states, this specific document supersedes the Next of Kin (NOK) hierarchy and even the Executor of the Will.

4. **Review your state’s Right of Disposition hierarchy to confirm your standing.**  
   **Verify where you fall in the standard legal order: (1) Designated Agent, (2) Surviving Spouse, (3) Majority/All Adult Children, (4) Parents, (5) Siblings.**  
   - If you and the other person are in the same tier (e.g., both are adult children), inform the funeral home that you do not consent to the current plans.
   - Many states/funeral homes require *unanimous* consent from all members of a tier before proceeding with cremation.

5. **Submit a written "Notice of Dispute" to the funeral home manager.**  
   **Send a brief, factual email or hand-delivered letter stating your legal relationship to the deceased and your formal objection to the current arrangements.**  
   - This creates a paper trail that protects the funeral home from liability if they pause, and places them on notice of potential liability if they proceed without your consent.

6. **Contact your state’s Funeral Service Licensing Board if the home refuses to pause.**  
   **Call the state regulator to ask for an emergency intervention or to verify if the funeral home is following state-mandated dispute protocols.**  
   - Most states allow funeral homes to refrigerate remains at the family's expense while a dispute is settled by a court or mutual agreement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the final bill or payment details while the Right of Disposition is in dispute.
- You do not need to decide on the location of the service or the type of casket/urn until authority is established.
- You do not need to resolve estate or inheritance issues; these are legally separate from the right to handle the body.
- You do not need to communicate with the person who made the unauthorized arrangements if a funeral director is mediating the hold.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard industry practice for funeral homes to pause services when a family dispute arises. They have a strong legal incentive to avoid "wrongful cremation" or "wrongful burial" lawsuits. Asking for a hold is a protective measure for both your family and the funeral provider, and it does not mean you are being difficult or disrespectful.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps to pause irreversible funeral actions and verify legal authority. It does not cover probate court proceedings or the long-term resolution of estate disputes.

## Important note
The Right of Disposition is governed by state-specific statutes in the USA. If equal-priority relatives (such as four siblings) cannot reach a consensus, a funeral home may require a court order from a probate judge before proceeding.
