# What to do if… you find standing water in your basement or crawl space after heavy rain (USA)

## Short answer
Keep everyone out of the basement or crawl space until electricity, gas, and structural hazards are checked from a dry, safe place. If you cannot shut power off without stepping into water, do not enter; call an electrician or ask the utility how to have service disconnected safely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water if outlets, appliances, wiring, extension cords, or the breaker panel may be wet.
- Do not turn power on or off, unplug items, or use corded tools while standing in water or on wet flooring.
- Do not use a generator, gasoline pump, pressure washer, grill, or other fuel-burning equipment inside the house, basement, garage, porch, or within 20 feet of any door, window, or vent.
- Do not pump out several feet of water quickly while water outside is still high or the water level is rising.
- Do not let children or pets into the basement or crawl space.
- Do not throw damaged items away before taking photos or video, unless keeping them creates an immediate safety hazard.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area off-limits.** Close the basement or crawl space access if you can, and keep people and pets away from the water.

2. **Check for hazards from outside the water.** If you smell gas, hear buzzing or crackling, see sparks, see sagging floors or walls, or feel the building may be unsafe, leave the home and call 911. If you smell gas or suspect a leak, also contact the gas utility or fire department from outside.

3. **Make electricity safe before entering.**
   - If you can reach the main breaker or fuse box from a completely dry, safe spot, turn the power off.
   - If reaching it means stepping into water or touching wet equipment, do not try. Call an electrician, or ask the utility how to have service disconnected safely.
   - Do not turn power back on until wet electrical equipment and wiring have been checked by a qualified electrician.

4. **Stop easy water sources only if you can do it safely.**
   - If a plumbing break may be adding water, shut off the main water valve only if you can reach it without wading.
   - If gutters or downspouts are dumping water against the foundation and you can redirect them from outside without standing in floodwater, do that.
   - Move small valuables up from dry edges only if you do not have to enter standing water.

5. **Document the damage before cleanup.** Take photos or video of the water depth, water line, entry points, walls, floors, furnace, water heater, outlets, stored items, and any damaged belongings. Keep receipts for pumps, fans, cleanup supplies, contractors, and emergency mitigation.

6. **Contact the right people early.**
   - If you own the home, contact your homeowners insurer and, if you have a separate flood policy, your flood insurer or agent.
   - If you rent, contact the landlord or property manager and document what you reported.
   - Ask what they want photographed, what emergency work is allowed, and whether they require specific cleanup or mitigation contractors.

7. **Remove water cautiously only after the immediate hazards are controlled.**
   - If there are only shallow puddles and the power situation is safe, use non-corded tools or equipment only as directed and keep yourself out of contaminated water where possible.
   - If the water is several feet deep, do not rush to drain it all at once. Official flood cleanup guidance warns that removing deep basement water too quickly can contribute to wall or floor cracking or collapse.
   - If you are unsure whether the outside water level has dropped or whether the structure is stable, wait and get professional help.

8. **Treat the water as contaminated unless you are sure it is clean.**
   - Wear waterproof boots, gloves, and eye protection if you must be near wet materials.
   - Avoid splashing water onto your face, and wash hands after contact with wet items.
   - If you suspect sewage backup, strong chemical odor, fuel, or unknown debris, keep everyone out and contact a qualified cleanup service or local public health authority.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause today, such as grading, gutters, foundation cracks, groundwater, or sump pump failure.
- You do not need to choose a permanent waterproofing, drainage, or remodeling plan right now.
- You do not need to start demolition before the area is safe and documented, unless a professional tells you an immediate removal is needed for safety.
- You do not need to decide the final insurance value of losses in the first call; start the report, keep records, and ask what the next step is.

## Important reassurance
Standing water after heavy rain is upsetting, but the first priorities are simple: keep people out, avoid electrical and gas hazards, document the damage, and slow the cleanup down enough to avoid making things worse. You can handle the cause, repairs, and prevention decisions later.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Electrical work, structural concerns, sewage cleanup, mold prevention, drying, insurance claims, and repairs may need licensed professionals, qualified cleanup workers, insurer guidance, or local code advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, engineering, electrical, remediation, or other professional advice. If electrical equipment has been wet, structural damage is possible, or sewage or chemical contamination may be present, prioritize professional help and follow local emergency or public health instructions.
