# What to do if… your bike lights will not turn on and you realise before it gets dark (USA)

## Short answer
Secure an alternative form of transportation or repair the lighting system immediately; riding after sunset without a white front light and red rear visibility is a violation of state laws and creates an immediate collision risk.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ride after sunset assuming streetlights or reflectors provide sufficient visibility to motorists.
- Don’t attempt to hold a handheld flashlight or phone light while operating the handlebars.
- Don’t assume "flashing" mode is legally compliant for a front light in all jurisdictions (some require solid beams).
- Don’t move to unlit side streets to avoid detection; these often have lower sightlines for turning vehicles.
- Don’t attempt to disassemble sealed LED housings or perform electrical repairs while in transit.

## What to do now
1. **Move the bicycle completely off the roadway to a brightly lit area.**  
   Confirm the light is actually non-functional rather than obscured by bags, brackets, or a "travel lock" mode (often triggered by holding the power button for 3 - 5 seconds).
2. **Execute a physical connection check.**  
   - **Battery units:** open the compartment to ensure batteries are seated against the terminals and free of corrosion or moisture.  
   - **USB / Rechargeable units:** check for a charging indicator light when plugged into a power bank; ensure the charging port is free of debris.  
   - **Mounting:** ensure the light is securely clicked into the bracket and not angled downward toward the tire.
3. **Verify the presence of "fail-safe" reflectors.**  
   Check for a CPSC-compliant red rear reflector and side-visibility (wheel or pedal) reflectors. Under 2026 standards, these are often mandatory regardless of whether you have active LEDs.
4. **Determine the local sunset time and your remaining travel time.**  
   If your estimated arrival time is within 15 minutes of sunset and lights remain non-functional, cease riding and switch to an alternative method.
5. **Utilize public transit or ride-hail services.**  
   - **Bus / Rail:** verify if the local agency allows bicycles at this hour (some have peak-hour restrictions).  
   - **Ride-hail:** request an "XL" or "Pet" vehicle type to ensure adequate trunk space, and message the driver immediately to confirm they can accommodate a bicycle.
6. **Walk the bicycle on the sidewalk or pedestrian path.**  
   If the distance is short, walking the bike is legally safer than riding unlit on the road. Stay on sidewalks where permitted.
7. **Contact a local bike shop if within operating hours.**  
   Many shops stock "emergency" clip-on lights or replacement batteries that can be installed in seconds to maintain legal compliance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose internal circuitry or battery degradation issues now.
- You do not need to report the equipment failure to any agency.
- You do not need to purchase a permanent, high-end replacement system immediately if a temporary light is available.

## Important reassurance
Equipment failure is a standard part of bicycle commuting. Realizing the issue before darkness falls allows for a controlled transition to a safer plan. Choosing not to ride unlit is a standard safety protocol, not a failure of preparation.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and transportation alternatives for a lighting failure detected before dark. It does not cover long-term maintenance or legal defense for citations already received.

## Important note
Bicycle lighting requirements are governed by state statutes and local ordinances. Most states require a white front light visible from 500 feet and a red rear reflector or light visible from 300 - 600 feet. As of 2026, many jurisdictions and updated CPSC standards mandate specific lateral (side) visibility and require e-bikes to operate rear lights at all hours. Always assume that a lack of functional lighting makes roadway operation illegal after sunset.
