# What to do if… you receive an official request to update your address urgently and you are unsure what happens if you do not (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the request using an official agency website or known phone number, then update your address directly through that agency's secure portal to avoid scams and legal penalties.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click links or scan QR codes in an unsolicited email or text message claiming to be from a government agency.
- Don't pay more than $1.25 for a USPS address change; sites charging $40 or more are often third-party "processing" scams.
- Don't provide your Social Security number or birth certificate copies until you have independently verified the recipient is a legitimate government official.
- Don't ignore mail from a court or supervision office (probation/parole), as failing to update your address can lead to warrants or "absconder" status.
- Don't assume that forwarding your mail through USPS automatically updates your address with the DMV, IRS, or USCIS.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific agency and the requested action.** 
   - Note whether the sender is the U.S. Postal Service (USPS), Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV), U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS), or a local court.
2. **Verify the request through an independent official channel.** 
   - Search for the agency’s official website ending in ".gov" to find their "Change of Address" page.
   - Call the agency’s publicly listed phone number rather than any number provided in the urgent message.
3. **Check legal reporting deadlines if you are a non-citizen.** 
   - Most non-citizens must report a change of address to USCIS within 10 days of moving using Form AR-11.
4. **Notify your supervision officer immediately if you are on probation or parole.** 
   - Federal standard conditions require notifying the probation office at least 10 days before a planned move or within 72 hours of an unplanned change.
5. **Report suspected address-change scams to the authorities.** 
   - If the request asks for a high fee or seems fraudulent, report it to the U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) at uspis.gov/report.
6. **Submit a formal USPS Change of Address if you have actually moved.** 
   - Complete the process at moversguide.usps.com; expect a $1.25 identity verification fee if done online. 
   - If you prefer not to pay or provide a card, visit a local Post Office and fill out PS Form 3575 for free.
7. **Document your verification attempt.** 
   - Save a screenshot of the official confirmation page or keep a log of the date, time, and name of the official you spoke with on the phone.

## What can wait
- You do not need to update every commercial subscription or retail account in the next few minutes.
- You do not need to respond to the original suspicious message once you have used an official channel.
- You do not need to hire a lawyer to update your address unless you are already in an active, contested legal proceeding.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an urgent notice about address records often triggers immediate stress, especially when it mentions legal consequences. However, most official systems provide clear, secondary ways to verify your status. Taking the time to use a known, official government portal is the standard way to protect your identity and your legal standing.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and verification steps for official address requests. It is intended to prevent identity theft and missed legal notifications but does not replace the specific instructions of a court order or immigration attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Timelines and requirements vary significantly between state agencies (like the DMV) and federal agencies (like the IRS). If you are under court supervision, a failure to report an address change can result in immediate legal action; always prioritize direct contact with your assigned officer.
