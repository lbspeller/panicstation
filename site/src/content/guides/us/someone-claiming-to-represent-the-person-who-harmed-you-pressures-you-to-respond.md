# What to do if… someone claiming to represent the person who harmed you pressures you to respond (USA)

## Short answer
You do not have to reply immediately. Move to a safer pause, avoid engaging while you feel pressured, and contact confidential support. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not get drawn into a back-and-forth conversation or try to explain your side.
- Do not agree to a private call, meeting, or informal conversation while you feel pressured.
- Do not click links or open attachments in an unexpected message.
- Do not share your address, workplace, daily routine, private messages, passwords, verification codes, or copies of identification.
- Do not use a phone number, email address, or link from the message itself to verify who contacted you.
- Do not make major legal or personal decisions while you feel rushed.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause first.** Move somewhere you feel safer, step away from the conversation, and call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
2. **Do not reply while you feel pressured.** If it feels safe, keep the original message, voicemail, email, or call log and make a copy.
3. **Contact confidential support.** RAINN can provide confidential sexual-assault support, and the Office for Victims of Crime directory can help you find local victim services.
4. **Tell an existing official contact if you have one.** If there is already a criminal-justice, school, campus, or workplace process, you may tell the official contact you already have that you received unexpected pressure. An advocate can help you decide whether to do this.
5. **Treat the person’s identity as unverified.** If you need to check who contacted you, use publicly listed contact details, not details supplied in the message. A state lawyer-licensing directory may help you check whether a person is licensed, but it does not confirm why they contacted you.
6. **Choose the safest way to reduce further contact.** You may mute, filter, or block the sender after keeping a copy if doing so feels safer.
7. **Consider local law enforcement for safety concerns.** If the contact includes threats, repeated unwanted messages, or pressure that makes you fear for your safety, you may contact local law enforcement. Call 911 if the danger is immediate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write the perfect response.
- You do not need to agree to a call or meeting.
- You do not need to decide whether the contact is a scam, intimidation, or a genuine request before asking for support.
- Longer-term legal options and personal decisions can wait unless you have an urgent safety concern.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken, frozen, or unsure after an unexpected message is understandable. You are allowed to pause, get support, and avoid making decisions while someone is pressuring you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilize the moment and reduce immediate pressure. Later decisions may depend on your safety, your location, and whether there is an existing official process, and may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
