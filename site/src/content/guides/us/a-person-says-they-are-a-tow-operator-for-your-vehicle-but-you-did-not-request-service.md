# What to do if… a person says they are a tow operator for your vehicle but you did not request service (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe position, lock your vehicle or home, and verify the operator’s dispatch independently through a trusted number you look up yourself. If the operator is aggressive, refuses to leave, or begins hooking up the vehicle without your consent, call 911 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don't hand over your keys, wallet, driver's license, or vehicle registration to an unverified person.
- Don't provide your insurance policy number or lender information to an unsolicited operator.
- Don't sign any "towing authorization" or "release" forms that have blank lines or missing fee schedules.
- Don't pay any amount—cash, card, or digital transfer—to "stop the tow" on the spot.
- Don't physically place yourself between the tow truck and your vehicle or attempt to block the lift.
- Don't enter the tow truck or follow the driver to an unverified secondary location.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a physical barrier between yourself and the operator.**
   - If you are in the vehicle: Lock all doors, roll up windows, and remain inside.
   - If you are at home: Stay inside and communicate through a closed door or window.
   - If you are in a public lot: Move to a well-lit area with other people or into a nearby business.

2. **State your refusal clearly and cease further negotiation.**
   - **Inform the operator that you did not request service and are currently verifying the situation with authorities.**
   - Do not engage in an argument regarding the "legitimacy" of their presence; simply state that you will not authorize a tow until you have independent confirmation.

3. **Call 911 if the situation meets emergency criteria.**
   - **Request police assistance if the driver is using threats, refuses to leave your property, or is actively hooking up your vehicle against your will.**
   - Advise the dispatcher that you believe you are being targeted by a "bandit tow" or predatory towing scam.

4. **Verify the dispatch through an official source you find yourself.**
   - **Call your insurance provider, roadside assistance app, or the police non-emergency line using a number from your own records.**
   - Do not use a phone number provided by the tow driver or call a number they have pre-dialed on their own phone.
   - Ask your provider for the specific name of the company they dispatched and the truck's unit number.

5. **Check for mandatory commercial markings from a distance.**
   - **Look for a company name, city, state, and a US DOT (Department of Transportation) number on both sides of the truck.**
   - Legitimate commercial tow trucks in most states are required to display this information permanently; temporary magnetic signs are a significant red flag.

6. **Confirm if law enforcement ordered the tow.**
   - **Ask for the name of the officer or the agency that allegedly requested the tow if the driver claims police dispatch.**
   - If an officer is on-scene, speak to them directly to confirm the tow company is on the official "rotation list" for that jurisdiction.

7. **Verify repossession identity without physical interference.**
   - **Ask the agent for their name, the repossession company name, and the name of the lending institution if they claim the action is a legal repossession.**
   - Note that while repossession agents can often take a car without notice, they are legally prohibited from "breaching the peace," which includes using physical force or threats against you.
   - Call your lender’s customer service department immediately to verify if a repossession order is active.

8. **Document the interaction for later reporting.**
   - **Take photos or video of the truck’s license plate, the driver’s face (from a safe distance), and any company logos.**
   - Write down the exact time, the location, and what the driver said to you.

## What can wait
- You do -not- need to determine if this is a scam or a simple clerical error right now.
- You do -not- need to argue about "storage fees" or "drop fees" while the driver is pressuring you.
- You do -not- need to prove you own the car or that your payments are up to date in the middle of a confrontation.
- You do -not- need to decide which body shop the car should go to until you have verified the operator's legitimacy.

## Important reassurance
Unsolicited towing attempts are a common high-pressure tactic designed to capitalize on confusion. Taking a moment to lock your doors and make a verification call is a standard safety procedure, not an overreaction. Legitimate operators will understand the need for verification and will wait for official confirmation.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and safety steps for an unrequested towing encounter. It does not cover long-term legal disputes regarding predatory towing fees or the recovery of a vehicle once it has reached an impound lot.

## Important note
This information is for safety and fraud prevention only and does not constitute legal advice. Towing laws vary significantly by state and municipality. If you believe a crime has been committed or you are in immediate physical danger, contact local law enforcement.
