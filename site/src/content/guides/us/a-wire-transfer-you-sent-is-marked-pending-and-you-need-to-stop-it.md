# What to do if… a wire transfer you sent is marked pending and you need to stop it (USA)

## Short answer
Call your bank immediately using a trusted number and ask for the wire team. Ask whether the wire is still waiting to be processed or has already been sent; if it is still waiting, ask the bank to cancel it now, and if it has been sent, ask for an urgent recall and an investigation.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send another wire to try to reverse the first one.
- Don’t pay a cancellation, release, or recovery fee to anyone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Don’t assume that a pending status means the wire can still be canceled.
- Don’t keep using the same email thread or phone number that supplied the wire instructions if fraud may be involved.
- Don’t click links in messages about the transfer. Use your bank’s app, website, debit-card number, or another trusted contact route.
- Don’t delete emails, invoices, messages, or payment records connected to the wire.
- Don’t wait for a perfect written account before contacting the bank or making a prompt report if fraud may be involved.

## What to do now
1. **Pause related payments.**  
   Do not send another payment to the same recipient, including a smaller test payment.

2. **Call your bank using a trusted number and ask for the wire team.**  
   Use the number on your debit card, your banking app, or your bank’s official website. Give the amount, date, recipient details, and any wire reference shown in your account. Ask: “Has this wire already been sent, or is it still waiting to be processed?”

3. **If the bank says the wire is still waiting to be processed, ask for immediate cancellation.**  
   Say: “Please cancel the wire now and tell me the current status.” Ask for confirmation through a secure message or another bank-approved channel.

4. **If the wire has already been sent, ask for an urgent recall and an investigation.**  
   Say: “Please request an urgent recall, start an investigation, and contact the receiving bank.” Ask for the bank’s case number or reference number for follow-up.

5. **If fraud may be involved, tell the bank clearly.**  
   Say: “I believe this may be wire fraud.” If email, an online account, or another internet-related route may be involved, file an IC3 complaint promptly and keep the complaint details. FinCEN says its Rapid Response Program has had greater success when fraudulently induced wires are reported to law enforcement within 72 hours of the transaction, but recovery is not guaranteed. Do not try to contact FinCEN directly.

6. **Contact the receiving bank through an official route if fraud may be involved.**  
   If the receiving bank is shown in your records, use that bank’s official fraud contact route. Explain that your bank is requesting a recall.

7. **If the recipient is legitimate, contact them through a verified route.**  
   Use a phone number you already trusted before this transfer. Ask them not to move the funds and to speak with their bank if the money arrives.

8. **Secure accounts if compromise is possible.**  
   Use official websites or apps to change passwords for any affected email or banking accounts, sign out of other sessions where available, and turn on multi-factor authentication.

9. **Save a short record.**  
   Screenshot the status and confirmation, save the wire instructions and related messages, and note who you spoke with, when you spoke, and what the bank said it would do.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether to hire an attorney or take civil action.
- You don’t need to write a detailed narrative before calling the bank or filing an IC3 complaint. Start with the key facts and add details later.
- An FTC report and any complaint escalation can follow after the urgent bank contact and, when relevant, an IC3 filing.
- You don’t need to decide today what the pending label meant. Focus on the bank’s current answer and the next available action.

## Important reassurance
A pending label does not tell you by itself whether the wire can still be canceled. Calling the bank promptly is still worthwhile: the bank can check the actual status and tell you whether cancellation, a recall, or an investigation is available.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your bank’s fraud team, law enforcement, or a qualified legal or financial professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Wire-transfer status labels and recovery options can vary by bank, transfer route, and the facts. Cancellation may become harder after a wire has been sent, so use official bank channels promptly.
