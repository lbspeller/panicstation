# What to do if… a friend seems suddenly disoriented or drugged at a social event and you fear they are unsafe (USA)

## Short answer
Stay beside your friend and move together to a well-lit, staffed area with trusted people nearby. Call 911 now if they are hard to wake, having trouble breathing, having a seizure, collapsing, or in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave them alone or let an unfamiliar or unwanted person lead them away.
- Don’t confront, follow, or physically challenge a suspected person yourself.
- Don’t give them more alcohol or force food or drink.
- Don’t pressure your friend to explain what happened, make a report, or decide what to do next.
- Don’t post accusations or broadcast details to the group while the situation is unclear.
- Don’t send them away alone in a rideshare or with someone you do not trust.

## What to do now
1. Stay beside your friend. Use a calm line such as: “I’m staying with you. Let’s move somewhere safer with staff nearby.” Ask before touching or moving them where possible.
2. Ask venue staff or security for help if that feels safe. Move together to a well-lit, staffed area with trusted people nearby. Ask staff to keep an unwanted person away and help arrange a safer exit if leaving is safer.
3. Call 911 if your friend is hard to wake, having trouble breathing, having a seizure, collapsing, or in immediate danger. If you are unsure whether it is an emergency, tell the dispatcher what you can see and follow their instructions.
4. Stay with them and watch their breathing and responsiveness while help is coming. If they become unresponsive but are breathing, place them on their side if you can do so safely and follow the dispatcher’s instructions. If a head, neck, or back injury may have happened, avoid moving them unless the dispatcher tells you to or movement is needed for immediate safety.
5. If you think they may have been drugged, call 911 or go to a hospital emergency room. Tell clinicians about the sudden symptoms and your concern about possible drugging and sexual harm.
6. Protect their choices where possible. If someone is pressuring them or trying to separate them from the group, stay together and ask safe staff or 911 for help rather than physically confronting anyone.
7. Offer confidential specialist support without pressure. If your friend thinks something sexual may have happened, or is unsure, you or your friend can contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline for support and referrals for local services.

## What can wait
- You do not need certainty tonight about what caused the symptoms or whether any substance was involved.
- You do not need to confront anyone, prove anything, or convince the group in the moment.
- You do not need to decide about reporting to police right now.
- If your friend may want options later, ask a clinician or sexual assault support service what to preserve; do not delay urgent care or do anything that distresses them.

## Important reassurance
Sudden confusion can have more than one cause. You do not need to work out the cause at the venue before taking practical safety steps. Staying beside your friend, asking safe staff for help, and getting emergency care when needed are reasonable first responses.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the earliest safe pause. Later decisions about medical care, specialist support, or reporting may need help from an appropriate professional or support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, investigative, or other professional advice. If someone is in immediate danger or severely unwell, call 911. Your friend’s consent and wellbeing come first where possible.
