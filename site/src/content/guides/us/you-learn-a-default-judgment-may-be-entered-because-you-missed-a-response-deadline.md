# What to do if… you learn a default judgment may be entered because you missed a response deadline (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the case status on the official court docket immediately; if an "Entry of Default" or "Default Judgment" is recorded, you must file a formal motion to "set aside" or "vacate" the order to regain the right to defend the case.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a hearing will be scheduled before a judgment is signed—defaults are often granted on paperwork alone.
- Do not attempt to resolve the issue solely by calling the opposing lawyer—only a court filing can stop or undo a default.
- Do not ignore the case if you believe you were never served—you must still file a motion to contest jurisdiction or the judgment remains enforceable.
- Do not wait weeks to act—per 2026 rulings, even "void" judgments must be challenged within a reasonable time of discovery.
- Do not send informal emails or letters to the judge's chambers—these are typically discarded and do not count as legal filings.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the specific court and case number on the summons or notice.** If you do not have the papers, search your name on the local County Clerk’s website (for state cases) or PACER.uscourts.gov (for federal cases).
2. **Identify the current status of the case on the docket.** Look specifically for:
   - "Entry of Default" (Step 1: The clerk notes you failed to respond).
   - "Default Judgment" (Step 2: A judge or clerk has issued a final order for money or relief).
3. **Contact the Clerk of Court to confirm the fastest method for a "pro se" (self-represented) filing.** Ask:
   - If an Entry of Default is present, can I file a "Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default"?
   - If a Default Judgment is present, what is the form for a "Motion to Vacate Judgment"?
   - Does the court have a "Stay of Execution" form to pause bank levies or wage garnishments?
4. **File a Motion to Set Aside or Vacate according to the court's local rules.**
   - State the reason for the delay, such as "excusable neglect" (e.g., illness, moving, or confusion) or "lack of notice" (if you were never served).
   - Include a "meritorious defense" (a brief statement explaining why you would win the case if allowed to fight it).
   - Attach your proposed "Answer" to the original lawsuit to show you are ready to proceed immediately.
5. **Request a "Stay of Enforcement" if the plaintiff is already attempting to collect money.** This is a separate request to the judge to stop the sheriff or banks from seizing assets while the court considers your motion to vacate.
6. **Gather evidence of improper service or external obstacles.**
   - Save dated mail, hospital records, or proof of residence at a different address if the papers were sent to the wrong place.
   - Keep a log of every contact you have with the court clerk and the date you first discovered the default.
7. **Access specialized self-help resources for your specific state.**
   - Use LawHelp.org or LSC.gov to find local legal aid offices that handle "consumer defense" or "housing" defaults.
   - Check the court's website for "Self-Help" or "Pro Se" templates specifically for vacating defaults.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your entire case or provide all evidence today; you only need to provide "good cause" for the delay.
- You do not need to negotiate a settlement with the plaintiff until you have stabilized your legal position in court.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator to prove service was wrong; start with your own records and the court's "Affidavit of Service."

## Important reassurance
Default judgments are common, and the legal system generally prefers cases to be decided on their merits rather than on technical deadlines. If you act quickly after learning of the default and follow the local filing procedures, many judges will grant a request to "set aside" the default and allow the case to proceed.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for civil lawsuits in US state and federal courts. It does not apply to criminal proceedings or specialized administrative hearings which may have different jurisdictional rules.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Court procedures and timelines vary significantly between states and between small claims, civil, and federal jurisdictions. Use official court self-help centers or consult a licensed attorney to ensure your filings meet local statutory requirements.
