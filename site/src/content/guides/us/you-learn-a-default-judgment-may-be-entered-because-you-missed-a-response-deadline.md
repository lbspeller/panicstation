# What to do if… you learn a default judgment may be entered because you missed a response deadline (USA)

## Short answer
Act today: check the court docket to see whether an **entry of default** or a **default judgment** is already on file, then file the fastest protective document the court will accept (often an answer and/or a motion to set aside/vacate).

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “nothing happens until a hearing” — defaults can be entered on paperwork.
- Do not talk only to the other side and stop there — you usually must **file** something with the court.
- Do not ignore papers because you think service was wrong — improper service is usually raised through a motion, not by doing nothing.
- Do not pay anyone who contacts you claiming they can “clear the judgment today” — verify through the court’s official docket and clerk’s office.
- Do not message the judge directly or send scattered emails; use the court’s filing process.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the exact court and case number.** Use the summons/complaint, any notice you received, or the court’s online case search. Write down: court name, case number, plaintiff, and your deadline (if you know it).
2. **Check the docket for two key entries:**
   - **Entry of default** (often clerk-entered).
   - **Default judgment** (often judge-entered; sometimes separate from the entry of default).
   If you can’t access the docket online, call the clerk and ask what has been entered and on what date.
3. **Ask the clerk what the court will accept as “filed today.”** Specifically ask:
   - Can I file a **late Answer** (and do I need permission/“leave of court”)?
   - If default/default judgment is entered, what is the process to file a **motion to set aside / motion to vacate**?
   - How do I file (e-file, in person, by mail), and what are the local format rules?
4. **If default is NOT entered yet: file the fastest protective response the court accepts.**
   - Often that’s an **Answer**. If you’re past the deadline, ask whether you should file the Answer with a short request/motion explaining the late filing under that court’s rules.
   - Keep proof of filing (stamped copy or electronic receipt).
5. **If default or default judgment IS entered: file a motion to set aside/vacate (and consider a request to pause enforcement).**
   - The motion name varies by court, but common wording is “set aside,” “vacate,” or “relief from judgment.”
   - If wage garnishment, bank levy, eviction enforcement, or other collection is about to start, ask what to file to **stay/suspend enforcement** while the judge decides your motion.
6. **Write a short, factual timeline (this is often the difference-maker).** Include:
   - why you missed the deadline (papers never received, address issue, illness, mistake, other “excusable neglect” type reason);
   - the date you first learned about the case/default;
   - what you did immediately after learning (calls, docket check, filing steps);
   - a brief outline of your defense (bullet points are fine).
7. **If you believe service was wrong, preserve proof without escalating.** Save envelopes, screenshots, address-change records, and anything showing you didn’t receive service as claimed. Keep it factual and organized.
8. **Get rapid local help if possible.** Use legal aid and court self-help resources for your state/county. If money is tight, prioritize legal aid over paid “debt settlement” pitches tied to the lawsuit.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle the whole case.
- You do not need a long narrative — you need a clear request the court recognizes (answer and/or motion to set aside/vacate).
- You do not need to fight about every dollar today; first stabilize the case posture and stop enforcement risk.

## Important reassurance
Missing a deadline happens for ordinary reasons (moving, illness, confusion about paperwork). Courts routinely see motions to set aside/vacate defaults. Acting quickly and filing the right request matters more than perfect wording on day one.

## Scope note
These are immediate steps to prevent a default judgment or try to undo one. The correct forms, deadlines, and standards vary by state and by court (small claims, state civil, federal), so your specific court’s rules and self-help resources control.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. If you’re unsure what to file, focus on confirming what is already entered (no default vs entry of default vs default judgment) and filing the fastest protective document your court will accept, then follow the court’s instructions for the next required motion or hearing.
