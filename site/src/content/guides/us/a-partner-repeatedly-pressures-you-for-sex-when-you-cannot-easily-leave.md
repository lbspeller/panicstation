# What to do if… a partner repeatedly pressures you for sex when you cannot easily leave (USA)

## Short answer
If you can do so safely, move toward a public or staffed place or contact someone you trust, then bring in specialist support. You do not owe sex to keep the peace, and pressure is not consent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move somewhere more private just to reduce their pressure.
- Do not confront them while you are isolated if that could increase danger.
- Do not make help-seeking visible if you think they may be watching your phone or internet use.
- Do not blame yourself if you froze, went quiet, or complied because you felt trapped.
- Do not force yourself to decide what label to use, whether to report, or whether to end the relationship during this first pause.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause if you can.** Move toward other people, staff, or a public place when it is safe to do so.
2. **Bring in another person quietly.** Contact someone you trust or ask a staff member for help leaving or contacting support. Use another device if you think your phone may be monitored.
3. **Use emergency help when needed.** If you are in immediate danger or need urgent police, fire, or ambulance help, dial **911**.
4. **Contact specialist sexual-assault support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available by phone at **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, by text by sending **HOPE** to **64673**, and by online chat through RAINN.
5. **Contact partner-abuse support if that fits your situation.** The National Domestic Violence Hotline is available by phone at **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)**, by text by sending **START** to **88788**, and by online chat through TheHotline.org.
6. **Consider medical care if sexual contact happened that you did not freely choose.** A medical provider can discuss care, and RAINN can help you ask about local services and whether a sexual assault forensic exam is available.
7. **Only if it feels useful and safe, save a brief private note later for your own memory and choices.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now what label to use for what happened.
- You do not need to make a reporting decision during this first pause.
- You do not need to confront your partner or decide the future of the relationship right now.
- You do not need to gather proof now.
- You do not need to make a long-term plan before asking for support.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, going quiet, or complying to reduce danger can happen when someone feels trapped or pressured. None of those responses mean that you freely chose what happened or that it was your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist advocacy and, if you choose, advice from an appropriate medical or legal professional.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm reduction and support. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think your phone or internet use may be monitored, consider using another device or asking a trusted person or staff member to contact help for you.
