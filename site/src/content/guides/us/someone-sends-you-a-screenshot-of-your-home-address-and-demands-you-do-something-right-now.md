# What to do if… someone sends you a screenshot of your home address and demands you do something right now (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer pause and do not rush to comply or reply. If you think someone may be nearby, on the way, following you, or likely to harm you, call 911 now; otherwise save the message and use official reporting routes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, verification codes, photos, or documents while you are under pressure.
- Do not click links, open attachments, install apps, or call a number the sender gives you.
- Do not share more information about your home, household, workplace, location, or plans.
- Do not go outside alone to check for someone if you feel unsafe.
- Do not delete the message before saving a copy.
- Do not make a public post that could reveal more personal information.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   If you are at home, lock doors if you can do so safely and stay inside if that feels safer. If you are away from home, move to a populated, staffed place. Keep your phone with you.

2. **Use 911 for immediate danger.**  
   Call 911 if someone is outside, trying to enter, following you, threatening violence, or appears to be on the way to harm you. If there is no immediate danger, find the official contact details for the local law enforcement agency where the threat is happening and ask how to make a non-emergency report.

3. **Save the message before blocking or muting the sender.**  
   Save screenshots of the full conversation, the sender’s profile or contact details, timestamps, the address screenshot, the demand, any deadline, and any payment instructions. Keep the original message if possible.

4. **Reduce the live pressure.**  
   After saving the message, use the platform’s report function if available. Mute or block the sender if that feels safer. If new accounts contact you, save the new messages and avoid replying.

5. **Tell anyone who is at home with you.**  
   Ask them not to open the door to an unexpected visitor. If someone appears, tries to enter, or makes you feel in immediate danger, call 911 rather than confronting them.

6. **Contact a payment provider promptly if money was sent.**  
   Use the official app, website, or phone number for your bank, card issuer, payment app, gift card issuer, wire-transfer service, or cryptocurrency service. Tell them the payment was connected to a threat or scam and ask whether a stop or reversal is possible.

7. **Take only the account-safety steps that apply.**  
   If you shared a password, change it using the official site or app and change it anywhere else you reused it. If you installed software or gave someone remote access to a device, update your security software and run a scan.

8. **Use the appropriate official reporting route.**  
   IC3 is not a substitute for urgent local help. Call 911 for immediate danger. Contact local law enforcement directly if the matter is time-sensitive. For a cyber-enabled threat or scam, file an IC3 report. For a scam or fraud report, you can also use the FTC reporting service. The FBI cyber page lists additional routes for an ongoing crime or threat to life.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out how the sender found your address right now.
- You do not need to do a full privacy clean-up tonight.
- You do not need to contact everyone you know or post publicly.
- You do not need to decide on longer-term legal, workplace, school, or housing steps during the urgent moment.

## Important reassurance
A screenshot of your address is frightening, but the screenshot alone does not prove that the sender is nearby or able to carry out the threat. Treat the message seriously, slow the situation down, save the evidence, and use official support routes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from law enforcement, a lawyer, a financial provider, a cybersecurity professional, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. It does not replace emergency services. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
