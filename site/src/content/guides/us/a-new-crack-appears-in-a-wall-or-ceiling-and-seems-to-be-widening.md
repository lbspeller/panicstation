# What to do if… a new crack appears in a wall or ceiling and seems to be widening (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the area immediately and keep all occupants away from the affected wall or ceiling. Call 911 if you hear grinding or popping sounds, or if the structure is visibly shifting or sagging.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand, sit, or sleep directly under a widening ceiling crack.
- Do not attempt to "test" the crack by pushing on the wall or poking the ceiling.
- Do not use tools to remove drywall or plaster to "see what is behind it."
- Do not try to shore up, jack, or brace a structural member yourself.
- Do not ignore the crack if it is wider than 1/4 inch or follows a "stair-step" pattern in masonry.
- Do not assume it is "normal settling" if the crack has appeared suddenly or is lengthening visibly.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the immediate area and call 911 if collapse appears imminent.**
   - Listen for **loud popping, grinding, or groaning sounds** coming from the framing.
   - Look for a **visible sag or bulge** in the ceiling or a wall that is noticeably **bowing inward or outward**.
   - Check if a **primary support beam or post** has shifted or if debris is actively falling from the crack.
2. **Clear the "fall zone" around the crack.**
   - Keep pets and children out of the room entirely.
   - If the crack is in a multi-story building, avoid the floor directly above the affected area as well.
3. **Check for secondary utility hazards from a distance.**
   - **If you smell gas (rotten eggs) or hear hissing:** Evacuate the entire building immediately and call 911 from outside. Do not touch light switches or electronics.
   - **If the crack is near electrical outlets or smells like burning plastic:** Turn off the power at the main breaker only if you can do so without entering the "fall zone."
   - **If water is spraying or pooling near the crack:** Shut off the main water valve if it is located in a safe, unaffected part of the building.
4. **Notify the appropriate authority for an urgent safety assessment.**
   - **If you rent:** Contact the landlord or property manager via phone and follow up immediately in writing. State that you suspect a "structural emergency." 
   - **If you own:** Contact a licensed structural engineer or a foundation specialist for an emergency inspection. 
   - **If the building is a workplace or public space:** Notify the building's facilities manager or Fire Marshal.
5. **Document the damage from a safe distance.**
   - Take photos or video showing the crack's location relative to doors, windows, and the floor.
   - Note if doors or windows in that specific area have suddenly become difficult to open or close (sticking).
6. **Report the condition to local code enforcement if response is delayed.**
   - Call **311** or your local Building Department if you believe the building is unsafe and the owner is not responding.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the cause is soil subsidence, water damage, or seismic activity right now.
- You do not need to clean up fallen plaster or dust until the structure is cleared as safe.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent repair method or hire a contractor today.

## Important reassurance
Sudden cracks can be startling, but taking the immediate step of clearing the area and calling for a professional assessment is the correct and safest response. Most structural issues can be stabilized once identified by an expert, and your primary goal is simply to prevent injury while that assessment happens.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. A widening crack requires an on-site physical inspection by a qualified professional to determine the underlying cause and the required remediation.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for a professional structural engineering assessment. If you have any doubt about the stability of the building, evacuate immediately and contact emergency services.
