# What to do if… you witness unsafe practice at work and are told not to report it (USA)

## Short answer
If someone could be hurt, move to a safer pause, warn people nearby if you can, and make a clear factual record. Report the hazard through a documented safety channel; if reporting is blocked or you are threatened or punished, OSHA or an OSHA-approved State Plan may be a route for a safety complaint and a whistleblower complaint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a serious hazard because someone told you to keep it quiet or not write it down.
- Do not argue about motives, blame, or workplace politics; focus on what happened, where it happened, and the risk.
- Do not post about it on social media or in group chats.
- Do not delete messages, notes, photos, reports, or instructions you already have.
- Do not sign a statement you know is misleading or that says the issue is fixed when you do not know that.
- Do not put yourself in danger to get proof.

## What to do now
1. **Make the moment safer first.**  
   If there is immediate danger, step back, pause your own task if you can do so safely, and warn people nearby in plain words. If someone is seriously injured, there is fire, violence, chemical release, collapse risk, or another emergency, call **911**.

2. **Write a short factual record while it is fresh.**  
   Record the date, time, exact location, equipment or substance involved, what you saw, who was present, what could happen if it continues, and the words used when you were told not to report it. Keep it factual and avoid guesses about intent.

3. **Save what you already have safe access to.**  
   Keep relevant texts, emails, work orders, safety forms, photos you already lawfully took, and your own notes. Do not take confidential, medical, trade-secret, personal, or protected information that you do not need for the safety concern.

4. **Report internally in a documented way if that is safe enough to do.**  
   Use the employer’s safety reporting system, incident form, hotline, safety officer, HR, or union safety representative if you have one. A short message can be: “I am reporting a safety concern: [facts]. I am concerned about risk of injury. Who is addressing it, and what controls are in place?” Keep a note of what you sent, to whom, and when.

5. **Escalate away from the person blocking the report.**  
   If your direct supervisor told you not to report it, go to the next appropriate route, such as a higher manager, site safety lead, compliance hotline, HR contact, or union safety representative. You can ask for confidentiality, while understanding it may not always be possible during an investigation.

6. **Use OSHA or the State Plan route if the hazard is serious, ongoing, not being addressed, or reporting is blocked.**  
   Workers can generally report unsafe or unhealthful working conditions to OSHA and ask for an inspection. OSHA complaints can usually be made online, by phone, by mail, or through a local OSHA office; a signed complaint is more likely to result in an onsite inspection.

7. **Act quickly if you are threatened or punished for raising the safety concern.**  
   Retaliation can include firing, demotion, discipline, reduced hours, threats, intimidation, or other negative action. For retaliation under the OSH Act Section 11(c), a complaint generally must be filed within **30 days** of the retaliatory action or when you were notified of it. OSHA enforces other whistleblower laws with different deadlines, so file as soon as possible if anything negative happens.

8. **Check coverage without getting stuck.**  
   Federal OSHA covers many private-sector workers, but coverage can vary by state, industry, and public-sector status. OSHA-approved State Plans cover safety and health in some states and territories; some cover both private and public workers, and some cover state and local government workers only. If you are unsure, contact OSHA and ask which office or program covers your workplace.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to quit, sue, contact the media, or make a long-term career decision.
- You do not need perfect evidence before reporting a serious hazard.
- You do not need to win an argument with the person who told you not to report it.
- You do not need to describe motives; a clear timeline and concrete safety facts are enough for first steps.

## Important reassurance
Being told to stay quiet can make you doubt yourself. A calm record, a documented report, and a safer reporting route are reasonable first steps when the issue could hurt someone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce harm, preserve options, and prevent rushed mistakes. Later decisions about formal complaints, legal strategy, workplace discipline, union processes, or job changes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes and coverage can vary by state, industry, employer type, and employment status.
