# What to do if… someone is trying to follow you or others into your secure building (USA)

## Short answer
Do not grant access for them. From a safe distance, let the door close and lock, move to a safer place, and call 911 if you feel threatened or a crime appears to be happening now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hold the door open or use your key, fob, code, or app to let them in.
- Do not step into an elevator, stairwell, mailroom, parking garage, or enclosed corridor with them.
- Do not physically block, grab, shove, or try to detain them.
- Do not argue at close range or keep repeating yourself if they push back.
- Do not follow them through the building if they get in.
- Do not assume you must decide whether they are a resident, guest, delivery worker, or trespasser.

## What to do now
1. **Create space first.** Step aside, keep the door or another barrier between you where possible, and let the entrance door close and latch.
2. **Move to a safer pause.** Go toward a staffed desk, a public area, a better-lit area, outside with other people, behind a locked door, or into a nearby business.
3. **Give one neutral boundary only if it feels safe.** Say, “I can’t let anyone in. Please use your own access, the call box, or contact the person you are visiting.” Then stop engaging.
4. **If another resident is about to hold the door, warn them briefly from a safe distance.** Say, “Please let the door close.” Do not argue if they ignore you.
5. **Use the proper access route instead of becoming the gatekeeper.** Point them to the intercom, call box, front desk, security desk, concierge, leasing office, property manager, or the resident they are visiting.
6. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Call if they threaten anyone, try to force the door, damage the lock, follow someone aggressively, enter a private area, or you believe a crime is happening now. Give the dispatcher the address, entrance or floor, what they are doing, direction of travel, and a brief description.
7. **Use local non-emergency reporting if it is suspicious but not urgent.** Contact your local police or sheriff’s department through its official website or listed non-emergency number. If you cannot find the correct route quickly and you feel at risk, call 911 and explain what is happening.
8. **Capture details only if it does not escalate the situation.** Note the time, entrance, clothing, direction of travel, vehicle details, and whether the door failed to latch. Do not move closer to record video.
9. **Notify building staff or management.** Report the incident and any broken latch, propped door, damaged lock, failed fob reader, or camera issue as urgent access-control information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the person had no permission to enter.
- You do not need to investigate where they went.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether the building needs cameras, new locks, guards, or policy changes.
- You do not need to write a perfect report before getting to safety.

## Important reassurance
It can feel rude to let a door close on someone, especially in a shared building. A controlled-access entrance only works if residents do not grant access to unknown people, and it is reasonable to use the official access process instead.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you notice a possible unauthorized entry into a shared building. Later decisions about repeated incidents, lease duties, building security, police reports, or repairs may need help from building management, local law enforcement, a tenants’ organization, or a qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, or professional advice. If you believe there is immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 911.
