# What to do if… you hear someone trying keys or a keycard on your door while you are inside (USA)

## Short answer
Do not open the door. Lock it, move away from the doorway, and call 911 if you think someone is trying to get in or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to see what is going on, even if the person says it is a mistake.
- Do not step into the hallway to confront them.
- Do not shout personal details, such as that you are alone.
- Do not stand where you are visible through door glass, a window, or a lit entryway.
- Do not focus on recording video instead of calling for help.
- Do not assume it is harmless if the person keeps trying the lock, keycard reader, handle, or another entry point.

## What to do now
1. **Lock and reinforce the door immediately.** Use any deadbolt, latch, chain, or security bar that is already installed. If you have a building buzzer or intercom, do not buzz anyone in.
2. **Move away from the entry door.** Go to a room or position farther from the door, out of sight from door glass or nearby windows. Keep your phone with you.
3. **Bring essentials with you.** Take your phone, keys, and shoes. If other people are with you, bring them into the same area and keep voices low.
4. **Call 911 if you believe a crime may be happening or you feel in danger.** Say your exact address and unit number, then say: “Someone is trying keys or a keycard on my door and I am inside.” Give any details you can, such as handle rattling, key scraping, card beeps, voices, or whether it sounds like more than one person.
5. **If you cannot safely speak, try to contact 911 another way.** A voice call is usually best if you can make one safely. If you cannot safely call and your area supports Text-to-911, text 911 with your exact location and a short message, such as: “Someone is trying keys on my door. I am inside.” If Text-to-911 is not available where you are, you may receive a bounce-back message.
6. **Use only safe ways to observe.** If you can check without exposing yourself, use a peephole, door camera, or camera feed. Note clothing, direction of travel, and any vehicle details if the person leaves, but do not stand directly in front of the door.
7. **If you are in an apartment, dorm, hotel, or staffed building, contact staff or security after 911 if it is urgent.** Ask them to follow their emergency procedure and check remotely, review entry logs, or check the hallway only when it is safe for them to do so.
8. **Stay behind the locked barrier.** If the sounds stop, do not rush to open the door. Wait for instructions or confirmation from police, building staff, or someone you trust before opening.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether it was the wrong unit, a former tenant, maintenance, or something else.
- Reviewing footage carefully or saving clips.
- Calling property management about rekeying, locks, or keycards.
- Posting in a neighbor or building chat.
- Writing a detailed account.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to prove what is happening before you protect yourself. Staying behind a locked barrier, creating distance, and getting help is a reasonable response when someone is trying keys or a keycard on your door.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. Later decisions about police reports, lock changes, building access, hotel security, or lease issues may need help from local authorities, property staff, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate first steps, not legal, security, or professional advice. If you believe someone is attempting to enter your home or room, or you feel unsafe, treat it as an emergency and contact local authorities.
