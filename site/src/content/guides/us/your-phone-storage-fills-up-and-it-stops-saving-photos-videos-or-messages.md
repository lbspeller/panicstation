# What to do if… your phone storage fills up and it stops saving photos, videos, or messages (USA)

## Short answer
Do not delete any files manually—create a standalone backup of the entire library first, then use the platform’s native "Merge" tool to consolidate copies without losing metadata or quality.

## Do not do these things
- Do not perform mass deletions of "identical" files until a secondary offline backup is verified.
- Do not empty the "Recently Deleted" or "Trash" folders for at least 30 days.
- Do not install third-party "cleaner" apps from the App Store or Play Store as your first response, as these may strip metadata or select the lower-quality copy.
- Do not toggle cloud synchronization settings on and off repeatedly, as this can trigger recursive upload loops.
- Do not move or rename system-managed photo folders (like the .photoslibrary bundle on Mac) while a sync is in progress.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate one primary device to perform the cleanup.** **Avoid opening the Photos app on multiple devices simultaneously, which can cause sync conflicts while you are trying to stabilize the library.**
   - Choose a computer over a mobile device if possible for better visibility of file details.
   - Ensure the device has a stable Wi-Fi connection and is connected to power.

2. **Generate a "Safety Export" that exists outside of your live cloud library.** **You must have a copy of your data that will not be affected if a "Merge" or "Delete" command syncs across your account.**
   - **iCloud / Apple Photos:** Log into iCloud.com on a computer, select all photos, and choose the "Download" option (select "Unmodified Originals").
   - **Google Photos:** Go to Google Takeout, deselect all services except Google Photos, and request a .zip archive of your entire library.
   - **Local Storage:** Copy the entire folder or library file to a physical external hard drive and disconnect it once the copy is complete.

3. **Verify the nature of the duplicates by inspecting file metadata.** **Open three to five pairs of duplicates to confirm they are identical in file size, resolution, and "Date Taken" before assuming a bulk tool is safe.**
   - Check if one copy has a "(1)" in the filename, which often indicates a conflict during a manual import.
   - Note if one copy is significantly smaller (e.g., a thumbnail or "Optimized" version) versus the full-resolution original.

4. **Trigger the native Apple "Merge" tool if using iPhone, iPad, or Mac.** **Apple’s built-in tool is designed to keep the highest quality version and combine metadata like captions, keywords, and "Favorite" status into a single file.**
   - **On iPhone/iPad:** Open Photos → Collections (tab) → Utilities (section) → Duplicates.
   - **On Mac:** Open Photos → Duplicates (sidebar).
   - If the "Duplicates" album is missing, plug the phone into power and lock it overnight; the system only indexes for duplicates while the device is idle and charging.

5. **Utilize "Photo Stacks" for Google Photos on mobile devices.** **Google Photos uses AI to stack similar images, allowing you to select a "Top Pick" and remove the redundant frames.**
   - Open Google Photos → Tap the three-dot menu (top right) → Turn on "Stack similar photos."
   - Open a stack, select the images you wish to remove, and tap the Trash icon.

6. **Secure your account if the duplication appeared without a device change or migration.** **Sudden, unexplained changes to library structure can occasionally indicate unauthorized account access.**
   - Update your Apple Account or Google Account password.
   - Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) if it is not already active.
   - Review the list of "Trusted Devices" and sign out of any hardware you do not recognize.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the "perfect" copy of every photo today.
- You do not need to clear storage space immediately; most cloud providers offer a grace period before blocking services.
- You do not need to organize albums or fix incorrect dates until the library count is stabilized.
- You do not need to investigate the technical root cause (e.g., database corruption) until your "Safety Export" is safely stored on an external drive.

## Important reassurance
Digital photo duplication is a common side effect of cloud syncing, device migrations, and software updates. It rarely signifies that your original high-quality files have been replaced or lost. By creating a physical backup before using automated tools, you eliminate the risk of permanent data loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to prevent accidental deletion of original media. It does not cover long-term library management or the repair of corrupted database files.
