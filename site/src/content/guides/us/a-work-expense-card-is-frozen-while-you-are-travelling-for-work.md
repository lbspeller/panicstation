# What to do if… a work expense card is frozen while you are travelling for work (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign any document with an inaccurate date or falsify record history. Document the request in writing, maintain a record of the original timeline, and escalate the matter through your organization's formal Compliance, Legal, or Internal Audit channels.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign, initial, or approve any record using a date other than the current actual date.
- Do not "recreate" approvals or memos by choosing a past date to make it appear the action happened earlier.
- Do not alter electronic metadata, version histories, or system logs to match a requested timeline.
- Do not delete communications, including "off the record" chats or texts, related to the request.
- Do not move confidential company files to personal email or cloud storage to "protect" yourself.
- Do not confront the requester with legal accusations; keep the interaction focused on procedural accuracy.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify the request in an observable format.** **Ask the requester to specify exactly which document is involved, the specific date they want applied, and the audit gap they are attempting to close.**
2. **Create a contemporaneous written record of the instruction.** **If the request was verbal, send a follow-up email stating: "To ensure I follow the correct procedure for the audit, I am confirming your request for me to sign [Document Name] and date it as [Past Date]. I am not comfortable using an inaccurate date; please advise on the approved correction memo process."**
3. **Offer an accurate correction path instead of backdating.** **Propose signing the document with today's date and attaching a "Note to File" or memo that truthfully explains when the original event occurred and why the documentation is being completed now.**
4. **Identify the correct internal reporting channel for audit integrity.** **Route the concern to Internal Audit, Compliance, or the Legal department, as these functions are typically responsible for maintaining the integrity of audit evidence under Sarbanes-Oxley or industry-specific regulations.**
5. **Preserve evidence within approved company systems.** **Keep a folder of relevant emails and drafts in your work account, and if you fear deletion, request a formal "litigation hold" or preservation instruction from the Legal department.**
6. **Note the presence of specific high-risk triggers.** **If the documents involve federal grants, Medicare/Medicaid billing, or Small Business Administration (SBA) programs, be aware that the 2026 Administrative False Claims Act (AFCA) thresholds and 10-year statute of limitations may apply.**
7. **Observe the strict 30-day filing window for general whistleblower protection.** **If you experience immediate retaliation such as a demotion or firing, be aware that OSH Act Section 11(c) complaints must typically be filed within 30 days of the retaliatory act.**
8. **Set a neutral boundary regarding your signature.** **Use a standard response: "I am unable to sign a document that contains an inaccurate date. I am happy to sign with today's date and a clarifying attachment once Compliance has reviewed the approach."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you are quitting your job immediately.
- You do not need to prove "fraudulent intent" on behalf of your manager; your only task is ensuring your own actions remain accurate.
- You do not need to report to external federal agencies (like the SEC or DOJ) in the first hour unless you have already consulted with counsel.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure when a supervisor frames backdating as a "minor cleanup" for an audit. Hesitation in this moment is a standard response to an ethical conflict. Taking a pause to verify the correct procedure is a professional requirement, not a failure of loyalty.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling a request to falsify dates. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, specific industry licensing requirements (e.g., FINRA/SEC), or individual employment litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for recordkeeping and whistleblower protections vary significantly by industry (e.g., healthcare, defense, finance) and by state law. If you believe you are being asked to participate in illegal activity or fear imminent retaliation, consult with a qualified employment attorney or a specialized whistleblower advocate.
