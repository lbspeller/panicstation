# What to do if… your vehicle is blocked in and you cannot drive out (USA)

## Short answer
Do not touch, damage, or try to move the other vehicle. First try to locate the driver through the business, building, security desk, or property manager; if that does not work, use your local police non-emergency number, parking enforcement, or 311 where your city uses it. Call 911 for immediate danger, a crime in progress, a fire, or a medical emergency; if your local official page or dispatcher specifically tells you to use 911 for a blocked-driveway report, follow that local instruction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not push, tow, roll, jack, unlock, enter, or tamper with the other vehicle.
- Do not threaten the driver, block them in for revenge, or wait in a way that could become a confrontation.
- Do not call a random tow company before confirming who is allowed to request a tow where you are parked.
- Do not damage the vehicle, even if you urgently need to leave.
- Do not assume the process is the same on a public street, in a private lot, at an apartment building, or in your own driveway.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether this is an emergency.**
   - Call 911 if someone is in immediate danger, you are being threatened, there is a fire, there is a medical emergency, or a crime is happening now.
   - Give the exact location, what is blocked, and whether anyone is in danger.
   - If an official local page, parking office, police non-emergency line, or dispatcher says blocked-driveway reports in your city use 911, follow that local instruction.

2. **Record the facts before anything changes.**
   - Take photos or video showing your vehicle, the blocking vehicle, the license plate, the position of both vehicles, nearby signs, and the surrounding area.
   - Write down the time, address, lot name, nearest entrance, vehicle make, vehicle color, and anything posted about parking or towing.

3. **Try to locate the driver through the place you are parked.**
   - In a store, venue, school, hospital, or office lot, ask customer service, reception, or security to page the driver using the make, color, and plate.
   - At an apartment, condo, HOA, hotel, or workplace, contact the front desk, property manager, concierge, or on-site security.
   - If there is a posted towing sign, photograph it and ask the property manager or security who is authorized to request removal.

4. **Use the local official route for parking enforcement.**
   - Call your local police non-emergency number, parking enforcement office, or 311 if your city uses 311 for non-emergency city services.
   - Say: “My vehicle is blocked in and I cannot drive out.”
   - Give the address, plate, make, color, and whether this is a public street, private lot, apartment lot, workplace lot, or your own driveway.
   - Ask: “Can this be ticketed or towed here, and who is allowed to request the tow?”

5. **For private property, involve the property manager or authorized agent.**
   - In many places, a property owner, manager, security officer, or authorized agent may need to approve towing from a private lot.
   - Ask them to contact the approved tow company or enforcement channel, rather than arranging an unauthorized tow yourself.
   - If the vehicle is blocking your own driveway, ask local police non-emergency, parking enforcement, or your city service line what your city allows for that exact location.

6. **Keep the situation calm if the driver appears.**
   - Stand back and keep your tone neutral.
   - Say only what is needed: “Your vehicle is blocking mine and I need to leave.”
   - Do not argue about fault; focus on getting the vehicle moved.

7. **Make a safe backup plan while you wait.**
   - If you must leave before the vehicle is moved, arrange a ride, taxi, rideshare, transit, or help from someone nearby.
   - Keep receipts if the blockage creates costs.
   - Send a simple update to anyone waiting: “My vehicle is blocked in; I have reported it and I am arranging another way there.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to seek reimbursement, file a complaint, or take legal action.
- You do not need to prove intent or argue with the other driver.
- You do not need to contact your insurer unless there is damage.
- You do not need to solve a recurring parking problem while you are still trying to leave.

## Important reassurance
Being blocked in can feel trapping and urgent. A calm sequence usually works best: document it, try the building or business, then use the local non-emergency, parking enforcement, 311, or locally instructed official route.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation. Later decisions, such as complaints, reimbursement requests, recurring enforcement, or legal options, may need help from the property manager, local parking authority, insurer, or a qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Parking enforcement and towing rules vary by state, city, street type, and property type, so follow instructions from local official channels for your exact location.
