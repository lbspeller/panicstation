# What to do if… a private folder was accidentally shared publicly and you need to stop access immediately (USA)
## Short answer
Open the folder’s sharing settings now. Change access to the closest private setting, such as **Restricted** or **Specific people**, and remove, disable, or restrict any public or broad shared links you are allowed to change. If you cannot change the settings, contact the folder owner or administrator immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rely on deleting the message that contained the link. The link may already have been opened or forwarded.
- Don’t delete the folder as your first containment step. Use the sharing controls first.
- Don’t treat a change to view-only access as full containment. Remove, disable, or restrict any broad link as well.
- Don’t assume view-only access prevents copying or capture.
- Don’t send broad public or internal messages before notifying the appropriate incident contact if this involves work, school, or organization data.
- Don’t delete logs, messages, or records that may help confirm what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Restrict access in the folder’s sharing settings.**
   - Change general access to the closest private option, such as **Restricted**, **Specific people**, or **Only people you add**.
   - Turn off any public option or **Anyone with the link** setting.
   - If you cannot change the settings, send the folder link to the owner or administrator and ask them to restrict access immediately.

2. **Review every link and access route shown for the folder.**
   - Check for view links, edit links, direct access, external guests, and groups.
   - Remove, disable, or restrict any broad link you are allowed to change.
   - Remove people, guests, or groups that should not have access.

3. **Test the old link while signed out.**
   - Open the old link in a private browser window where you are not signed in to the relevant service.
   - If the folder still opens, check the folder settings again and review any parent folder, shared drive, site, workspace, team, or group that may still grant access.

4. **Report it promptly if this involves work, school, or organization data.**
   - Contact the required IT, security, privacy, or incident-response route.
   - Share the folder name, when you noticed the issue, the type of information involved, the access setting you found, and the changes you made.
   - Ask the incident contact to preserve relevant logs and help check whether anyone accessed the folder.

5. **Revoke exposed working credentials.**
   - If the folder contained passwords, API keys, private certificates, backup codes, or other working credentials, contact the relevant owner or administrator and revoke or rotate the affected credentials now.

6. **Record the containment steps.**
   - Note the time you found the issue, the access settings you saw, the links or users you removed, and the time you tested the old link.
   - Keep a screenshot of the final access view if your organization permits it.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide who was at fault right now.
- You don’t need to write a full incident report or postmortem before access is contained.
- You don’t need to notify every possible recipient yourself before the appropriate incident contact has assessed the exposure.
- You don’t need to reorganize the whole folder structure today.

## Important reassurance
Public access can often be restricted quickly from the folder’s sharing controls. Focus on closing the access route, testing the old link, and escalating promptly where needed.

## Scope note
These are first-step containment actions only. Later decisions, including any notifications, may depend on the information involved, the account type, your organization’s process, and applicable requirements. Specialist help may be needed.
