# What to do if… you are asked to arrange a funeral but you cannot locate a will or instructions (USA)

## Short answer
Identify the legal "right of disposition" holder in your state and conduct a time-limited search for a will, while requesting an itemized General Price List (GPL) from providers to avoid unnecessary costs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not authorize irreversible actions like cremation or body donation until the legal decision-maker is identified and agrees.
- Do not sign a funeral home contract that lacks an itemized statement of goods and services.
- Do not pay for a "package" without first reviewing individual prices; you have a legal right to buy only what you want.
- Do not remove any items other than a will or burial deed from a safe deposit box before probate, as this can lead to criminal or civil liability.
- Do not assume that "Power of Attorney" is still valid; legal authority under a POA typically expires immediately at the moment of death.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the person with the legal "Right of Disposition" for the state where the death occurred.** 
   - Most states follow a hierarchy: 1) a designated agent named in a formal document; 2) a surviving spouse; 3) a majority of adult children; 4) parents; 5) siblings.
   - If the deceased was active military, check for a DD Form 93 (Record of Emergency Data), which supersedes state law.
   - If multiple people have equal priority (e.g., three adult children), ensure all agree in writing before authorizing services to prevent legal disputes.
2. **Conduct a focused 90-minute search for end-of-life documents.**
   - Search the home for labeled folders, safe boxes, or "In Case of Emergency" (ICE) files.
   - Look through recent emails for keywords like "funeral," "pre-paid," "cremation," "cemetery," or "will."
   - Check the refrigerator or back of the front door for a "Vial of Life" or "File of Life" which may contain instructions.
3. **Contact the bank regarding safe deposit box access.**
   - Request a "Will Search" or "Limited Access" to the box. Many states (including CA, NY, and TX) have laws requiring banks to allow next-of-kin to open a box in the presence of a bank officer to retrieve a will or burial plot deed.
   - You will typically need a certified death certificate and government-issued photo ID.
4. **Exercise your rights under the FTC Funeral Rule.**
   - **Ask any funeral home for their General Price List (GPL).** They must give this to you to keep if you visit in person.
   - **Request price information over the phone.** Funeral providers are legally required to give you price information over the telephone; you do not have to provide your name or address first.
   - **Identify the "Basic Services Fee."** This is the only non-declinable fee; all other items (like embalming, viewing, or limousines) are optional.
5. **Report the death to the Social Security Administration (SSA).**
   - Call 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) between 8:00 AM and 7:00 PM local time.
   - Inform the bank if the deceased received direct deposits; payments received for the month of death and any subsequent months must be returned to the SSA.
6. **Inquire about "Indigent Burial" or "Public Disposition" if funds are unavailable.**
   - Contact the county Coroner, Medical Examiner, or Social Services office.
   - Ask for the "Undertaker of Last Resort" or "County Burial" program. Eligibility is usually based on the deceased's assets, not the family's income.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a casket, urn, or floral arrangements in the first few hours.
- You do not need to write or publish an obituary immediately.
- You do not need to begin the probate process or move assets today.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family disagreements about the deceased's legacy.

## Important reassurance
It is common for families to feel an urgent pressure to "finalize everything" immediately. However, most facilities (hospitals, morgues) can hold remains for several days while you locate the correct documents. Taking the time to confirm legal authority protects you from future litigation and ensures you are not pressured into high-cost contracts during a period of shock.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying legal authority and locating burial instructions in the USA. It does not cover the long-term probate process, complex estate litigation, or international repatriation of remains.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding the right of disposition and safe deposit box access vary significantly by state. If you are facing a family dispute or the bank refuses access despite state statutes, consult a local probate attorney.
