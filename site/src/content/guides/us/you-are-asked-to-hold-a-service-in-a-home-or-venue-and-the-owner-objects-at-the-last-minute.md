# What to do if… you are asked to hold a service in a home or venue and the owner objects at the last minute (USA)

## Short answer
Cease all service activities immediately and confirm if the person withdrawing permission has the legal authority to control the property. If permission is revoked, you must move all attendees off the private property to avoid criminal trespass charges.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue any part of the service on the property once an authorized owner or manager has directed you to stop.
- Do not engage in verbal arguments or physical confrontations with staff or owners in front of mourners.
- Do not block driveways, entrances, or public rights-of-way while organizing the group to leave.
- Do not attempt to "force" the service to happen based on a contract; property rights generally override contract disputes in the immediate moment.
- Do not use 911 for a contract dispute unless there is an immediate threat of physical violence or a medical emergency.

## What to do now
1. **Call a formal pause to the gathering and assign a coordinator.**  
   **State clearly to the group: "We are pausing for a moment to finalize logistical arrangements."**
   - Appoint one person to keep the attendees together in a safe area away from the property owner.
   - Ensure the group remains quiet to avoid further noise-related escalations.

2. **Verify the identity and authority of the person objecting.**  
   **Ask for the person's name and their specific role or relationship to the property.**
   - If in a private home, confirm they are the homeowner or a legal tenant.
   - If in a commercial venue, confirm they are the manager on duty or the property owner.

3. **Identify the specific boundaries of the refusal.**  
   **Ask the owner to clarify if they are banning the entire gathering or specific activities.**
   - Determine if the objection is limited to specific items (e.g., incense, open flames, amplified music).
   - Ask if a specific time window is available for a quiet, orderly exit.

4. **Document the revocation of permission immediately via digital means.**  
   **Request that the owner send a brief text or email confirming that permission for the service has been withdrawn.**
   - If they refuse to put it in writing, make a brief note of the time, the person's name, and their exact words.
   - Do not record video of the person without checking state-specific "two-party consent" laws.

5. **Execute a pre-planned "Alternative Service Plan" in a neutral location.**  
   **Move the group to the nearest public space where gatherings are permitted (such as a public park or sidewalk) to hold a brief, respectful moment.**
   - Limit this to a single reading or a moment of silence.
   - Use this moment to announce the confirmed location for the full service.

6. **Coordinate with a funeral director or alternate venue provider.**  
   **Contact a local funeral home, community center, or place of worship to request an emergency space.**
   - Ask if they can host the group immediately for a shortened ceremony.
   - Use a group text or digital "phone tree" to distribute the new address to all attendees.

7. **Check on vulnerable attendees during the transition.**  
   **Ensure elderly guests, children, and those with mobility aids have assistance moving to the new location.**
   - Verify that all personal belongings (handbags, coats, service programs) have been removed from the original property.

8. **Leave the premises completely once directed to do so.**  
   **Move the entire group off the private property including the lawn, driveway, and private parking areas.**
   - Remaining on-site after being told to leave can lead to arrest for criminal trespass in most jurisdictions.

9. **Use non-emergency lines if police presence is required for crowd safety.**  
   **Dial 311 or the local 10-digit police non-emergency number if you need help maintaining order while the group exits.**
   - Only dial 911 if a person is brandishing a weapon, making credible threats of injury, or if someone has a medical crisis.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine who is at fault for the cancellation right now.
- You do not need to review the fine print of the venue contract while attendees are standing outside.
- You do not need to decide on legal action or civil complaints today.
- You do not need to respond to social media inquiries or publicize the dispute.

## Important reassurance
A service is defined by the community and the memory of the deceased, not the physical walls of a building. Moving the group to a safer, more welcoming location is a protective act that preserves the dignity of the occasion. Panic or frustration from the family is a normal response to this disruption and does not mean the day is a failure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate venue or home-access crisis. It does not cover the resolution of contract breaches, refund requests, or long-term legal remedies.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. In the United States, religious organizations may have specific exemptions from federal public accommodation laws. If you are told to leave private property by the owner, you must comply to avoid criminal trespass charges, regardless of any existing contract or reservation.
