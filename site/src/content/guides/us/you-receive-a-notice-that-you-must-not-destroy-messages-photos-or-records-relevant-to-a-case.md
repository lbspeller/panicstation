# What to do if… you receive a notice that you must not destroy messages, photos, or records relevant to a case (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately cease all deletion or modification of records and disable auto-delete or disappearing-message settings on all relevant devices. Notify your legal counsel or your organization’s legal department immediately to ensure compliance with your legal duty to preserve.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete, edit, "clean," or selectively remove any data, including texts, DMs, photos, emails, browser history, or call logs.
- Do not factory-reset, wipe, or migrate data to a new device without explicit instructions from counsel.
- Do not turn on disappearing messages or auto-delete features; turn them off immediately if they are active.
- Do not rely on screenshots as a substitute for original messages, as screenshots lack required metadata.
- Do not open or "check" files unnecessarily, as this can alter "last accessed" or "last modified" metadata.
- Do not discuss the case or the preservation notice on the messaging apps or accounts that are subject to the hold.
- Do not ask others to delete communications or records on your behalf.

## What to do now
1. **Disable all automated deletion and cleanup tools immediately.**  
   - Turn off "disappearing messages" in apps like Signal, WhatsApp, or Telegram.  
   - Disable email retention policies that automatically archive or delete old messages.  
   - Stop any "storage optimization" settings that offload or delete older photos and files from your device.
2. **Secure all physical devices and accounts mentioned in the notice.**  
   - Identify every phone, tablet, computer, and external drive that may contain relevant information.  
   - Keep these devices in their current state; do not attempt to "organize" or "move" folders to make them easier to find.
3. **Review the notice to determine the specific scope of the preservation request.**  
   - Look for the specific date ranges, names of individuals, and subject matters listed.  
   - If the scope is unclear or broad, default to preserving all communications and files until you receive clarification.
4. **Confirm receipt of the notice in writing if required.**  
   - Follow the specific instructions in the notice regarding acknowledgment.  
   - If you are an employee, notify your supervisor or the legal department via a secure, official channel as directed.
5. **Start a simple preservation log to document your actions.**  
   - Record the exact date and time you received the notice.  
   - List every action you took to stop deletion, such as which settings you changed and which devices you set aside.
6. **Isolate devices from syncing if you are unsure of the effects.**  
   - If you believe a change on one device might trigger a deletion on another (syncing), stop using those devices and wait for technical or legal instructions.  
   - Do not "test" deletion or syncing behavior to see what happens.
7. **Avoid curating or judging what is "important" or "relevant."**  
   - Your legal duty is to preserve everything that *might* be relevant, not just what you think helps your side.  
   - Let your legal team handle the review and selection of evidence later.
8. **Consult with a lawyer before taking any forensic or collection steps.**  
   - If law enforcement or a government agency (like the DOJ or FTC) is involved, do not attempt to collect the data yourself.  
   - Use your lawyer or an appointed public defender to manage all interactions and data handovers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to respond to the underlying legal claims today.
- You do not need to produce or "hand over" documents immediately unless the notice is accompanied by a court order or subpoena with a specific deadline.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of data storage right now; preservation is the priority.
- You do not need to "fix" or restore things you deleted *before* you knew about the case; simply document what is currently available.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a preservation notice or litigation hold is a standard procedural step in American law and does not mean you have done anything wrong. Taking immediate, documented steps to stop deletion is the most effective way to protect yourself from "spoliation" claims or court sanctions.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate stabilization and the prevention of irreversible evidence loss. It does not cover the technical processes of e-discovery, forensic imaging, or the legal strategies of a specific case.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding evidence preservation (such as Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37(e)) and criminal obstruction vary by jurisdiction and case type. If you suspect relevant data was deleted before you took these steps, do not attempt to hide or "recover" it yourself; consult a lawyer immediately.
