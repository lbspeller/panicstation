# What to do if… a teacher, coach, or mentor asks you to communicate through secret or disappearing messages (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the conversation if you can do so safely. Move to a safer place or safer person, then tell one safe adult outside the situation. Call 911 if you need immediate police, fire, or ambulance help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to keep the messages secret, switch to a private app, or use disappearing mode.
- Don’t delete chats, images, or call logs because they ask you to.
- Don’t meet them alone to discuss the messages.
- Don’t confront them directly if you feel pressured, threatened, or unsure.
- Don’t investigate, search for more messages, or pressure yourself to decide what the messages mean right now.
- Don’t change chat settings if doing so could alert them or make you feel less safe.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.**  
   Leave the conversation if you can do so safely and move toward a place where you feel safer, such as somewhere with trusted people nearby. If you need immediate police, fire, or ambulance help, call 911.

2. **Create distance from the channel.**  
   Stop replying. Mute or block the account only if that feels safest. Do not move the conversation to another app or change chat settings if doing so could alert them or make you feel less safe.

3. **Only if it is safe, save a small amount.**  
   Only if it will not put you at risk, save or screenshot a small amount showing the account name and the request for secrecy or disappearing messages; do not search for more.

4. **Tell one safe adult outside the situation.**  
   This could be a parent, guardian, relative, friend’s parent, school counselor, or another adult you trust. You can keep it simple: “They asked me to use disappearing messages and keep it secret.”

5. **Consider a school support route if it is relevant.**  
   If the person is connected to your school, college, or school activity and you feel able, you may contact a principal, dean, school counselor, district office, or Title IX Coordinator. If sexual or sex-based conduct is involved, ask what support options are available, such as schedule changes or restrictions on contact.

6. **Use child-safety support if you are worried about a child.**  
   The Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline is a support line, not a child-abuse reporting line or a replacement for 911. Call 1-800-422-4453 or text GO to 1-800-422-4453 for support and help finding reporting routes in your state.

7. **Use sexual-violence support if anything sexual has happened or you feel sexually pressured.**  
   Contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline by calling 800-656-HOPE (4673), texting HOPE to 64673, or using its online chat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal complaint, contact law enforcement, or explain the situation to everyone.
- You do not need to write a perfect timeline or collect more messages.
- You do not need to warn people publicly or investigate on your own.
- You do not need to decide what the adult intended before asking for support.

## Important reassurance
An adult asking for secrecy or disappearing messages is a reason to pause and get support. You did not cause the situation by replying, feeling unsure, or taking time to tell someone. You are allowed to create distance and choose the next step that feels safest.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a school contact, child-safety service, sexual-violence support service, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If any step feels unsafe, prioritize reaching a safer place and contacting a trusted person. Call 911 if you need immediate police, fire, or ambulance help.
