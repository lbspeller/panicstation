# What to do if… you are asked to show proof of an airport departure fee or tax and cannot find a receipt (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the airline or airport information desk to identify the exact fee and the official office handling it. Ask what proof they will accept and whether they can verify the payment or issue a duplicate receipt before you make any further payment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a person who approaches you away from the official airline, airport, immigration, customs, or cashier channel.
- Do not hand an unofficial person your passport, phone, wallet, or payment card.
- Do not unlock your phone or give anyone access to your banking app unless you are choosing to show a specific transaction yourself.
- Do not send money to a personal account, payment handle, or transfer address because someone is rushing you.
- Do not argue with frontline staff. Ask calmly which official office can verify the fee and what proof is accepted.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact fee and the official payment channel.**
   - Ask: “What is the name of the fee or tax?”
   - Ask: “Which official office handles payment or verification?”
   - Use the airline desk or airport information desk to locate that office.

2. **Ask what proof is accepted.**
   - Ask whether staff can accept an itemised booking receipt, e-ticket, airline or travel-agent invoice, payment confirmation, or card transaction.
   - Ask whether the fee may already be included in your ticket.
   - Ask whether staff can check the booking or passenger record in their system.

3. **Check your booking records.**
   - Open the airline app, booking website, or travel-agent account.
   - Look for a receipt, invoice, e-ticket, payment confirmation, or taxes-and-fees breakdown.
   - Search your email using the airline or agent name, booking reference, and words such as `receipt`, `invoice`, `tax`, or `fee`.

4. **Check your payment record.**
   - For a card or mobile-wallet payment, open the matching transaction and show the merchant, date, and amount yourself.
   - For a cash payment, ask whether the official counter can verify the payment or issue a duplicate receipt.
   - Treat a bank or wallet record as supporting information unless staff confirm that it is accepted proof.

5. **Ask for official verification or a duplicate receipt.**
   - Ask: “Can you check whether this booking or passenger record shows payment?”
   - Ask: “Can you issue a duplicate receipt or written payment confirmation?”
   - Note the name of the official office handling the issue.

6. **If an additional payment is required, keep it traceable.**
   - Pay only through the official channel identified by airline or airport staff.
   - Use the payment method that the official counter accepts.
   - Ask for an official receipt or other official payment confirmation.
   - Photograph or screenshot the confirmation immediately.

7. **If you suspect a scam, pause and change channels.**
   - Step away from the person pressuring you.
   - Return to the airline desk or airport information desk.
   - If you are a U.S. citizen abroad and need urgent help finding a legitimate assistance route, use official U.S. Department of State information to contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve a refund, complaint, or card dispute while you are standing at the airport counter.
- You do not need to decide who made the mistake right now.
- You can organise screenshots, receipts, names, and notes after you have dealt with the immediate official verification or payment step.

## Important reassurance
You only need to deal with the next official step. Ask what proof is accepted, use the official channel, and keep a record of any payment or confirmation you receive.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Local departure-fee rules and airport processes vary by destination, and later refund or complaint decisions may need help from the airline, travel agent, card provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. U.S. embassy or consulate staff may explain available emergency-assistance options for U.S. citizens abroad, but they are not a substitute for the airport’s official fee-verification process. If you feel unsafe or pressured into an unofficial payment, prioritise your immediate safety and seek help from clearly identified airport staff.
