# What to do if… a stranger pressures you to reveal your phone number in person (USA)

## Short answer
Put your phone away, move toward a staffed or populated place, and decline the request. You do not have to let them call your phone or share your number.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand your phone to them.
- Do not unlock your phone and hold it out where it could be grabbed.
- Do not read out or show any verification codes or one-time passcodes.
- Do not stay to debate or explain. A brief refusal is enough. Leave if you can do so safely.
- Do not follow them somewhere private.

## What to do now
1. **Create space.** Step back, put your phone away, and move toward a staffed or populated place, such as a store counter, venue staff, or transit staff.
2. **Use a brief refusal.** Say: “No, I’m not sharing my number.” You do not need to explain or offer another contact method.
3. **Disengage if they keep asking.** Move closer to other people or tell staff that someone is pressuring you and you want help creating distance.
4. **Treat blocking, following, or threats as a safety incident.** If they block your path, follow you, threaten you, or become aggressive, call **911** when it is safe to do so.
5. **If your phone already rang, deal with the call after you are away.** If a number appears, consider blocking it. Do not reply to further pressure.
6. **If a verification-code message arrives, do not share the code.** If the message names an account, check that account using its trusted app, website, or a phone number you already know is genuine.
7. **If your cellular service unexpectedly stops or you notice an unexpected carrier-account change, contact your mobile carrier promptly using its official app, website, or a trusted phone number.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what the stranger’s intention was.
- You do not need to choose another way to stay in contact.
- Once you are safe, you can decide whether to report a suspected scam through the FTC or an unwanted call or text through the FCC.
- Later, you can ask your mobile carrier whether an account PIN or password is set up to help protect against unauthorized account changes.

## Important reassurance
You are allowed to refuse a contact request without explaining yourself. You do not need to work out why the stranger asked before moving away.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment and shortly after. Ongoing harassment, threats, or account-security concerns may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, prioritize reaching a safer place and contacting emergency services.
