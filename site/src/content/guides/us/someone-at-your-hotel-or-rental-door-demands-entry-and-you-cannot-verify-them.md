# What to do if… someone at your hotel or rental door demands entry and you cannot verify them (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and the lock engaged. Verify the request through an official channel you access independently, and call **911** if someone is trying to force entry, threatening you, or creating an immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door, partly open it, or remove the lock or latch because someone sounds official, urgent, or annoyed.
- Do not treat caller ID, a uniform, a badge, or a message alone as proof of identity.
- Do not call a number, open a link, or switch messaging apps because the person outside tells you to.
- Do not step into the hallway to negotiate if you feel unsafe.
- Do not share personal information, identification details, payment information, or travel plans through the door.
- Do not ignore a sounding fire alarm, visible smoke, or fire.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the room.** Keep the door closed. Use the lock, deadbolt, and latch if available. Speak through the closed door if you need to respond.
2. **Use one sentence to buy time.** Say: “I am not opening the door until I verify this through the front desk or booking app.”
3. **Verify through an official channel you access independently.**
   - **Hotel:** use the in-room front-desk button if available, the hotel’s official website that you access yourself, or the number in your booking confirmation.
   - **Rental:** use the host thread, support route, or contact details inside the booking app.
4. **Ask for confirmation before opening.** Ask the property to confirm the person’s name, role, and reason for entry. For a hotel, ask whether the front desk can call your room and whether another staff member or security can attend if available.
5. **Keep the door closed if the request is not verified.** You do not need to partly open the door while you are checking.
6. **Call 911 if you feel threatened or someone tries to force entry.** Tell the call-taker the hotel or rental address, your room or unit number, and what is happening. Answer the call-taker’s questions and do not hang up until you are told to do so.
7. **Respond to fire warning signs.** If a fire alarm is sounding or you see smoke or fire, use the nearest safe exit if you can. Use stairs rather than an elevator unless the fire department directs otherwise. Once outside, call 911. If you cannot leave safely, call 911 and follow the call-taker’s instructions.
8. **Move the conversation to a safer setting only after the situation is stable.** If the request is confirmed but you still feel uneasy, ask whether the issue can be handled in the lobby, another public area, or later.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the contact was a scam, a mistake, or a genuine property issue.
- Discussing policies, refunds, complaints, or reviews.
- Repacking, changing accommodation, or making longer-term travel decisions.
- Reporting the incident to a booking platform after you are safe.

## Important reassurance
Unexpected urgency can be used to push people into acting before they verify. Keeping the door closed while you check through an official channel is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions may need help from hotel management, the booking platform, local authorities, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
