# What to do if… police ask you to provide account statements or transaction records on short notice (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign consent forms or hand over records immediately; determine if the request is voluntary or a legal demand (subpoena/warrant) and consult an attorney to prevent over-disclosure.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t provide online-banking usernames, passwords, or Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) codes to any official.
- Don’t sign a blanket "Release of Information" or "Financial Authorization" form without a legal review of the scope.
- Don’t attempt to delete, edit, or "clean up" transaction history, messages, or digital receipts.
- Don’t assume your bank will notify you; under the Right to Financial Privacy Act (RFPA), notice can be legally delayed by a court.
- Don’t discuss the underlying investigation or explain specific transactions while you are still verifying the request.
- Don’t ignore a subpoena or court order directed at you personally, as these carry strict legal deadlines.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the official to clarify the legal basis of the request.**
   - Determine if they are asking for "voluntary consent" or if they are serving a "subpoena, search warrant, or court order."
   - Ask for the specific agency name, the badge number of the requesting officer, and the case number.
2. **Request the demand in writing with specific parameters.**
   - Ask for a written list of the specific accounts, the exact date range (e.g., January 1, 2026, to March 1, 2026), and the types of records sought.
   - Note if the request includes "metadata," "IP logs," or "associated device information" in addition to transaction history.
3. **Decline voluntary consent until you have consulted with a lawyer.**
   - Use a neutral statement: "I am not consenting to a voluntary release of my records at this time; I need to speak with my attorney first."
   - If you are on probation, parole, or supervised release, check your specific court conditions, as these may require you to sign certain financial disclosures.
4. **Direct the officials to use formal legal processes with the institution.**
   - You may inform the officer that they can seek the records directly from the financial institution (Bank, Venmo, PayPal, or Crypto Exchange) using the appropriate legal process.
   - This ensures the institution's legal department reviews the request for compliance with the Right to Financial Privacy Act (RFPA).
5. **Secure and preserve all original copies of legal paperwork served on you.**
   - Scan or photograph every page of a subpoena or warrant immediately upon receipt.
   - Record the date, time, and method of service (e.g., hand-delivered, certified mail, or email).
6. **Contact a criminal defense attorney or a public defender immediately.**
   - Ask the attorney to review whether the request is overly broad or if the date range can be narrowed.
   - Determine if there are grounds to "quash" (cancel) or "modify" the subpoena if it is directed at you.
7. **Prepare a disclosure log if you are legally required to provide records.**
   - List exactly which documents were provided, the date range included, and the date/time the records were sent.
   - Provide bank-generated PDF statements or CSV exports rather than screenshots to ensure accuracy and professionalism.
8. **Verify the delivery method for any records provided.**
   - Use an official secure upload portal or have your attorney handle the physical or digital transfer of documents.
   - Avoid sending sensitive financial data through standard SMS or unencrypted personal email.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide verbal explanations for any transaction while you are still in the document-gathering phase.
- You do not need to contact other parties involved in the transactions (e.g., employers or family) until your attorney advises on the impact.
- You do not need to provide records for accounts not specifically named in a legal demand.
- You do not need to agree to a "follow-up interview" regarding the records on the same day they are requested.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard and protective legal practice to ask for a request in writing and to seek counsel before releasing sensitive financial data. Taking a pause to verify the legal scope does not constitute "interference" or "obstruction" when handled through proper legal channels.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for individuals asked to provide personal or small-business financial records. It does not cover international banking regulations, corporate-level compliance for large entities, or national security letters.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. The Right to Financial Privacy Act (RFPA) applies to federal agencies; state and local police are governed by state-specific privacy laws. Always consult a qualified attorney when served with legal process or asked to waive your privacy rights.
