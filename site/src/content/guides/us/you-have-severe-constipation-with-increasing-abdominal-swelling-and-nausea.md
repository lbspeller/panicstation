# What to do if… you have severe constipation with increasing abdominal swelling and nausea (USA)

## Short answer
If you cannot pass gas or stool, your abdomen is swelling, you keep vomiting, you have severe or worsening abdominal pain, or you feel very unwell, go to an emergency department now or call 911. Severe constipation with increasing abdominal swelling and nausea can sometimes mean a bowel blockage or fecal impaction that needs urgent assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep stacking laxatives, suppositories, or enemas to “force it” when your abdomen is distended and you feel nauseated, unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not force a large meal or a sudden heavy fiber load while you feel blocked and swollen.
- Do not take extra opioid pain medicine unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not ignore inability to pass gas, worsening swelling, repeated vomiting, or severe pain, even if constipation has happened before.
- Do not drive yourself if you are weak, dizzy, confused, faint, or actively vomiting; get a ride or call 911.

## What to do now
1. Go to an emergency department now, or call 911, if any of these apply:
   - You cannot pass stool or gas.
   - Your abdomen is swollen, tight, or clearly getting bigger.
   - You keep vomiting or cannot keep liquids down.
   - You have severe or worsening abdominal pain or cramping.
   - You have fever, confusion, fainting, blood in stool or vomit, green vomit, brown vomit, or vomit that smells like stool.

2. If none of those emergency signs are present but the swelling and nausea are increasing today, call your primary care clinic or nurse line for same-day direction. If you cannot reach them promptly, it is reasonable to go to an emergency department for assessment.

3. While arranging care, do not try to “clear it out” at home with repeated medicines. If you are not actively vomiting, take small sips of water or an oral rehydration drink; stop if vomiting starts or symptoms worsen.

4. Write down a short triage note:
   - The last time you passed stool.
   - The last time you passed gas.
   - When the abdominal swelling started and whether it is getting worse.
   - Whether pain is constant or comes in waves.
   - Any vomiting, including green, brown, bloody, or stool-smelling vomit.
   - Current medicines, especially opioids, iron, anticholinergic medicines, or recent new medicines.
   - Recent surgery, hernia, bowel disease, pregnancy, cancer treatment, or a history of bowel blockage.

5. If you are alone, contact someone who can stay reachable and help with transport. If vomiting, severe pain, inability to pass gas, faintness, or confusion develops, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose the best laxative routine right now.
- You do not need to decide at home whether this is constipation, fecal impaction, ileus, or obstruction.
- Diet changes, supplements, cleanse products, and long-term constipation plans can wait until urgent causes have been ruled out.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to wonder whether you are overreacting, but severe constipation with increasing abdominal swelling and nausea is a sensible reason to get checked promptly. Getting assessed early can reduce the chance of complications and can help you get safer relief.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours. Later decisions about constipation treatment, medication changes, diet, testing, or specialist care may need a clinician who can examine you.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. If you cannot pass gas or stool, have a swollen abdomen that is not going down, keep vomiting, have severe or worsening pain, or feel very unwell, seek urgent medical care.
