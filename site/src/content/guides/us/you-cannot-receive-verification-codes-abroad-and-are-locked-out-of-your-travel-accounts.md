# What to do if… you cannot receive verification codes abroad and are locked out of your travel accounts (USA)

## Short answer
Pause repeated code requests. Identify the next travel action you need to complete, then use the simplest official route available: an existing alternative sign-in method, your carrier’s support route, or direct help from the travel provider.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep requesting new codes one after another. This can make it harder to tell which code is current.
- Do not share a verification code, backup code, or password with anyone who contacted you first.
- Do not install unknown recovery apps or pay someone who claims they can unlock an account.
- Do not move your SIM or eSIM between devices unless you understand how that may affect access to the current device and account.
- Do not use a public or shared computer for recovery unless there is no safer option. If you must use one, use only the official website, do not save credentials, and sign out fully.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the next travel action you need to complete.**  
   Focus on the immediate booking, check-in, accommodation, insurance detail, or document you need. You do not need to recover every account at once.

2. **Check whether your US phone line is active abroad.**
   - Confirm that your US SIM or eSIM is enabled.
   - Check whether the phone is connected to a local mobile network.
   - Over Wi-Fi, use your carrier’s official app, website, or support route and ask whether international roaming is active and whether inbound verification texts are supported or being blocked on your line.

3. **Use an official alternative sign-in method if the account offers one.**
   - Look for an authenticator app, backup code, passkey, security key, trusted device, recovery email, or another option already set up on the account.
   - Use only recovery choices shown in the provider’s official app or website.

4. **Recover your email first when it is the recovery route you need.**
   - Use the email provider’s official recovery options.
   - If you are already signed in on a trusted phone, laptop, or tablet, stay signed in while you restore urgent access.

5. **Contact the travel provider directly when the immediate booking matters more than the account login.**
   - Use the airline, rail company, hotel, or booking provider’s official website, app, or published support route.
   - Have your name and booking reference ready.
   - Ask how to access the reservation or receive the itinerary at an email address you can reach.

6. **Use the specific Login.gov route if the locked account uses Login.gov.**
   - If you previously added another authentication method, choose that option and sign in.
   - If your only authentication method is unavailable, Login.gov says it cannot unlock the account for you. Follow its official deletion and account-creation instructions.
   - Read the instructions before starting. When you no longer have access to your authentication methods, the official deletion route includes a 24-hour waiting period.

7. **Use the passport route only if your physical valid U.S. passport is lost or stolen abroad.**
   - Follow the U.S. Department of State instructions to report the passport lost or stolen.
   - Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate for replacement help.
   - A local police report is not mandatory, but it can help confirm the circumstances.

## What can wait
- You do not need to recover every non-urgent account now.
- You do not need to replace your phone, change your number, or redesign your security setup during the immediate travel problem.
- After the urgent travel step is stable, you can add another supported authentication method and store backup codes somewhere you can reach without your phone.

## Important reassurance
Verification texts or recovery methods can fail while you are abroad. You may still be able to handle the next travel step directly with the provider while account recovery is in progress.

## Scope note
These are first steps for urgent access and immediate travel continuity. Later account changes or provider-specific problems may need help from the carrier, travel provider, account provider, or relevant specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. Processes vary by provider and can change. Use official support routes for the account, carrier, travel provider, or U.S. government service involved.
