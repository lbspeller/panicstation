# What to do if… you are told a colleague has made a formal complaint about you (USA)

## Short answer
Do not contact the coworker about the complaint unless HR or management directs or permits it. Ask for the general allegation, the process, the next step, and any response deadline in writing where possible before giving a detailed response.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront, message, apologize to, or try to negotiate with the coworker about the complaint unless HR or management directs or permits contact.
- Do not ask coworkers to take sides, compare stories, pressure the complainant, or pressure possible witnesses.
- Do not delete, edit, or reorganize messages, files, calendar events, or notes that may be relevant.
- Do not post about the complaint on social media or discuss it in workplace group chats.
- Do not make threats, jokes, or off-the-record comments about the complaint.
- Do not secretly record meetings. Before recording, check applicable state law and employer policy, and ask whether recording is permitted.
- Do not forward or download restricted workplace information to a personal account or device.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the basics in writing.** Ask HR or the person handling the complaint for:
   - the general allegation and relevant timeframe
   - the policy or process being used
   - what they need from you next
   - any meeting date or response deadline
   - any contact restrictions you must follow
2. **Clarify the next meeting before it starts.** Ask what the meeting is for, whether you will be asked questions, whether your answers could lead to discipline, whether attendance is required, and whether you may bring a representative or support person.
3. **If you are union-represented, request representation when relevant.** If you reasonably believe an investigatory interview could lead to discipline, ask for your union representative before answering questions. Make the request clearly.
4. **If you are not union-represented, ask what the policy allows.** Ask whether you may bring a support person, representative, or witness. Do not assume this will be permitted.
5. **Preserve relevant records in place.** Keep relevant emails, messages, notes, schedules, and project records intact. Make a list of where the records are stored without moving restricted information to a personal account or device.
6. **Make a factual timeline for your own reference.** Note what you were told, who told you, when you were told, the relevant events, who was present, and which records may help clarify what happened. Do not assume your personal notes are confidential or legally privileged.
7. **Keep any initial response bounded.** If asked for an immediate detailed response, say: “I want to cooperate. I would like to review the general allegation and the relevant records so I can respond carefully.”
8. **Avoid actions that could look retaliatory or interfere with the process.** If you manage, schedule, evaluate, or assign work to the coworker, do not change their shifts, workload, access, evaluation, or day-to-day treatment because of the complaint. Ask HR how to handle any necessary operational changes.
9. **Use the appropriate private support.** Contact your union representative if you have one. Consider speaking privately with an employment attorney if the complaint could seriously affect your job or involves alleged discrimination or harassment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a long statement before you know the general allegation and relevant timeframe.
- You do not need to assemble character references immediately.
- You do not need to decide now whether to resign, transfer, or make a separate complaint.
- You do not need to repair the workplace relationship while the formal process is beginning.

## Important reassurance
A formal complaint is not itself a finding that you did something wrong. Your safest first steps are to preserve records, ask what process applies, avoid direct discussion with the coworker about the complaint, and respond carefully rather than quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial panic period. Later decisions may depend on your employer’s policy, your union status, the allegation, your state, and specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Workplace rights, employer policies, and available support vary by state, employer, industry, and union status.
