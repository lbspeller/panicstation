# What to do if… your bike lights will not turn on and you are worried about riding in the dark (USA)

## Short answer
Do not plan to ride into dusk or darkness without working lights. Use the daylight you still have to stop safely, try a quick fix, and switch to a non-riding way home if the lights are not reliable.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ride after dark assuming drivers will still see you.
- Do not hold a phone flashlight while riding; it can reduce control and may not meet local equipment rules.
- Do not switch to darker or quieter roads just because they feel safer.
- Do not stop to tinker in a traffic lane, at a blind corner, or on a narrow shoulder.
- Do not rely on reflectors alone once dusk, darkness, or poor visibility is approaching.
- Do not rush to beat the sunset if you already feel stressed or unsure.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe pause first.**  
   Get fully out of traffic, away from the curb edge if possible, and put the bike somewhere stable before checking the lights.

2. **Check how much daylight you actually have.**  
   Look at the time and sunset, then give yourself a margin. Treat dusk as the cutoff, not the moment it is fully dark.

3. **Do a fast reset of both lights.**  
   Toggle power and modes, check for a travel lock, reseat the light on its mount, and make sure it is not in a dim daytime or flashing pattern that is hard to see.

4. **Check power and simple connections.**  
   For battery lights, replace batteries if you have spares and make sure the battery door closes fully. For USB lights, try a different cable, a wall outlet, or a power bank, and look for a charging indicator.

5. **Dry and clean contact points.**  
   If the light or battery area is wet or gritty, wipe the terminals and battery compartment dry. Moisture, grit, or a slightly loose cap can stop the light working.

6. **Make the no-night-ride call early.**  
   Bicycle lighting rules vary by state and sometimes by city, but night riding generally requires a working white front light and rear red visibility, often a red rear light, reflector, or both. If you cannot get a reliable front and rear setup working, treat that as no riding after dusk or dark.

7. **Choose a way home while options are still open.**  
   Check whether nearby transit allows bikes, call someone for a pickup, request a ride-hail or taxi that can take a bicycle, walk the bike, or stop at a nearby bike shop or store for replacement batteries or lights.

8. **If you ride only briefly in clear daylight, reduce exposure.**  
   Take the most direct low-speed route with good sightlines, avoid left-turn-heavy intersections where possible, and aim to be off the road before dusk.

9. **Use any extra visibility you already have.**  
   Put on reflective or bright clothing, ankle bands, or reflective items from your bag. This helps others notice you, but it is not a substitute for working lights after dusk or dark.

## What can wait
- You do not need to open sealed housings, troubleshoot wiring, or repair a mount at the roadside.
- You do not need to decide which new bike lights to buy right now.
- You do not need to finish the ride just because that was the original plan.
- You do not need to work out the exact local rule before choosing the safer option.

## Important reassurance
This is common: dead batteries, a bumped switch, a loose mount, wet contacts, or a bad cable can all cause it. Noticing before dark gives you time to choose a calmer and safer way home.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep you visible, avoid a risky night ride, and get you home. Later decisions, including repair, replacement, or checking local bicycle equipment rules, may need specialist help or local advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, professional, transport, or safety advice. Bicycle lighting rules differ by state and locality, including what counts as night, whether flashing lights are allowed, and which reflectors are required.
