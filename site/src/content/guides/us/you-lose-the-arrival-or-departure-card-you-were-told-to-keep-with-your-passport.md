# What to do if… you lose the arrival or departure card you were told to keep with your passport (USA)

## Short answer
Check the official I-94 website first; many U.S. arrival and departure records are electronic and can be retrieved or printed. If the record is missing, wrong, or was a paper I-94, I-94W, or I-95 that you lost, use the official CBP correction route or USCIS replacement route instead of trying to recreate it yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make or buy a replacement card, stamp, or immigration document.
- Do not ignore it if the record shows the wrong name, date, passport number, or class of admission.
- Do not assume an airline, hotel, school, or employer can fix the immigration record for you.
- Do not send passport or visa copies through unofficial messaging channels.
- Do not pay for a replacement filing before checking whether your official electronic record is available, unless a qualified immigration adviser tells you there is a reason to act differently.
- Do not wait until airport check-in or a filing deadline to start checking the record.

## What to do now
1. Work out what you were told to keep:
   - If it was only a loose card or slip in your passport, treat it as possibly important.
   - If it said Form I-94, I-94W, or I-95, treat it as an official arrival-departure document.
   - If you entered the United States by air, sea, or land, your record may already be electronic.

2. Go to the official I-94 website and try to retrieve your most recent I-94:
   - Enter your details exactly as they appear on the passport or travel document you used to enter.
   - Save or print the record if it appears.
   - Check the name, passport number, date of entry, class of admission, and admit-until date.

3. If the I-94 site says the record is not found:
   - Try again using the document number and name format from the passport or visa you used at entry.
   - Check for simple spacing, order, or spelling differences.
   - Use the official I-94 help page if the record still does not appear.

4. If the record appears but is wrong:
   - Contact the CBP Deferred Inspection Site or port of entry that handles I-94 corrections.
   - Follow that site’s instructions, because some ports ask for correction requests by email and some use a different route.
   - Ask what documents they want before sending copies.

5. If you had a paper I-94, I-94W, or I-95 and it is lost, stolen, or damaged:
   - Check whether CBP can locate or correct the electronic record first.
   - If you are a nonimmigrant and still need a replacement or initial arrival-departure document, read the USCIS Form I-102 instructions before filing.
   - Keep a copy of anything you submit and any receipt or confirmation you receive.

6. Put together a small evidence folder:
   - passport photo page
   - visa page, if you have one
   - admission stamp, if there is one
   - flight, ship, or land-entry details
   - boarding pass, itinerary, or ticket receipt
   - any photo or scan of the lost card, if you have one

7. If your passport is also missing:
   - Treat that as a separate urgent travel-document problem.
   - Contact your country’s embassy or consulate in the United States.
   - Do not delay checking the I-94 record, but make identity-document replacement the immediate travel priority.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to change travel plans unless you are due to travel very soon. You also do not need to contact every agency at once; start with the official I-94 lookup, then use CBP correction or USCIS replacement only if the record cannot be retrieved or is wrong.

## Important reassurance
Losing the card or slip does not mean you have automatically lost your status or that you must leave immediately. Many people can retrieve the official record online, and there are official routes for correcting or replacing arrival-departure records when needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to protect your record, reduce immediate travel problems, and avoid unofficial fixes. If you have an upcoming immigration filing, an expired or unclear admit-until date, a removal history, or a complicated entry history, later decisions may need qualified immigration legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or professional advice. Immigration processes can change, and the right step depends on your status, entry route, and documents. Use official CBP and USCIS instructions and keep copies of everything you retrieve, send, or receive.
