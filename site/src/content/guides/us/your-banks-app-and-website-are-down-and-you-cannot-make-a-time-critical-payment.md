# What to do if… your bank’s app and website are down and you cannot make a time-critical payment (USA)

## Short answer
Call your bank immediately using the number on your physical card to request an offline payment, then contact the payee to notify them of the outage and request a temporary grace period. Use a branch or phone-based payment system to meet the deadline if digital channels remain inaccessible.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep submitting the same payment repeatedly as this often results in duplicate charges once the system recovers.
- Do not trust "alternative" payment links sent via text, social media, or email claiming to be from your bank or the payee.
- Do not move your money to a "safe account" suggested by an unknown caller; this is a standard scam pattern during outages.
- Do not share your full PIN, one-time passcodes (OTP), or complete security answers with anyone, including bank staff.
- Do not use unverified third-party "fast pay" apps or cryptocurrency to settle the bill under pressure unless they are official options offered by the payee.

## What to do now
1. **Verify that the outage is on the bank's side.**
   Check the bank's official social media accounts or a third-party outage tracker to confirm the issue is not limited to your device or connection.
   - If the website is down, try the mobile app using cellular data instead of Wi-Fi.
2. **Call your bank using the number on the back of your debit or credit card.**
   Ask the representative to process the payment manually through their internal systems.
   - **Ask specifically about "Same Day ACH"** if it is before the 4:45 PM ET national cutoff (individual bank cutoffs may be earlier).
   - **Ask for a Fedwire transfer** if the payment must be settled within the hour, noting that wires typically require action before 4:00 PM ET and may incur a fee.
   - **Check for FedNow or RTP (Real-Time Payments) availability**, which allow for instant settlement 24/7 if both the sending and receiving institutions participate.
3. **Locate the nearest physical branch if phone lines are overwhelmed.**
   Bring a valid government-issued ID and your account details. A banker can often initiate transfers or provide a cashier's check that can be hand-delivered or overnighted to the payee.
4. **Contact the payee (the person or company you are paying) immediately.**
   Inform them that your financial institution is experiencing a documented service outage.
   - **Request a "grace hold"** on your account to prevent late fees, service disconnection, or negative credit reporting.
   - **Ask for their official "pay-by-phone" number** or alternative payment locations (such as a local office or authorized payment center).
5. **Request a fee waiver from your bank for any costs incurred due to the outage.**
   If you are forced to pay for an expedited wire, overnight mail, or if the payee charges a late fee, document these costs and ask the bank to credit your account once systems are restored.
6. **Keep a detailed log of all attempts to pay.**
   Record the times you attempted to log in, the names of representatives you spoke with, and any reference numbers or "case IDs" provided by the bank or the payee.
   - Take screenshots of error messages or "site under maintenance" screens.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal regulatory complaint (e.g., with the CFPB) while the deadline is still active; focus on the payment first.
- You do not need to decide whether to change banks or move your primary accounts right now.
- You do not need to troubleshoot your local hardware extensively if the bank has confirmed a system-wide outage.

## Important reassurance
Outages are technical failures, not a reflection of your financial reliability. Most major creditors and landlords are familiar with banking disruptions and will offer short-term flexibility if you notify them before the deadline passes. Documenting your proactive steps is your best defense against late penalties.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a temporary digital banking failure. It does not cover long-term debt management or legal defense for non-payment unrelated to technical outages.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Deadlines for wire transfers and ACH vary significantly by institution. If you are facing an immediate legal consequence such as an eviction filing or foreclosure sale, contact a local legal aid office or a HUD-approved housing counselor for specific jurisdictional protections.
