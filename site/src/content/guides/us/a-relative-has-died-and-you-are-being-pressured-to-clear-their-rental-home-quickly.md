# What to do if… a relative has died and you are being pressured to clear their rental home quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before signing, returning keys, or agreeing to a clear-out date. Ask for the deadline and proposed handling of the belongings in writing, then get local legal-aid guidance on the tenancy, the belongings, and who may act for the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a termination, surrender, move-out agreement, or waiver on the spot.
- Do not return keys or agree that the unit has been surrendered until you have checked who has authority to make that decision.
- Do not assume that a named executor, a relative, or a landlord can decide everything immediately.
- Do not enter the unit, change locks, or begin a full clear-out if you are unsure whether you have permission.
- Do not remove disputed items, discard belongings, or agree to disposal before checking the local rules.
- Do not personally promise to pay rent, storage charges, or clear-out costs without checking whether the estate should handle them.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the demand in writing.** Request an email or letter stating the deadline, the lease term or rule being relied on, whether access will continue, what may happen to belongings left in the unit, and whether any charges are proposed.
2. **Ask for a short written pause.** Say that there has been a death and that you are checking who may act for the estate and what local rules apply. Ask the landlord not to remove or dispose of belongings during that pause.
3. **Locate the basic papers.** Find any lease, recent landlord messages, rent records, keys, will, and probate-court paperwork that already exists. Do not assume that finding a will automatically confirms who can sign or remove property.
4. **Check who may act before agreeing to anything.** Ask local legal aid about who may deal with the tenancy and belongings. Ask the local probate court clerk where to find local filing and procedural information. Tell the landlord in writing that authority is being checked.
5. **Document the unit only if access is already clear and safe.** Take photographs or video of each room and make a simple room-by-room list of belongings. Keep copies of messages and note the dates of calls.
6. **Treat an active lockout as urgent if anyone still lives there.** If a lawful occupant is locked out or access is about to be blocked, contact local legal aid promptly and ask which local court or self-help route applies. Call 911 for immediate danger.
7. **Ask for a manageable written plan.** Once authority and local advice are clearer, propose realistic dates for access and removal. Ask the landlord to confirm any agreed pause, access arrangements, and handling of remaining belongings in writing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who receives sentimental items.
- You do not need to accept the first deadline or sign a surrender before checking the local rules.
- You do not need to settle security-deposit questions, damage claims, or estate accounting now.
- You do not need to personally pay charges just to reduce pressure in the moment.

## Important reassurance
A short deadline does not need to be accepted or answered on the spot. Getting the demand in writing, checking authority, and asking for a brief pause can reduce the risk of belongings being lost while you get local help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a local landlord-tenant lawyer, legal-aid organization, probate professional, or court clerk because state and local rules differ.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules about tenancy, access, estate authority, storage, notice, and disposal vary by state and sometimes by city.
