# What to do if… you cannot find keys to a home after a death and the property seems unsecured (USA)

## Short answer
Physically secure the property immediately by locking all entry points or arranging a lock change through the designated executor, while documenting the state of the home and limiting access to one responsible person.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t break windows or force doors to gain entry unless there is a life-safety emergency or suspected fire.
- Don’t change the locks without the knowledge of the named executor or personal representative, as this can create legal liability regarding other heirs or co-owners.
- Don’t allow family or friends to enter the home to "browse" or "take memories" before a formal inventory is completed.
- Don’t post about the vacancy or the lack of keys on social media or in public forums.
- Don’t remove large items or furniture from the home without legal authorization, even if you believe you are protecting them.

## What to do now
1. **Verify if the property has been sealed by law enforcement.** If the person died at home and lived alone, police may have placed a "seal" over the door to protect the estate; if this is present, you cannot enter without an "Order to Examine the Residence" from the local probate (surrogate) court.
2. **Perform a thorough perimeter check for immediate hazards.** Walk around the exterior to identify open windows, unlocked sliding doors, or signs of recent forced entry such as pry marks or broken glass.
   - If you suspect a gas leak or fire hazard, do not enter and call **911**.
   - If you see signs of a burglary in progress, call **911** from a safe distance.
3. **Contact the person named as executor in the will immediately.** The executor has the primary legal responsibility to secure the home and its contents; if there is no will, the closest next of kin should contact the local probate court to begin the appointment of an administrator.
4. **Hire a licensed locksmith to secure the home if keys are truly lost.** If the home cannot be locked and no keys are found, the executor should authorize a locksmith to re-key or replace the locks to prevent unauthorized access by anyone who might have had spare keys.
5. **Secure sensitive items such as firearms or prescription medications first.** If you gain entry and find weapons or large quantities of medication, these should be placed in a locked cabinet or safe within the home until they can be legally transferred or disposed of.
6. **Take date-stamped photographs of every room and entry point.** Document the exact condition of the home as you found it, focusing on the status of locks, windows, and any visible valuables without disturbing the contents.
7. **Notify the homeowners insurance company within 30 days of the death.** Most standard US policies (HO-3 forms) contain a "Death Clause" that protects the estate, but many also have vacancy clauses that limit or void coverage for theft, vandalism, and water damage if the home sits unoccupied for more than 30 to 60 days.
8. **Prevent mail and package accumulation at the front door.** If mail is piling up, write "Deceased, Return to Sender" on envelopes and leave them for the carrier, or ask a trusted neighbor to move packages out of sight while the executor prepares a formal USPS change-of-address request.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to begin cleaning out the home or sorting through personal closets today.
- You do **not** need to decide which family members will receive specific pieces of furniture or jewelry right now.
- You do **not** need to pay utility bills or mortgage payments in the next few hours; these are handled by the estate later.
- You do **not** need to confront neighbors or acquaintances who claim they were "promised" a key.

## Important reassurance
It is completely normal to feel a sense of urgency and anxiety when you cannot find keys to a loved one's home. Security issues are common after a death, and taking steps to lock the doors and limit entry is a responsible act of protection for the estate, not a sign of distrust toward others.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate property security. It does not cover the long-term legal processes of probate, the distribution of assets, or the formal sale of real estate.

## Important note
Laws regarding property access and the rights of heirs vary significantly by state. If you are facing a dispute over home access or if the property is part of a complex estate, consult with a probate attorney in the county where the home is located.
