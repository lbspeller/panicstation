# What to do if… your building access badge stops working at work and you are told to wait outside (USA)

## Short answer
Submit your resignation and final working day in writing via email to create a permanent record—in all states except Montana, employment is "at-will" by default, meaning you can quit at any time and your employer cannot legally force you to continue working.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in verbal arguments or repeated in-person justifications for leaving.
- Do not sign new "retention agreements," "repayment forms," or contract amendments under pressure.
- Do not delete work emails or company records that prove you submitted your notice.
- Do not return company equipment without obtaining a dated receipt or written acknowledgement.
- Do not assume a "stay or pay" clause is valid without verification, as many are banned or restricted under 2026 regulations.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your employment status to determine notice obligations.**
   - Check if you are an "at-will" employee (the legal presumption in 49 states).
   - Review your offer letter or signed agreements for specific notice period requirements.
   - Determine if you are covered by a union Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA).
2. **Send a formal resignation via email to manager and HR simultaneously.**
   - **State clearly: "I am resigning from my position as [Title] effective immediately."**
   - **Include your definitive last day of work: "My final working day will be [Date]."**
   - **Bcc your personal email address to ensure you have a copy of the timestamped sent message.**
3. **Request your "Know Your Rights" notice if in a state requiring annual distribution (e.g., California SB 294).**
   - **Ask HR for a copy of the most recent mandatory labor rights notice provided to employees.**
   - **Review the section on "at-will" employment and resignation rights.**
4. **Issue a single written response if they verbally "reject" the notice.**
   - **Write: "I am confirming that my resignation was submitted on [Date] and my final day remains [Date]. This is a notification of my departure, not a request for approval."**
   - **Cease further discussion regarding the validity of the resignation once this is sent.**
5. **Request a written citation for any claimed "requirement" to stay longer.**
   - **Ask: "Please provide the specific page and section of the signed contract or employee handbook that requires notice beyond [Date]."**
   - **Collect copies of any cited documents for your own records immediately.**
6. **Verify final paycheck deadlines for your specific state.**
   - **Check if your state requires immediate payment upon resignation (e.g., California requires pay on the last day if 72 hours' notice was given).**
   - **Confirm whether accrued vacation or Paid Time Off (PTO) must be paid out by law in your jurisdiction.**
7. **Report threats of wage withholding to the appropriate labor agency.**
   - **Contact your State Department of Labor or the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division if the employer threatens to deduct pay for "early" departure.**
   - **Keep a log of who made the threat, the date, and the specific amount of money involved.**

## What can wait
- Whether you agree to provide transitional training for a replacement.
- Whether you participate in an optional exit interview.
- Whether you provide a reason for leaving or details about your next employer.
- Whether you pursue a formal legal claim for "constructive discharge" or "retaliation."

## Important reassurance
It is common for short-staffed employers to use high-pressure tactics or misleading language to delay a departure. An employer’s "refusal" to accept a resignation does not nullify your right to leave. Moving the interaction to a written, documented format often stops the escalation by creating a trail of evidence that management must answer for.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for standard employment resignations. It does not cover specific procedures for military personnel, government officials with security clearances, or medical professionals whose departure might involve "patient abandonment" rules.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including final pay requirements and the enforceability of "stay or pay" clauses (such as California's AB 692), vary significantly by state and contract type. If you are threatened with a lawsuit, face significant financial penalties for leaving, or feel physically unsafe, consult with your union representative, a state labor agency, or a qualified employment attorney.
