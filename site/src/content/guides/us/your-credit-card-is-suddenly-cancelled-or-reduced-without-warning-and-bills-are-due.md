# What to do if… your credit card is suddenly cancelled or reduced without warning and bills are due (USA)

## Short answer
Call your card issuer using the number on the physical card to verify the status, and immediately move any bills due within the next 48 hours to a different payment method to avoid late fees.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call numbers from unsolicited texts or emails about the cancellation; only use the number on the back of your card or the official bank app.
- Don’t repeatedly attempt transactions to test if the card works, as multiple declines can trigger secondary security blocks.
- Don’t apply for multiple new credit cards today; the sudden "hard inquiries" while your current credit profile is unstable can lower your score further.
- Don’t assume a "cancelled" card means you don't have to pay the remaining balance; interest and fees will continue to accrue.
- Don’t take out high-interest payday loans or cash advances without first asking your billers for a hardship extension.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the account status through the official issuer app or website.** Check if the account status is listed as **closed**, **suspended**, or if the **credit limit has been reduced**. 
   - Look for specific "Adverse Action" notifications in your secure message center.
   - Note any transactions you do not recognize that may indicate fraud.
2. **Call the issuer’s fraud or customer service department immediately.** Ask for the specific reason for the change and if there is a path to reinstatement.
   - **Why was the action taken?** (Common reasons include inactivity, high risk, credit score changes, or suspected fraud).
   - **Is the decision final?** If it was a security block, they may unblock it after you verify recent activity.
   - **How can I make an immediate payment?** Confirm the fastest way to pay the current minimum (phone payment, ACH transfer, or debit card) so you do not miss the due date.
3. **Request a written statement of the principal reasons for the action.** Under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), if the issuer takes "adverse action," they must provide a notice. If they only provided a general notice, you have the right to request the specific reasons in writing within 60 days, and they must respond within 30 days.
4. **Switch all essential autopayments to a backup payment method today.** Focus first on "survival" bills.
   - Rent or mortgage payments.
   - Utility bills (electricity, water, heating).
   - Insurance premiums (auto, health, home).
   - Phone and internet service.
5. **Contact billers for any payments due in the next 7 days.** If you cannot pay because of the card limit cut, call the company directly.
   - Inform them the payment method failed and you are resolving a bank issue.
   - Ask for a **due-date extension** or a one-time **late fee waiver**.
   - Request a temporary "hardship" arrangement if the card closure has caused a significant cash flow gap.
6. **Pull your free credit reports to check for errors or identity theft.** You can currently get a free credit report from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion every week.
   - Visit **AnnualCreditReport.com** (the only federally authorized site).
   - Check for any new accounts or inquiries you did not authorize.
7. **File a report if you suspect the closure was due to identity theft.** If you see fraudulent activity, go to **IdentityTheft.gov** to create an official FTC Identity Theft Report and recovery plan.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide whether to switch banks or cancel your other accounts today.
- You don’t need to fully diagnose your credit score or long-term financial health in the next hour.
- You don’t need to dispute the issuer's decision through legal channels until you have their written explanation.

## Important reassurance
Sudden card closures or limit reductions are often the result of automated risk algorithms and are not necessarily a reflection of your personal character or financial failure. Bank errors and fraud-prevention "false positives" happen frequently. Securing your essential bills today gives you the breathing room to address the credit issue formally.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate 24–72 hours following a credit interruption. It does not cover long-term credit repair, bankruptcy, or debt consolidation.
