# What to do if… you think there is an active warrant in your name (USA)

## Short answer
Do not rush into an in-person police or courthouse check just to ask whether a warrant exists. First verify safely, ideally through a criminal defense attorney or an official court contact you looked up yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not walk into a police station, sheriff’s office, or courthouse just to ask whether there is a warrant without getting legal advice first; you may be arrested if the warrant is active.
- Do not ignore it for days if there is a safe way to verify it.
- Do not make avoidable travel or ID-check plans until you have verified what is real.
- Do not pay anyone who contacts you claiming you must pay to clear a warrant, avoid arrest, post bond over the phone, or handle “preemptive bail.”
- Do not pay by gift card, wire transfer, crypto, payment app, prepaid card, or kiosk because a caller, text, or email told you to.
- Do not use paid unofficial warrant-check websites or people on social media offering to run your name.
- Do not post details online or ask many people to check for you; it can spread private information and create confusion.
- Do not carry weapons, drugs, someone else’s ID, or unnecessary paperwork while you are trying to sort this out.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a calm pause and write down exactly what happened.**  
   Write down who told you, what they said, any court, county, city, agency, case number, warrant number, date, phone number, email address, screenshot, letter, or voicemail.

2. **Treat payment demands as a scam until proven otherwise.**  
   If a call, text, email, or document says you will be arrested unless you pay now, stop responding. Do not call back using the number they gave you. Verify through an official court, sheriff, police, or agency number you find yourself.

3. **Contact a criminal defense attorney in the county or court area where the warrant may be.**  
   Say: “I need help verifying whether there is an active warrant in my name and the safest way to address it.”  
   If money is tight, ask a local bar association about referral or low-cost consultation options. You can also ask the local public defender office about eligibility, intake, and whether they can help before a court appearance; availability varies.

4. **If you cannot reach a lawyer yet, use the lowest-risk official checks first.**
   - Look for an official court case search from a state, county, or court website you reached yourself.
   - Call the court clerk using a number from the official court website and ask whether there is an active case, a missed date, or a next scheduled date.
   - If a sheriff or police warrant unit says you must appear in person to check, pause and try to get legal advice before going.

5. **If a warrant is confirmed, make a controlled plan instead of improvising.**  
   With a lawyer if possible, ask whether the warrant can be recalled, quashed, lifted, or the local equivalent; whether a court date can be set; whether a scheduled self-surrender is safer; and what booking, release, bail, bond, or conditions usually look like in that court.

6. **Prepare for the next contact without making things worse.**
   - Keep your phone charged and reachable.
   - Keep ID, court paperwork, medication information, and emergency contacts easy to find at home.
   - If you take regular medication, write a simple list of medication names and doses.
   - Choose one trusted person who can hold key information, help with transport, or look after urgent responsibilities if you have to appear.

7. **If you are in immediate danger, separate that from the warrant question.**  
   Call 911 if there is immediate danger. If there is no immediate danger, move to a safer place and then make verification calls from there.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to defend the whole case right now.
- You do not need to explain this to everyone in your life today.
- You do not need to write a statement, apology, confession, or long message.
- You do not need to negotiate with a caller, texter, email sender, or anyone demanding payment.
- You do not need to solve bail, work, family, travel, and the whole case at once; first confirm whether a warrant exists, where it is, and what kind it may be.

## Important reassurance
Information about warrants is often incomplete, delayed, or wrong, and scammers commonly use warrant threats to create panic. A verification-first approach helps you avoid the most harmful mistake: walking into an arrest risk or paying a scammer before you know what is real.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the initial panic moment. Once you know whether the warrant is real and where it is, the safest next step depends on the state, county, court, type of warrant, charge or case, and your history; local legal help may be needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws, court procedures, public defender access, warrant information, booking, bail, bond, and release conditions vary by state, county, and court. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
