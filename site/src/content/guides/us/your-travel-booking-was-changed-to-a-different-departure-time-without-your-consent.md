# What to do if… your travel booking was changed to a different departure time without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Save screenshots of the original itinerary, the changed itinerary, and any carrier notice. Contact the carrier immediately, say that you did not choose the change, and ask for the next workable option; for an airline itinerary, say clearly if you do not accept the changed itinerary and want the refund rules checked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tap an acceptance or confirmation option before checking whether the new departure works for you.
- Do not cancel until the carrier or its official terms show what cancellation will do to your booking.
- Do not accept a voucher, travel credit, or miles instead of a refund unless that is the option you prefer.
- Do not rely only on a verbal promise. Ask for an email, app update, or chat record.
- Do not leave an Amtrak booking untouched past departure if the new time does not work. A no-show can affect the value of the affected segment.

## What to do now
1. **Save the record.**
   - Screenshot the original itinerary, the changed itinerary, the booking code, the departure date and time, and any notice from the carrier.
   - Save emails, app notifications, and chat messages.
   - Note when you first saw the change.

2. **Check the booking status before tapping anything.**
   - Look for any wording suggesting that the itinerary was accepted, confirmed, exchanged, or recorded as a voluntary change.
   - Screenshot any status that does not match what you did.

3. **Contact the carrier through the fastest live channel.**
   - Use the official app, live chat, phone line, or a staffed desk.
   - Say: “My departure time was changed without my consent. I have not accepted the changed itinerary. Please show me the next workable option and confirm the outcome in writing.”

4. **For an airline itinerary, decide whether you can travel on the changed itinerary.**
   - For a flight to, from, or within the United States, DOT treats some changes as significant when the passenger does not travel or accept the changed itinerary, a rebooking, or other compensation instead of a refund.
   - The listed significant changes include:
     - a domestic itinerary departing at least 3 hours earlier or arriving at least 3 hours later;
     - an international itinerary departing at least 6 hours earlier or arriving at least 6 hours later;
     - a different origin or destination airport;
     - more connections;
     - an involuntary downgrade to a lower class of service;
     - specified connection-airport or aircraft changes affecting a passenger with a disability.
   - If one of these applies and you will not travel on the changed itinerary or an offered alternative, say: “I decline the changed itinerary and any alternatives I do not want. Please document that I am requesting the refund owed to the original form of payment.”

5. **If you still need to travel soon, ask for a workable departure.**
   - Ask whether the original departure can be restored.
   - Ask for the next departure you can realistically reach.
   - Ask whether another available route or another carrier, where offered, is possible.
   - Ask for the confirmed itinerary in writing before leaving the desk or chat.

6. **For an Amtrak booking, act before departure.**
   - Use Amtrak’s official website or app, or contact Amtrak promptly.
   - Ask what change options are available for your booking and what fare difference, refund, or forfeiture applies to your fare.
   - Get the outcome in writing and avoid an unintended no-show.

7. **If you need a backup booking to avoid being stranded, keep the choice as reversible as possible.**
   - Check the cancellation and change terms before paying.
   - Save the receipt and screenshots showing why you needed a backup.
   - Keeping records does not guarantee reimbursement.

8. **Leave complaints and compensation checks until you are stable.**
   - For an unresolved airline issue, save your record and use the DOT complaint process later.
   - For a controllable airline cancellation or delay, you can also review the DOT dashboard later to check the carrier’s published commitments.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to pursue compensation for extra costs.
- You do not need to write a perfect timeline now. Save screenshots and confirmations first.
- You do not need to argue every policy point while the departure time is close. Focus on a workable departure or a clearly documented refund request.

## Important reassurance
You only need to protect your options and secure the next workable step. A few clear screenshots and a written carrier response are enough for the immediate moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation. Later decisions about complaints, disputes, or additional remedies may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or other professional advice. The available options depend on the carrier, the itinerary, the fare terms, and the circumstances.
