# What to do if… you discover an adult is messaging your child and asking for sexual photos (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure your child is physically safe, stop all contact with the adult, and report the incident immediately to the NCMEC CyberTipline and the FBI (1-800-CALL-FBI) without deleting or re-sharing evidence.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront, threaten, or attempt to "shame" the adult; this can trigger the deletion of evidence or escalate threats (sextortion).
- Don’t pay any money, buy gift cards, or comply with any "one last photo" demands if the adult is using blackmail.
- Don’t delete the messages, the account, or the app immediately; law enforcement needs these records for investigation.
- Don’t forward, upload, or re-share any sexual images of the child to yourself or others, as this can constitute a separate legal violation.
- Don’t ask your child to show you the sexual images or "re-enact" the conversation; keep your focus on the fact that contact has happened.
- Don’t screenshot or download the sexual images specifically; only capture the surrounding text, profile names, and identifiers.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the device and stay with your child.** Take the device and move to a quiet space where you can stay together. Ensure the adult cannot send new notifications that might startle or pressure the child.
2. **Stop the contact without deleting data.** Block the user on the specific app and use the app’s internal "Report" feature for child safety/grooming. Do not close the account or wipe the phone yet.
3. **Preserve identifying information.** Use a separate device to take photos of the screen, or write down the following:
   - The adult’s username, handle, and display name.
   - Any links to their profile or associated phone numbers/emails.
   - The name of the platform (e.g., Discord, Instagram, Snapchat).
   - Dates and times of the most recent messages.
4. **Report to the NCMEC CyberTipline.** **Go to report.cybertip.org or call 1-800-843-5678 (1-800-THE-LOST) to file an official report.** This is the centralized U.S. system that routes information to the correct law enforcement agencies.
5. **Contact the FBI if threats or money are involved.** **Call 1-800-CALL-FBI (1-800-225-5324) or submit a tip at tips.fbi.gov if the adult is threatening to leak images or demanding payment.** The FBI specializes in these "financial sextortion" cases.
6. **Use "Take It Down" for existing images.** **Visit takeitdown.ncmec.org if sexual images were already sent.** This service allows you to generate a "digital fingerprint" (hash) of the images on the original device so participating platforms can automatically block or remove them without you having to upload the actual file.
7. **Notify local law enforcement.** Call your local police department’s non-emergency line (or 911 if there is an immediate threat of the adult showing up in person) to ensure a local record of the exploitation is created.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to understand every detail of how this started right now; the priority is stopping the contact and reporting.
- You do **not** need to notify the school or other parents this second—focus on the immediate legal and safety reporting first.
- You do **not** need to decide on long-term therapy or "grounding" measures while in this initial shock phase.

## Important reassurance
Adults who target children are experts at manipulation and coercion. Your child is the victim of a crime, regardless of whether they "replied" or "sent" something voluntarily. Taking over the situation now and handling the reporting is the most protective action you can take to stop the cycle of fear.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety and legal preservation in the USA. It does not replace legal advice or long-term psychological support.

## Important note
If you believe the adult knows your location or is threatening immediate physical harm, call 911 immediately. Reporting to NCMEC or the FBI is an essential step for investigation, but local police are the primary responders for physical safety.
