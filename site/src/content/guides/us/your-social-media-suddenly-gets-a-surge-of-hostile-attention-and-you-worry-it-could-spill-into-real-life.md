# What to do if… your social media suddenly gets a surge of hostile attention and you worry it could spill into real life (USA)

## Short answer
Cease all public engagement and activate privacy restrictions immediately to reduce your digital footprint. If you receive credible threats of violence or believe your physical location is compromised, contact local law enforcement via 911 or their non-emergency line to establish a record.

## Do not do these things
- Don't reply, "quote-post," or attempt to explain your position to hostile accounts.
- Don't post real-time updates of your location, commute, or identifiable surroundings.
- Don't delete hostile messages or threads before capturing clear evidence (screenshots).
- Don't accept private message requests from unknown accounts.
- Don't use your platform to "dare" harassers to find you or escalate the conflict.
- Don't interact with individuals claiming to be "mediators" or "neutral parties" who are strangers.
- Don't ignore specific mentions of your home address, workplace, or family members.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure location and pause digital activity.**  
   **If you are in public and feel exposed, go to a staffed, well-lit building like a library or police station lobby.** Stop scrolling and set your device to "Do Not Disturb" to prevent the psychological impact of constant notifications while you stabilize.

2. **Notify a trusted contact and establish a safety check-in.**  
   **Inform one person of the situation, your current location, and a scheduled time for your next update.** 
   - Ask them to monitor your public mentions so you do not have to view the vitriol directly.
   - If you feel unsafe at home, arrange for a friend to stay with you or relocate to a trusted secondary location.

3. **Enable maximum privacy settings across all platforms.**  
   **Switch accounts to "Private" or "Followers-Only" and restrict mentions, tags, and direct messages (DMs).**
   - Use "Restrict" or "Mute" tools for high-volume accounts rather than blocking, which can sometimes notify the harasser.
   - Remove any links in your bio that lead to your portfolio, workplace, or personal email.
   - Filter comments using keywords related to the current surge to auto-hide hostile replies.

4. **Sanitize your visible history for location cues.**  
   **Temporarily hide or archive posts that show your home's exterior, street signs, license plates, or your children's schools.**
   - Disable "Location Services" and "GPS tagging" in your camera and social media app settings.
   - Request that friends and family remove recent tags or "check-ins" that involve you.

5. **Capture and organize safety-critical evidence.**  
   **Take screenshots of any posts or messages that contain specific threats, your personal private information (doxxing), or impersonation.**
   - Ensure screenshots include the harasser's username, the platform timestamp, and the permanent URL (link) to the post if possible.
   - Store these in a dedicated folder or send them to your trusted contact for safekeeping.

6. **Contact law enforcement for physical safety concerns.**  
   **Call 911 if you believe someone is currently at your location or if you have received a specific, immediate threat of violence.**
   - For non-immediate concerns, call your local police non-emergency line to file an "informational report" regarding the harassment.
   - **Inquire if your local jurisdiction has an "anti-swatting" registry or a way to flag your address as a target of online harassment.**
   - For specific threats of terrorism or widespread violence, submit a tip to `tips.fbi.gov`.

7. **Alert professional or school security if they are identified.**  
   **If your workplace or school is being mentioned by harassers, notify their security or HR department immediately.**
   - Instruct them not to confirm your presence or schedule to any external callers or visitors.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to keep or delete your social media accounts permanently.
- Drafting a public apology, statement, or "setting the record straight."
- Checking the "metrics" or "reach" of the hostile posts.
- Responding to media inquiries or "interviews" about the situation.
- Investigating the identities or locations of the people attacking you.

## Important reassurance
The physical symptoms you may be feeling—shaking, racing heart, or "tunnel vision"—are the body's natural response to a perceived threat. A social media surge can feel like a physical attack because your brain is processing high-volume hostility as a survival risk. These feelings are temporary and will decrease as you regain control over your environment and digital access.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours of a harassment surge. It does not cover long-term legal action, reputation management, or recovery from identity theft.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not legal advice. Laws regarding cyberstalking and doxxing vary by state. If you are a victim of a crime, consult with law enforcement or a qualified legal professional.
