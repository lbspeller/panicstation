# What to do if… you get a needle-stick injury from a discarded needle in a public place (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately wash the wound with soap and running water for several minutes, then go directly to an Emergency Department for an assessment of HIV PEP (within 72 hours), Hepatitis B prophylaxis, and tetanus status.

## Do not do these things
- Do not scrub the wound aggressively or use bleach, alcohol, or peroxide on the skin.
- Do not squeeze or "milk" the wound forcefully to try and express blood.
- Do not put the injured area in your mouth or try to suck out the fluid.
- Do not attempt to recap, break, or carry the needle with you to the hospital.
- Do not delay seeking care to "see if it heals" if you are within the 72-hour window for preventative medication.

## What to do now
1. **Wash the wound immediately with soap and water.**  
   **Rinse the puncture site under a tap of running water for at least 5 to 10 minutes.** 
   - If blood or fluid splashed into your eyes, nose, or mouth, flush these areas with water or saline for 15 minutes.
2. **Cover the wound and note the time.**  
   **Apply a sterile bandage or clean cloth to the site once it is dry.** 
   - Record the exact time of the injury, as the 72-hour window for HIV Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (nPEP) is calculated from the moment of contact.
3. **Go to the nearest Emergency Department.**  
   **Choose an ER over Urgent Care to ensure immediate access to 28-day HIV PEP medication packs and rapid lab testing.** 
   - Urgent care centers often lack the specific antiretroviral medications required for immediate nPEP initiation.
4. **Clearly state the nature of the emergency to triage staff.**  
   **Tell the desk: "I have had a non-occupational needle-stick injury from a discarded syringe in public at [Time]."** 
   - This ensures you are prioritized for the 72-hour nPEP window and assessed for:
     - HIV PEP (highest efficacy if started within hours).
     - Hepatitis B vaccine or Hepatitis B Immune Globulin (HBIG).
     - Tetanus booster (if more than 5 years since your last dose).
5. **Prepare your vaccination history.**  
   **Check or recall your last tetanus shot date and whether you completed the Hepatitis B vaccine series.** 
   - If you cannot confirm your history, the clinician will typically treat you as "unprotected" and provide the necessary boosters.
6. **Report the location of the needle safely.**  
   **Do not touch the needle; instead, call 311 or the local health department to report the hazard location.** 
   - Use a physical marker (like a nearby trash can or a cone) to help authorities find it without staying in the immediate vicinity if you are alone.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the "source" person who used the needle; doctors treat community sticks as "unknown source" risks.
- You do not need to bring the needle for testing; hospitals generally do not test discarded needles as results are unreliable for clinical decisions.
- You do not need to decide on long-term follow-up testing schedules today; the ER will provide a schedule for blood work at 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months.

## Important reassurance
While the risk of contracting a virus from a discarded needle is statistically low (as many viruses die quickly outside the body), your anxiety is a natural response to a sudden health threat. Seeking medical care is the proactive way to manage this risk. Modern preventative treatments (PEP) are highly effective at preventing infection when started quickly.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and emergency triage only. Long-term monitoring and follow-up lab work must be managed by a primary care physician or infectious disease specialist.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not replace professional medical evaluation. If you have a compromised immune system or the wound shows signs of infection (pus, spreading redness, or extreme pain), seek medical attention immediately.
