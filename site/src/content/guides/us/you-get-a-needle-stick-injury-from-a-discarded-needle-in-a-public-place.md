# What to do if… you get a needle-stick injury from a discarded needle in a public place (USA)

## Short answer
Wash the puncture with soap and running water, cover it, and get same-day medical assessment. If it happened within the last 72 hours, say that clearly so a clinician can decide whether any time-sensitive prevention is needed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not scrub the wound hard, cut it open, suck it, or use bleach or harsh chemicals on your skin.
- Do not keep handling the needle, try to recap it, break it, or carry it around.
- Do not delay medical assessment if the needle broke your skin.
- Do not assume the clinic has understood the timing; say when it happened and that it was a discarded public needle.
- Do not rely on symptoms today to judge HIV, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, or tetanus risk.

## What to do now
1. Wash the puncture with soap and running water. Let any normal bleeding settle without squeezing hard, then cover the area with a clean bandage.
2. Write down the details you will be asked for: the time and date, where it happened, where on your body, whether it broke the skin, whether there was bleeding, whether it went through clothing, and whether you saw blood in or on the needle.
3. Get same-day medical care. Use an emergency department or an urgent clinic that can assess bloodborne-virus exposure, check tetanus and hepatitis B protection, do baseline blood tests if needed, give vaccines or immune globulin if indicated, and start HIV PEP immediately if a clinician recommends it.
4. If the first clinic cannot assess and start HIV PEP promptly when it may be needed, go to an emergency department rather than waiting for another day.
5. Use clear words at check-in: “I had a needle-stick injury from a discarded needle in public at [time]. It broke the skin. I need assessment for tetanus, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, HIV exposure, and whether HIV PEP is needed.”
6. Check your vaccine information if you can do it quickly. Bring or look up your last tetanus shot and whether you completed hepatitis B vaccination. Do not delay care to find records.
7. If the needle is still there, do not touch it. Keep children and pets away if you can do that safely, and report it to local non-emergency services, park staff, transit staff, building staff, or sanitation services.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to file a formal report.
- You do not need to photograph everything perfectly before getting care.
- You do not need to research every possible infection today.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions now; focus on wound care, same-day assessment, and any time-sensitive prevention a clinician recommends.

## Important reassurance
This is a frightening situation, and panic is understandable. A same-day assessment is there to sort the real risk from the fear, check your vaccine protection, and give you a clear follow-up plan.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Follow-up testing, vaccines, immune globulin, or HIV PEP depend on the wound details, timing, your vaccine history, and the clinician’s assessment.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a substitute for professional care. Seek urgent medical care now if bleeding will not stop, the puncture is deep, you feel very unwell, you are pregnant, you have a weakened immune system, or you develop increasing redness, swelling, warmth, fever, pus, red streaking, or worsening pain.
