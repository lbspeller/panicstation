# What to do if… you keep receiving unsolicited explicit images and it will not stop after you ask (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the hazard by blocking and reporting the sender on the platform immediately, and enable automated "Sensitive Content" filters to prevent further exposure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue the conversation to "explain" why the behavior is wrong, as interaction often encourages the sender.
- Do not forward or share the images with others, even for advice, as this may violate platform terms or state laws.
- Do not delete the message thread entirely before checking if you need a single record for reporting.
- Do not click any links or download attachments sent by the harasser.
- Do not attempt to "dox" or publicly shame the sender, as this can lead to retaliation or legal complications.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and create physical distance from the device.** **Put the phone or computer down for a moment to stop the immediate cycle of notifications and visual exposure.**
2. **Use platform-level tools to terminate the contact.** **Locate the "Block" and "Report" functions on the specific app or site you are using.**
   - Select "Harassment," "Sexual Content," or "Unsolicited Nudity" as the reason for the report.
   - Choose the option to block "this account and any other accounts they may create" if available.
3. **Enable automated content filtering in your device or app settings.** **Turn on "Sensitive Content Warnings" or "Nudity Blurring" features now standard on most US mobile operating systems and social platforms.**
   - On iOS/Android, check Privacy or Safety settings for "Sensitive Content Warning."
   - On Instagram/X/TikTok, check Privacy settings for "Hidden Words" or "Sensitive Content" filters.
4. **Restrict your incoming message permissions.** **Set your direct message (DM) settings to "Friends Only" or "People I Follow" to prevent new accounts from reaching you.**
   - Disable "Message Requests" from unknown senders entirely if the platform allows.
5. **Capture a single record of the harassment if you intend to report.** **Take one screenshot that includes the sender’s handle, the date/time, and the unsolicited content or threats.**
   - Store this image in a "Hidden" folder or send it to a trusted friend so you do not have to see it in your primary gallery.
6. **Report workplace-related harassment to your employer.** **If the sender is a coworker, supervisor, or client, initiate a report through your company’s HR department or designated harassment officer.**
   - Title VII of the Civil Rights Act protects employees at companies with 15 or more people from sex-based harassment, including digital sexual harassment.
7. **Escalate to federal authorities if extortion or threats are involved.** **File a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) if the sender is demanding money or threatening to "leak" your information.**
   - If there is an immediate physical threat to your safety, call **911** or your local police department.
8. **Report any content involving minors to the CyberTipline.** **If you suspect the images involve a minor, or if you were a minor when the images were taken, contact the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC).**
   - Use the "Take It Down" service provided by NCMEC to help remove explicit imagery from the internet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to identify the "real" identity of an anonymous sender today.
- You do not need to review your entire post history or change all your passwords immediately unless you suspect an account compromise.
- You do not need to respond to the sender one "last time" to get closure.

## Important reassurance
Receiving unsolicited sexual content is a digital boundary violation that can cause a genuine stress response, including disgust or anxiety. These reactions are normal. Taking the step to block and filter content is an effective way to regain control of your digital environment.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm reduction for online sexual harassment. It does not provide legal strategy for civil litigation or long-term cybersecurity auditing.

## Important note
While federal laws like Title VII and various state "Cyber-Harassment" statutes provide protections, enforcement procedures can vary significantly by jurisdiction. This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or safety advice.
