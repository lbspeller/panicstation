# What to do if… you feel unsafe walking to your car because someone is loitering along your path (USA)

## Short answer
Do not walk past the person or attempt to reach your car if you feel uneasy. Immediately return to a well-lit, staffed indoor area and request a security escort or arrange an alternative ride from inside.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue walking toward the person to avoid appearing "rude" or "paranoid."
- Do not confront, question, or make prolonged eye contact with the loiterer.
- Do not take shortcuts through dark areas, between parked vans, or down isolated stairwells.
- Do not look down at your phone or dig through a bag for keys while walking.
- Do not unlock the vehicle until you are physically next to the door.
- Do not remain in a parked vehicle with the doors unlocked or the engine off.

## What to do now
1. **Change direction immediately and return to safety.** Turn around and walk back to the nearest public, staffed entrance (e.g., store, lobby, security desk, or hospital entrance).
2. **Alert site staff or security of your concern.** Go to a reception desk or a cashier and state clearly: "I feel unsafe walking to my car because of someone loitering on the path. I need a safety escort or a safe place to wait."
3. **Utilize a security escort if available.** Many campuses, shopping centers, and workplaces provide "walk-out" services.
   - Provide your vehicle's make, model, and general location (e.g., Row G, Level 3).
   - Stay inside the staffed building until the escort is physically present.
4. **Arrange a different exit if an escort is unavailable.** If you cannot get a staff escort, call a friend to meet you inside the building or request a rideshare to pick you up directly at the main entrance.
   - If using a rideshare, verify the driver's name, photo, and license plate before approaching the vehicle.
5. **Call 911 if you are being followed or blocked.** If the person begins moving toward you or prevents you from returning to the building, call emergency services immediately.
   - State your exact location first (business name, address, and which side of the building you are on).
   - Describe the person's appearance and current actions.
6. **Execute a "get in and go" procedure if you reach the car.** If you choose to proceed with an escort or after the person has left:
   - **Have your keys in your hand before leaving the building.**
   - **Scan under and around the vehicle as you approach.**
   - **Check the back seat through the window before opening the door.**
   - **Get in, lock all doors immediately, and start the engine.**
7. **Use your car alarm as a deterrent.** If you are approached while walking to the car, press and hold the panic/alarm button on your key fob to draw immediate attention to your location.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person is "actually" a threat before seeking help.
- You do not need to take photos or video of the person if doing so keeps you in the area longer.
- You do not need to explain your departure to anyone other than safety personnel.
- You do not need to file a formal police report in the moment unless an actual crime has occurred; your priority is leaving the area.

## Important reassurance
Trusting your intuition is a proactive safety measure. Choosing to turn back or ask for help is a standard response to an environmental red flag and is not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and harm-reduction steps for navigating a perceived threat while walking to a vehicle. It does not cover long-term self-defense training or legal definitions of loitering.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, contact 911 immediately.
