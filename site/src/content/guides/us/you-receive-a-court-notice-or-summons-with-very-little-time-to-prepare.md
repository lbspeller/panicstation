# What to do if… you receive a court notice or summons with very little time to prepare (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as important until the court confirms otherwise. Verify the case, date, and required appearance through official court contact details, then take same-day steps to avoid a missed appearance or missed response deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice because it arrived late or feels confusing; a missed court date or missed response deadline may lead to serious consequences.
- Don’t rely only on a phone number printed on a suspicious document; look up the court through an official court website or directory.
- Don’t pay anyone who calls, texts, or emails demanding money to cancel a warrant or stop an arrest.
- Don’t contact the opposing party in panic or anger.
- Don’t post the notice online; it may show personal information, addresses, case numbers, or allegations.
- Don’t assume a new date is approved just because you asked for one; wait for court confirmation.

## What to do now
1. **Find the key details on the notice.**  
   Write down the court name, court address, case number, date, time, courtroom or remote link, and whether the paper says summons, notice to appear, order, hearing notice, subpoena, or citation.

2. **Verify the notice with the court through official contact details.**  
   Look up the court using an official court website, official state court directory, county court site, or the U.S. Courts directory for federal court. Ask: “Can you confirm this case number, the hearing date and time, and what I am required to do?”

3. **Check whether the court has an online case lookup.**  
   Use only the court’s official lookup system if one is available. Confirm that the case number, party names, hearing date, and court location match the notice.

4. **Work out your role in the case.**  
   If you are being sued, charged, cited, or listed as a party, you may need to appear and may also have a response deadline. If you are a witness or the notice is a subpoena, identify who issued it, what it asks you to do, and whether it asks for documents, testimony, or both.

5. **If the case is about you, seek legal help today.**  
   For a criminal or traffic case with possible penalties, contact a criminal defense lawyer, your assigned defender if you already have one, or ask the court how to request appointed counsel if you cannot afford a lawyer. For a civil, family, housing, debt, or benefits case, contact legal aid or a state or local lawyer referral service and say: “I received a notice to appear on short notice, and the hearing is on [date].”

6. **If you cannot attend, ask the court the exact process immediately.**  
   Ask the clerk: “What is the correct way to request a continuance, reschedule, or remote appearance, if the judge allows it?” The clerk can usually explain forms and procedure, but cannot give legal advice. Do not miss the hearing unless the court confirms the change.

7. **If it is a subpoena, respond through the proper channel.**  
   Note who issued it, what it requires, and the date, time, place, or document deadline. Federal civil subpoenas have specific rules, including possible objections or a request to quash or modify; state and other subpoenas vary, so check the issuing court’s procedure or get legal help quickly.

8. **Make attendance realistic if you can go.**  
   Plan transport, time off work, childcare, parking, building security, and arrival time. Bring photo ID, the notice, envelopes, proof of service or delivery, related paperwork, and a charged phone.

9. **Create a simple paper trail.**  
   Save the notice, screenshots of the official case listing, emails, voicemails, and proof of anything you file or send. Write down the name of anyone you speak with, the office, date, time, phone number, and what they told you.

## What can wait
You do not need to build your whole case tonight, write a full argument, confront anyone, or decide your long-term strategy. The priority is to verify the notice, contact the correct court channel, and avoid missing a required appearance or deadline.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice court paper can feel alarming, but you can still take useful first steps. Verifying the notice and using the court’s official process is usually safer than guessing, rushing, or going silent.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the court, state, case type, whether you are a party or witness, and whether the matter is criminal, civil, family, traffic, municipal, federal, or administrative.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Court rules and consequences vary by state, court, and case type; a qualified attorney, legal aid organization, or the court’s self-help office may be needed for your situation.
