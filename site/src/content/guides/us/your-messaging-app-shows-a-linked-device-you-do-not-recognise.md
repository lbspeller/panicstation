# What to do if… your messaging app shows a linked device you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the alert as possible account access: remove the unknown linked device in the messaging app now, then secure the account or phone number the app depends on from a device you trust and control.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t message the unknown device, negotiate, or wait to “see what happens.”
- Don’t approve QR pairing, login approvals, or new-device prompts you did not start.
- Don’t share verification codes, reset links, or screenshots with anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t reuse an old password or a small variation of one.
- Don’t announce it publicly before warning key contacts who could be tricked by impersonation.

## What to do now
1. **Remove the unknown linked device first.** In the messaging app, look for **Linked devices**, **Companion devices**, **Device pairing**, **Messages for web**, or **Sessions**, then log out or remove anything you do not recognize.
2. **Check whether any other sessions are open.** Review the whole linked-device list, not just the newest alert, and remove any session you cannot confidently identify.
3. **If you can do it safely, save quick proof for yourself.** Take a screenshot of the device name, last active time, browser, or location shown, and store it somewhere only you can access.
4. **Secure the account the app depends on.** From a device you trust and control, change the password for the connected Google, Apple, email, or app account if that account has a password.
5. **Turn on extra sign-in protection.** Enable two-factor authentication or the app’s account-protection setting where available, such as two-step verification, registration lock, or device approval.
6. **Sign out of unfamiliar account devices.** In the Google, Apple, email, or app security settings, review signed-in devices and sessions, then sign out of anything you do not recognize.
7. **Protect your email account.** Check for new forwarding, filters, rules, unfamiliar recovery email addresses or phone numbers, and unknown trusted devices.
8. **Check for phone-number takeover signs.** If you suddenly lost service, received carrier alerts about SIM or number-transfer changes, or cannot receive calls or texts, contact your mobile carrier using its official app, website, or a known official number. Ask them to check for unauthorized SIM changes or number transfers and to add any available account protection.
9. **Warn people who could be targeted.** Use a different channel if possible and tell key contacts or group admins: “My messaging account may have been accessed. Please do not click links or act on unusual requests from me.”
10. **If the device comes back or you cannot remove it, use official recovery.** Go directly to the app provider’s official website or in-app help and follow its account recovery or support process.
11. **If there is fraud, threats, or identity misuse, use official reporting routes.** For cyber-enabled fraud, you can report through IC3. For misuse of personal identity information, IdentityTheft.gov can guide recovery steps.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to know exactly how it happened before removing the device.
- You don’t need to confront anyone or make a public post right now.
- You don’t need to replace your phone, factory-reset it, or buy security tools unless the access returns or a trusted support source tells you there are signs of device compromise.
- You don’t need to decide now whether to make a police report, a carrier complaint, or a formal support case unless there is immediate danger, fraud, threats, or ongoing misuse.

## Important reassurance
An unknown companion device is serious, but removing it quickly often stops that particular access. If it returns, that is a sign to secure the underlying account, phone number, and recovery routes rather than blame yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop access and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions, such as provider recovery, carrier security, identity-theft steps, or specialist technical help, may depend on what you find after the unknown device is removed.
