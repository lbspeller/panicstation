# What to do if… you see a new fluid leak under your vehicle and you can’t identify what it is (USA)

## Short answer
Assume any unidentified new fluid leak is safety-critical: do not drive the vehicle if you suspect a fuel, brake, or cooling system failure. If the fluid smells of gasoline, move at least 100 feet away and call emergency services immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep driving to see if the leak stops on its own.
- Don’t touch the liquid with bare skin or inhale vapors directly.
- Don’t use flares, lighters, or any ignition source within 50 feet of the vehicle.
- Don’t use a cell phone or electronic device in the immediate presence of strong gasoline fumes.
- Don’t crawl under a vehicle that is only supported by a tire jack.
- Don’t flush the spill into storm drains, sewers, or onto unpaved ground.
- Don’t ignore dashboard warning lights for oil pressure, engine temperature, or brakes.

## What to do now
1. **Move the vehicle to a safe location if it is still operable and the leak is minor.**  
   Pull into a flat parking lot or driveway away from moving traffic if the vehicle does not smell of fuel and no critical warning lights are on. If you are on a highway shoulder and cannot move further, stay buckled inside the vehicle or move 100 feet behind a safety barrier.

2. **Isolate the hazard by turning off the engine and removing the key.**  
   Stopping the engine prevents pressurized fluids from continuing to spray and reduces the risk of ignition. If you smell gasoline or see smoke, evacuate all passengers and move at least 100 feet upwind.

3. **Conduct a visual-only assessment of the fluid and the dashboard.**  
   **Without touching the spill, observe the color and consistency of the liquid.**  
   - Clear and odorless (likely air conditioning condensation; usually safe).  
   - Bright green, orange, or pink with a sweet smell (coolant; do not drive if the reservoir is empty).  
   - Dark brown or black and slippery (engine oil; do not drive if the dipstick is low).  
   - Pale yellow or clear and oily (brake fluid; do not drive).  
   - Rainbow sheen or strong chemical odor (fuel; do not drive).

4. **Verify safety-critical fluid levels only if the engine is cool.**  
   **Open the hood and check the levels for engine oil, coolant, and brake fluid.**  
   - Use the dipstick for oil.  
   - Check the translucent reservoir for coolant (never open a hot radiator cap).  
   - View the brake fluid reservoir level through the side of the container.  
   - If any level is below the "minimum" or "add" line, the vehicle requires professional service before driving.

5. **Contact professional assistance for a recovery or tow.**  
   **Call a roadside assistance provider or a local towing company and inform them of an "unidentified fluid leak."**  
   - Specify if the fluid is flammable or if the brakes feel soft.  
   - Request a tow to a certified repair facility to prevent mechanical seizure or fire.

6. **Secure the area to prevent environmental contamination.**  
   **If the leak is on a private driveway or parking spot, cover the spill with an absorbent material.**  
   - Use cat litter, sand, or specialized spill kits to soak up the fluid.  
   - Sweep up the saturated material for proper disposal according to local hazardous waste rules.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact component that failed right now.
- You do not need to clean the stains on the pavement until the vehicle is moved.
- You do not need to decide on a specific repair plan until a technician provides a diagnostic report.

## Important reassurance
Sudden leaks are common and often look more dramatic than the actual repair required. Taking the time to stop and check the fluid levels is a standard safety procedure that prevents permanent engine damage or road accidents.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active vehicle leak in the USA. It is not a mechanical diagnostic tool or a substitute for professional inspection.

## Important note
This information is for general safety awareness and does not replace your vehicle manufacturer's specific emergency protocols. If you see active fire, smell strong gasoline, or lose braking power, call 911 immediately. Handling automotive fluids carries risks of chemical burns or toxicity; use personal protective equipment if cleanup is required.
