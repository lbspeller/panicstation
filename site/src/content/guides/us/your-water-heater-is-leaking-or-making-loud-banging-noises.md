# What to do if… your water heater is leaking or making loud banging noises (USA)

## Short answer
If you smell gas, a carbon monoxide alarm is sounding, there is smoke or fire, or water is near electrical equipment, get everyone away from the area and call 911 from a safe place. If it is safe to approach, turn off the water heater’s energy source and close the cold-water supply valve to the heater.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep running the heater to see if it stops; leaks, steam, and loud popping can worsen.
- Do not touch the heater, wiring, switches, breakers, cords, or panels while standing in water.
- Do not try to plug, cap, clamp, or block the temperature and pressure relief discharge pipe.
- Do not relight a pilot, reset controls, unplug anything, or operate switches if you smell gas.
- Do not open the drain valve, relief valve, access panels, or fittings during the panic moment.
- Do not put your face, hands, or body near steam, hissing water, or forceful discharge.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger before touching anything.**
   - If you smell gas, a carbon monoxide alarm is sounding, or anyone feels dizzy, nauseated, weak, confused, or short of breath, get everyone outside into fresh air.
   - From outside or another safe place, call 911. You can also call your gas utility’s emergency line if you have it, but do not delay leaving to look for it.

2. **Keep away from electricity and standing water.**
   - If water is touching outlets, power strips, cords, the electrical panel, or the heater’s electrical access panels, do not step into it or reach over it.
   - Keep people and pets out of the wet area.

3. **Turn off the heater’s energy source only if you can do it safely.**
   - **Electric water heater:** switch off the dedicated water-heater breaker at the electrical panel if the panel is dry and you can stand on a dry surface.
   - **Gas water heater:** if there is no gas smell and you can reach it safely, use the heater control or a clearly labeled appliance gas shutoff only if you already know how to turn it off.
   - If you are unsure, smell gas, or would have to reach over water, steam, or wiring, leave it alone and call for help.
   - Leave the heater off until a qualified person has checked it.

4. **Stop more water from feeding the heater.**
   - Turn the cold-water shutoff valve on the pipe entering the water heater clockwise until closed.
   - If you cannot find that valve, cannot move it, or the leak is heavy, shut off the home’s main water valve if you can do that safely.

5. **Limit water damage from a safe position.**
   - Move nearby boxes, chemicals, electronics, and valuables away from the wet area if you can do it without stepping in water.
   - Put a bucket, pan, or towels under a small drip only if the area is dry and clear of electrical hazards.
   - Do not work under steam, spray, or hot discharge.

6. **Back away and escalate if the heater seems pressurized or unstable.**
   - If you see steam, forceful discharge from the relief pipe, bulging, hissing, violent banging, smoke, fire, gas smell, or water around electricity, leave the area.
   - Call 911 if there is active danger or you cannot safely shut off power, gas, or water without approaching that danger.

7. **Call the right repair help after the immediate danger is controlled.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or property manager’s emergency maintenance line and say there is a leaking or noisy water heater and whether power, gas, and water have been shut off.
   - If you own, call a qualified plumber, licensed where required. If there is any gas or carbon monoxide concern, contact the gas utility from a safe location as well.

8. **Record only what is easy and safe.**
   - Take a quick photo or video of the leak location, surrounding water, and any visible damage.
   - Do not move panels, open valves, or disassemble anything to get a better photo.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is sediment, a failing tank, a pipe connection, or a relief-valve problem right now.
- You do not need to drain the heater, test the relief valve, remove covers, relight the pilot, or reset controls.
- You do not need to decide on repair versus replacement today.
- You do not need to start cleanup in any area with standing water near electricity.

## Important reassurance
This is scary because it can involve hot water, pressure, gas, electricity, and damage to your home. Getting people away from danger, shutting off energy and water when safe, and calling for help usually buys time and prevents the situation from getting worse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A qualified plumber, gas utility, electrician, landlord maintenance team, insurer, or local emergency service may need to help with later decisions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, electrical, plumbing, insurance, or other professional advice. Follow emergency services, your utility, your landlord or property manager, and qualified professionals for your specific situation.
