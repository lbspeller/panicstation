# What to do if… you are a friend of someone who has died with no known family and you are being pressured to take responsibility (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign or pay anything unless you have decided, after reading it, that you personally accept the responsibility. Get the official case contact, say clearly that you are a friend and not next of kin, and ask which county or court process applies when no eligible person comes forward.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything that makes you financially responsible unless you truly intend to pay.
- Do not sign anything saying you are next of kin, executor, personal representative, or estate administrator unless that is true or a court has appointed you.
- Do not pay deposits, funeral bills, storage fees, rent, or cleanup costs from your own money just to keep things moving.
- Do not take cash, documents, valuables, keys, vehicles, or property for safekeeping unless law enforcement, the medical examiner, the coroner, or a court-appointed person tells you to.
- Do not clear the home, distribute belongings, cancel accounts, or negotiate with creditors as if you control the estate.
- Do not let urgency from a funeral home, landlord, hospital, or workplace rush you into signing before you understand what the document does.

## What to do now
1. **If the death has not been formally handled, call 911.**  
   If you have found the person, there is any uncertainty, or anything seems unsafe or suspicious, call 911. If professionals are already involved, ask which office has the case now.

2. **Get one official point of contact and a case or report number.**  
   Ask for the name, office, phone number, and case or report number for the police, medical examiner, coroner, hospital, facility, or county office handling the death. Write down the date and who you spoke with.

3. **State your boundary before any paperwork.**  
   Use plain wording: “I am a friend. I can provide information and possible contacts, but I am not next of kin, I am not the executor, and I am not agreeing to be financially responsible.”

4. **Ask two separate questions.**  
   - “Who has authority to release or arrange disposition of the remains if no next of kin is located?”  
   - “Who handles the estate, property, apartment, bills, and belongings if there is no will or no one willing to administer the estate?”

5. **Give information without taking control of assets.**  
   Share what you know: full name, date of birth if known, address, workplace, doctor or facility if known, possible relatives, close friends, religious contacts, military history if known, pets, and any funeral wishes you personally know about. Use your own phone, messages, or memory where possible rather than removing items from the person’s home.

6. **If a funeral home contacts you, keep your role limited.**  
   Say: “Please list me only as a friend or contact person. Before I sign anything, I need to know whether it makes me responsible for payment or says I have legal authority.” If the form does either of those things and you do not want that role, do not sign it.

7. **If the residence is involved, protect access without taking over.**  
   If you are already inside and it is safe, lock the door when leaving. Tell the landlord, property manager, or building staff that the tenant has died and that you are only a friend contact. Give only the official case contact you have; do not agree to clear the unit or remove property.

8. **If there are pets, ask the official contact what to do.**  
   Tell the police, medical examiner, coroner, hospital, facility, landlord, or animal control that pets may be inside and need care. Do not enter unsafe property or take animals without clear permission.

9. **If someone keeps pressing you to sign, redirect them.**  
   Say: “I can provide information, but I cannot sign as responsible party or legal decision-maker. Please contact the medical examiner, coroner, county office, probate court, or public administrator process for this location.”

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to arrange a funeral, cremation, memorial, obituary, or service.
- You do not have to pay anything now to prove you cared about the person.
- You do not have to clear housing, collect belongings, contact creditors, close accounts, or work out probate today.
- You do not have to solve every family-contact lead yourself; give the leads to the official contact and let the proper process continue.
- You do not have to become the estate administrator just because you were a good friend.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to help does not automatically mean you must take legal or financial responsibility. You can still help by giving accurate information, being reachable, and pointing people to the official case contact while keeping a clear boundary about signing and paying.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: confirm the right authority is involved, avoid accidental financial or legal responsibility, and preserve the person’s affairs for the proper county or court process. Later decisions about funeral arrangements, estate administration, housing, property, and debts may need guidance from the relevant county office, probate court, public administrator, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, funeral, estate, or professional advice. U.S. rules vary by state and county, and offices use different names. Before signing anything, ask the medical examiner, coroner, hospital or facility office handling decedent affairs, probate court clerk, public administrator, or county office what the document means and whether it creates personal responsibility.
