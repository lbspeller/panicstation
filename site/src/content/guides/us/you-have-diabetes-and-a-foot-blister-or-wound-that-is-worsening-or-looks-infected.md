# What to do if… you have diabetes and a foot blister or wound that is worsening or looks infected (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your diabetes clinician, primary care clinician, or podiatrist right away today for a worsening or infected-looking foot blister or wound. Call 911 or go to the ER now if you feel very sick, confused, have fever or chills, rapidly spreading redness or swelling, red streaks, pus or odor with feeling unwell, or white, blue, purple, or black skin on or near the wound.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait a couple of days if the wound is worsening, warm, red, swollen, draining, smelly, or you feel unwell.
- Do not pop, cut, shave, or trim the blister, wound, corn, or callus yourself.
- Do not soak the foot or scrub the wound.
- Do not put antiseptic creams, powders, alcohol, peroxide, or home remedies on broken skin unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not walk around barefoot, in socks only, or in shoes that rub the area.
- Do not keep putting pressure on the wound if you can avoid it.
- Do not use a heating pad, hot water bottle, or very hot water on the foot.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s antibiotics.

## What to do now
1. **Take pressure off the area.** Sit down, keep weight off that foot as much as you can, and do not walk barefoot. If you must move, keep it brief and wear protective footwear that does not rub the wound.
2. **Check for emergency signs.** Call 911 or go to the ER now if you have fever, shaking chills, confusion, severe weakness, vomiting, rapidly spreading redness, red streaks up the foot or leg, a suddenly cold foot, or new white, blue, purple, or black skin on or near the wound.
3. **Protect the wound while you seek care.** If there is visible dirt, gently rinse with clean running water. Do not soak or scrub. Pat dry and cover with a clean, dry, non-stick sterile dressing if you have one.
4. **Ask for same-day assessment using clear words.** Call your diabetes care team, primary care clinician, or podiatrist and say: “I have diabetes and a worsening or infected-looking foot blister or wound. I need same-day advice or assessment.” If you cannot be seen or advised today, use urgent care or the ER, especially if the redness, swelling, drainage, odor, or pain is worsening.
5. **Make triage easier.** Take a photo of the wound, note when it started, whether redness is spreading, whether there is drainage or odor, your temperature if available, and recent glucose readings. Bring your current medication list, including insulin and other diabetes medicines.
6. **Keep diabetes care steady while you get help.** Check glucose more often, sip fluids if you can, and follow your sick-day plan if you have one. Keep taking prescribed diabetes medicines unless a clinician tells you otherwise. Tell the clinician, urgent care, or ER if glucose is repeatedly very high, you have ketones if you test for them, or you cannot keep fluids down.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is “just a blister,” an ulcer, or an infection before asking for help.
- You do not need to choose special wound-care products, shoes, inserts, or dressings right now.
- You do not need to research every possible complication before seeking same-day advice.
- You do not need to decide about antibiotics, imaging, or procedures yourself.

## Important reassurance
It makes sense to feel scared. With diabetes, reduced feeling or reduced blood flow can make a small-looking foot injury more serious, and getting it checked promptly is a practical way to reduce harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce pressure on the foot, protect the wound, and get you assessed. Later decisions, such as antibiotics, wound cleaning, imaging, circulation checks, offloading devices, or specialist referral, may need a clinician, podiatrist, wound-care team, or emergency department.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If you feel very unwell, the wound is rapidly worsening, or the foot looks white, blue, purple, or black, seek emergency care now.
