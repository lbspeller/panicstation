# What to do if… you receive an official notice of an inspection or visit with very little warning (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before you let anyone into nonpublic areas or hand over records. Verify the notice through an independent official contact route, then ask what legal authority and scope the agency is relying on.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, call numbers, pay money, or upload documents using contact details from the notice until you have checked the agency independently.
- Don’t consent to a wider inspection than the notice or legal authority describes.
- Don’t let an inspector walk unescorted through a workplace or nonpublic area.
- Don’t destroy, hide, edit, backdate, or create records to change what they show.
- Don’t argue, physically block anyone, or make detailed statements while panicked.
- Don’t guess about facts; it is safer to say you need to check and respond later.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the notice is real.**  
   Use an official government website or agency directory you found yourself, not the links or phone numbers in the notice. Ask the agency to confirm the case number, inspector name, scheduled time, location, and purpose.

2. **Ask for the authority and scope in writing.**  
   Ask:
   - “Is this a request for consent, or are you relying on a warrant, order, subpoena, license condition, or other compulsory process?”
   - “What areas do you want to inspect?”
   - “What records do you want, and for what time period?”
   - “Can you send the request and scope in writing before the visit?”

3. **Choose one person to handle the visit.**  
   For a workplace or business premises, choose an owner, manager, compliance lead, or attorney contact to greet the inspector, check credentials, receive documents, and keep notes. Tell other staff not to guess, volunteer extra records, or give informal interviews without checking first.

4. **Prepare a calm arrival script.**  
   If the inspector arrives, say:
   - “Please show your credentials.”
   - “Please give me a copy of the notice, warrant, order, subpoena, or written request you are relying on.”
   - “Please wait while I verify identity, scope, and who should speak for the site.”

5. **Separate public areas, nonpublic areas, and records.**  
   Public-facing areas may be different from offices, locked rooms, employee-only areas, private homes, devices, files, or sensitive records. Before allowing access to nonpublic areas or producing records, check whether the request is based on consent or a legal requirement.

6. **If the notice is from OSHA, use the inspection process calmly.**  
   OSHA compliance officers present credentials, explain the inspection scope, and usually conduct a walkaround of the covered parts of the workplace. Employers may require OSHA to obtain an inspection warrant before entering the worksite; if entry or scope is objected to, OSHA rules allow the officer to stop or limit the inspection and the agency may seek compulsory process.

7. **Control document handling.**  
   Gather only what is clearly requested and within scope. If the request says “all records” or feels too broad, ask for a narrowed written list and a response deadline. Keep a log of what was requested, what was provided, who received it, and when.

8. **Get legal help quickly if the stakes are high.**  
   Contact a qualified attorney before consenting or producing records if the notice mentions criminal enforcement, fraud, search or seizure, law enforcement, a private home, immigration, regulated licenses, confidential records, trade secrets, medical records, personnel files, or financial records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain everything immediately.
- You do not need to decide the whole legal strategy today.
- You do not need to volunteer extra records, extra locations, or extra interviews beyond the confirmed scope.
- You do not need to solve any citation, fine, license issue, or appeal question before you have verified the notice and the authority for the visit.

## Important reassurance
A short-warning official inspection can feel intimidating, but it is still reasonable to slow the moment down. Checking identity, asking for written authority, and keeping the visit within scope are normal control steps, not panic or obstruction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right response can depend on the agency, state, industry, license terms, type of premises, and whether the request involves consent, a warrant, an order, a subpoena, or another legal process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure what you must allow or produce, contact a qualified attorney or appropriate specialist promptly.
