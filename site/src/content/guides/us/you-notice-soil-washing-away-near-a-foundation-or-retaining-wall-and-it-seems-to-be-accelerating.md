# What to do if… you notice soil washing away near a foundation or retaining wall and it seems to be accelerating (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the area immediately to prevent falls or entrapment, redirect active water sources away from the washout, and contact a licensed Geotechnical or Structural Engineer for an emergency stability assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Don't stand or walk on the edge of the affected area, as undermined soil can collapse suddenly.
- Don't attempt to fill the hole with loose dirt, gravel, or concrete before an inspection, as this adds weight that can trigger a total collapse.
- Don't use a garden hose or pressure washer to "clean" the area or investigate the depth of the void.
- Don't park vehicles or place heavy equipment within 15 feet of the eroding zone.
- Don't enter a basement or crawlspace directly adjacent to an accelerating washout until a professional confirms the wall is stable.
- Don't ignore "muddy" water flowing from under a wall; this indicates active internal erosion (piping) of the structural backfill.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a safety perimeter around the site.** Block off the area with rope, cones, or tape at a distance of at least 1.5 times the height of the slope or wall. 
   - Ensure children and pets are kept indoors or away from the property section.
2. **Observe for signs of imminent structural failure from a safe distance.** Look for:
   - Sudden leaning, tilting, or bulging of the retaining wall.
   - New or rapidly widening diagonal cracks in the foundation or wall.
   - Water flowing out from the soil that is heavily loaded with silt or mud (turbid water).
   - "Stair-step" cracking in brickwork or masonry.
3. **Call 911 immediately if public infrastructure or life safety is at risk.** 
   - This includes situations where a wall may fall onto a public sidewalk, or if a sinkhole is forming under a utility meter or gas line.
4. **Identify and divert the water source driving the erosion.**
   - **Attach temporary flexible extensions** to downspouts to carry water 10+ feet away from the washout.
   - **Shut off the main irrigation valve** if the washout is near a sprinkler zone.
   - **Cover the eroding area with a waterproof tarp** if it is raining, weighting the edges of the tarp far back from the void to prevent water from getting underneath.
5. **Contact a licensed Geotechnical Engineer.** Request an "emergency geohazard assessment" or "foundation undermining inspection."
   - If the issue involves a structural wall, a Structural Engineer with geostructural experience is also appropriate.
6. **Verify utility safety before the ground moves further.**
   - Call **811 (Call Before You Dig)** even if you are not digging yet, to have lines marked so you know if gas or water mains are in the collapse zone.
7. **Document the progression without entering the zone.** 
   - Take photos from a distance and note the time.
   - Look at your insurance policy for "Earth Movement" or "Land Subsidence" exclusions before filing a formal claim, as many standard policies exclude erosion unless caused by a covered "sudden and accidental" plumbing burst.

## What can wait
- Determining the permanent engineering fix (such as soil nails, underpinning, or wall replacement).
- Negotiating with neighbors over shared wall costs or drainage responsibility.
- Cosmetic repairs like patching cracks or replacing sod.
- Hiring a landscaping contractor; wait for the engineer's report first to ensure the repair is structural, not just surface-level.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of urgency when soil moves, as foundations are the core of a home's stability. Identifying the water source and creating a safe distance are the most effective actions you can take in the first few minutes to prevent further escalation while waiting for professional help.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and hazard isolation only. It does not provide engineering specifications or repair methods, which must be determined by a licensed professional based on local soil conditions and building codes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the advice of a qualified engineer or emergency responder. Soil stability can change rapidly; if you hear loud cracking, popping, or see the ground moving, evacuate the area immediately.
