# What to do if… your flight is overbooked and staff say you may be denied boarding (USA)

## Short answer
Determine if the airline is asking for volunteers or bumping you involuntarily due to oversales, then secure written documentation of your rights and a confirmed rebooking or refund before leaving the gate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accept a travel voucher or "airline credit" until you have confirmed the cash compensation amount you are legally entitled to for involuntary bumping.
- Do not leave the boarding area until you have a printed or digital confirmation of your new itinerary or a formal refund receipt.
- Do not engage in verbal or physical confrontation with staff, as "disruptive behavior" is a legal basis for removal that voids your right to compensation.
- Do not assume you are entitled to compensation if the delay is caused by an "aircraft change" (switching to a smaller plane) or safety-related weight/balance issues.
- Do not agree to a "voluntary" offer until the agent confirms in writing whether your new seat is a confirmed reservation or a standby position.

## What to do now
1. **Verify your eligibility by keeping your original boarding pass and confirmation.**
   - Compensation rules only apply if you have a confirmed reservation and met the airline's specific check-in and gate arrival deadlines.

2. **Identify the specific type of boarding denial occurring.**
   - Ask: "Is this a request for volunteers, or am I being involuntarily denied boarding due to oversales?"
   - If you volunteer, the compensation is a private negotiation; if you are bumped involuntarily, federal law (14 CFR Part 250) dictates the minimum payment.

3. **Request the "Written Statement of Rights" immediately.**
   - Federal law requires airlines to provide a written document explaining how they decide who is bumped and what compensation you are owed.

4. **Confirm your Denied Boarding Compensation (DBC) amount if bumped involuntarily.**
   - For domestic flights, you are entitled to 200% of your one-way fare (up to $1,075) if delayed 1–2 hours, or 400% (up to $2,150) if delayed over 2 hours.
   - For international flights departing the U.S., you are entitled to 200% (up to $1,075) for a 1–4 hour delay, or 400% (up to $2,150) for delays over 4 hours.
   - Frequent flyer award tickets are compensated based on the lowest cash fare in the same class of service.

5. **Insist on payment at the airport.**
   - The airline must generally offer you a check or payment on the day you are bumped; if you are rebooked on a flight that departs before they can pay you, they must send payment within 24 hours.

6. **Refuse removal if you have already boarded the aircraft.**
   - Under DOT rules, once a passenger has had their boarding pass scanned and has boarded, the airline may not remove them to make room for another passenger unless it is for safety, security, or health reasons.

7. **Secure refunds for all ancillary fees paid for the original flight.**
   - Ask for confirmation that fees for seat selection, checked baggage, or Wi-Fi will be refunded if those services are not provided on the alternate flight.

8. **Obtain a confirmed alternate itinerary or an "involuntary refund" receipt.**
   - If you choose not to take the alternate flight offered, you are entitled to a full refund of the remaining value of your ticket plus the DBC payment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to calculate the exact fare-to-compensation ratio at the gate; the written statement allows you to verify the math later.
- You do not need to file a formal Department of Transportation (DOT) complaint while at the airport; this is a follow-up action if the airline refuses to pay.
- You do not need to settle additional claims for consequential damages (like missed hotel nights) immediately; these are handled through the airline's customer relations department post-travel.

## Important reassurance
Being bumped from a flight is a procedural event governed by federal regulations, not a personal failure. While the situation is disruptive, the Department of Transportation has established these rules specifically to ensure you are either compensated for your time or moved to your destination quickly.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and rights verification at a U.S. airport gate. It does not cover international flights arriving in the U.S. from foreign countries or long-term legal litigation for missed connections.

## Important note
The compensation limits mentioned ($1,075 and $2,150) are the minimums required by 14 CFR Part 250 as of 2025/2026. Airlines are permitted to offer higher amounts to attract volunteers. These rules apply to flights departing from a U.S. airport. Always ensure you receive any promises regarding hotels, meals, or future travel in writing from an authorized gate agent.
