# What to do if… you are safely parked after a minor collision (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure your vehicle is visible with hazard lights, exchange driver and insurance information, and document the damage and license plates before leaving the scene.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave the scene until you have successfully exchanged contact and insurance details.
- Don’t apologize or discuss fault, as these statements can be used against you in insurance determinations.
- Don’t agree to "off-the-books" cash settlements or digital payments while at the scene.
- Don’t stand between vehicles or in active traffic lanes to take photographs.
- Don’t allow the other driver to photograph your home address or social security number if possible.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your immediate environment and turn on hazard lights.** **Ensure your car is in park and the engine is off once you are in a safe spot.** If you are on a shoulder, stay inside the vehicle with your seatbelt fastened until you are certain it is safe to exit away from traffic.
2. **Conduct a physical check for yourself and any passengers.** **Look for signs of shock, dizziness, or confusion, which can mask injuries after impact.** If anyone reports pain or seems disoriented, call 911 immediately.
3. **Notify the correct authorities based on the situation.** **Call 911 if the road is blocked, a driver appears impaired, or someone is uncooperative or aggressive.** If the situation is calm and property damage is minor, call the local non-emergency police line to ask if an officer is required for a report.
4. **Exchange specific legal and insurance documents.** **Gather the following information from the other driver and provide your own:**
   - Full name and phone number
   - Driver’s license number and state of issue
   - Vehicle license plate number, year, make, and model
   - Insurance company name and policy number (digital or paper card)
   - Vehicle registration information
   - USDOT number (only if a commercial truck or bus is involved)
5. **Document the scene thoroughly with your phone camera.** **Take wide-angle photos of both vehicles' positions and close-up photos of all damage points.** Ensure you have a clear photo of the other vehicle’s license plate and any relevant street signs or signals nearby.
6. **Identify and note any witnesses before they depart.** **Ask anyone who saw the event for their name and a contact number.** You do not need a statement now; just a way to reach them later.
7. **Contact your insurance company to report the incident.** **Notify your provider even if you do not plan to file a claim today, as most policies require prompt reporting of any collision.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to determine who was at fault right now.
- You do **not** need to obtain repair estimates or visit a body shop today.
- You do **not** need to file a formal DMV crash report immediately at the scene; most states allow several days for this if required.
- You do **not** need to speak with the other driver's insurance company if they call you today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel shaky, lightheaded, or hyper-focused after a collision. These are physiological responses to adrenaline. If you have exchanged information and taken photos, you have successfully completed the most time-sensitive tasks required to protect yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for minor, non-injury collisions where the vehicles are already safely positioned. It does not cover emergency medical procedures or legal defense strategies for major accidents.

## Important note
Reporting requirements and damage thresholds for DMV reports vary significantly by state. Failure to file a required state report (often for damage over $500–$1,000) can result in license suspension. Verify your specific state’s deadline and threshold via an official DMV or Department of Public Safety website.
