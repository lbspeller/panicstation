# What to do if… your train, subway, or tram stops unexpectedly and you are not getting information (USA)

## Short answer
Stay on the vehicle and wait for crew, operator, or emergency-responder instructions unless there is immediate danger, such as fire, smoke, an active threat, or a serious medical emergency. If someone needs urgent help, use the onboard intercom or help system if you can, and call 911 if there is immediate danger to life or safety.

## Do not do these things
- Do not self-evacuate onto tracks, into tunnels, or along the right-of-way unless transit staff or emergency responders instruct you to.
- Do not force doors open or pry them; this can create injury, fall, electrification, or train-movement hazards.
- Do not pull an emergency brake, activate an alarm, use an emergency door release, or open an emergency window just to get delay information.
- Do not assume power is off because the vehicle has stopped; rails, overhead wires, or other equipment may still be energized.
- Do not move between cars, onto track areas, or into restricted areas unless staff direct you or there is immediate danger where you are.

## What to do now
1. **Do a quick safety scan.**  
   Look for smoke, fire, a serious medical emergency, an active threat, broken glass, or people trying to force doors. If none of these are present, treat this as a delay or disruption and stay inside the vehicle.

2. **Use the onboard help system for urgent safety or medical needs.**  
   Look near doors or marked panels for a two-way intercom, help point, passenger emergency communication system, or crew call button. Say what is happening, which car you are in if you know it, and whether anyone needs medical help.

3. **Call 911 for immediate danger.**  
   Call 911 if there is fire or smoke, a serious injury, someone cannot breathe, an active threat, or another immediate danger to life or safety. If the problem is unclear information rather than immediate danger, save battery and keep listening for official announcements.

4. **Collect location details without leaving the vehicle.**  
   Note the route or line, direction of travel, last station or stop passed, nearest visible station or landmark, and any car number posted inside the vehicle. Share these details if you contact the operator, crew, agency, or 911.

5. **Lower the risk inside the car.**  
   Move away from doors if people are crowding them. Sit down if you feel faint. Make room for children, older riders, disabled riders, and anyone who is overheating or unsteady.

6. **Check official information once you are stable.**  
   Use the transit agency’s official app, website, station displays, text alerts, or public-address announcements if your phone has service. Do not rely on rumors from other passengers as instructions to evacuate.

7. **If an evacuation is announced, follow it exactly.**  
   Wait for staff or emergency responders to say where to go. Assume tracks, third rails, overhead wires, and nearby equipment may be dangerous. Take only what staff tell you to take, keep one hand free where possible, move slowly, and tell staff if you or someone nearby needs assistance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your full reroute, refund, complaint, or onward travel plan right now.
- You do not need to keep refreshing social media for an explanation.
- You do not need to call everyone repeatedly. One short message, such as “stopped on train, safe for now, will update,” is enough if you can send it.

## Important reassurance
Unexpected stops with unclear information can happen for many reasons, including signals, congestion, mechanical checks, police activity, medical issues, or safety inspections. Limited information does not automatically mean the situation is dangerous; crews and control centers may need time to confirm the safest next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. Later decisions about rerouting, refunds, missed connections, complaints, accessibility arrangements, or incident reports may need help from the transit agency or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Transit procedures vary by agency and vehicle type. In immediate danger, follow crew or emergency-responder instructions and call 911 when it is safe to do so.
