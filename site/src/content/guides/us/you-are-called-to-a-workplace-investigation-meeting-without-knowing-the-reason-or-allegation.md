# What to do if… you are called to a workplace investigation meeting without knowing the reason or allegation (USA)
## Short answer
Ask in writing for a brief written summary of the concern and whether the meeting could lead to discipline. The employer may not provide a written allegation before the meeting, but asking creates a clear record. If you are union-represented, contact your union promptly, ask which representation rule applies, and clearly request representation before answering investigatory questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess what the allegation is or talk just to fill silence. Stick to what you know.
- Do not sign a statement or meeting summary on the spot if you have not read it carefully or it is not accurate.
- Do not delete, alter, or clean up messages, emails, time records, or files.
- Do not secretly record the meeting unless you have checked the law in your state and any workplace policy. Ask whether you may take notes or whether an agreed recording is permitted.
- Do not confront coworkers you think may be involved.

## What to do now
1. **Reply in writing and ask for the basics.** Ask:
   - what conduct or concern the meeting is about
   - the relevant timeframe
   - whether the meeting is investigatory and whether discipline could result
   - who will attend
   - whether you may bring a representative or support person
   - whether any documents will be discussed
   - whether a later meeting time is possible if you need time to review the concern or contact a representative

2. **If you are union-represented in a covered private-sector workplace, you may have Weingarten rights.**
   - Say: **“I am requesting a union representative before I answer questions.”**
   - These rights generally apply when the meeting is an investigatory interview and you reasonably believe it could lead to discipline.
   - Coverage can depend on whether the National Labor Relations Act applies to your workplace and role.
   - After a valid request, the employer may delay the interview until a representative is available, end the interview, or offer you the choice of continuing without representation or not having the interview.
   - Contact your union promptly and send them the meeting notice.

3. **If you are a federal employee in a bargaining unit, make the same clear request and contact your union.**
   - Federal-sector rules generally cover an examination by an agency representative connected with an investigation when you reasonably believe discipline may result and you request representation.

4. **If you work for a state or local public-sector employer and are union-represented, contact your union promptly.**
   - Representation rules can depend on state or local law and your collective bargaining agreement.
   - Ask your union which rule applies before answering investigatory questions.

5. **If you are not union-represented, do not assume you have a right to bring someone.**
   - Ask whether a support person is permitted under workplace policy.
   - Ask for the concern to be summarized in writing.
   - Take careful notes.

6. **Write a facts-only timeline.**
   - Note dates, times, people involved, what you were asked to do, what you did, and any witnesses.
   - Keep only records you are authorized to access and retain. Do not remove, forward, or keep confidential material without permission.

7. **At the meeting, ask them to define the concern before you answer detailed questions.**
   - Say: **“Before we begin, can you state what conduct is being investigated and the timeframe?”**
   - Ask for clarification if a question is vague.

8. **Answer carefully.**
   - Use simple phrases such as: **“I do not recall,” “I need to check my records,”** or **“Can you clarify the question?”**
   - Take notes.
   - Ask for a copy of anything you are asked to sign and ask for time to review it.

9. **Send a brief recap when you are able, preferably soon after the meeting.**
   - Record what you understood the concern to be, who attended, what you requested, and any important correction to the notes or summary.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to quit, threaten legal action, or make a formal external complaint.
- You do not need to produce a full written response before you understand the concern.
- Broader decisions can wait until you have a clearer record and a calmer view of the situation.

## Important reassurance
Being called into a workplace investigation meeting without details can feel alarming. Asking for the basics, slowing the pace, keeping a factual record, and requesting union representation where applicable are practical first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the earliest stage of a workplace investigation. Later decisions may need help from your union, an employment lawyer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace rules and legal protections can vary by employer, union status, sector, state, and local jurisdiction.
