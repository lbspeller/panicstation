# What to do if… your landlord or lender says your payment bounced and late fees are being added (USA)

## Short answer
Check with your bank first so you know whether the payment actually failed, then ask the landlord, lender, or servicer for a written ledger and the exact fee basis. If this is a mortgage-servicing posting or fee error, send a written notice of error to the mortgage servicer using its designated route if it has one.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use a phone number, payment link, or wiring instructions from a surprise email or text until you confirm through your usual portal, lease, monthly statement, or official website.
- Don’t pay by wire transfer, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or cash to “fix it quickly”; those payments can be hard or impossible to recover.
- Don’t pay a second time “just in case” until you check whether the first payment is pending, returned, reversed, or still able to post.
- Don’t rely on a verbal promise that a fee will be waived; ask for the ledger, fee decision, and any waiver in writing.
- Don’t ignore the next regular due date while disputing this payment.

## What to do now
1. **Check that the notice is real.**  
   Contact the landlord, lender, or servicer using a phone number or portal you already trust, such as your lease, monthly statement, existing account portal, or the official website. Do not use the link or number in the notice until you have verified it.

2. **Ask your bank what happened to the payment.**  
   Ask whether the payment was rejected, returned, reversed, still pending, or never sent. Ask for any available:
   - ACH trace number and return reason
   - card or portal authorization and reversal details
   - check return reason or returned-check image
   - money order tracking result or issuer record

3. **Save proof before replying.**  
   Save screenshots, receipts, bank entries, confirmation numbers, payment dates, amounts, payee details, and any returned-payment wording. Keep a short note of who you spoke with, when, and what they said.

4. **Ask the landlord, lender, or servicer for a written ledger and fee basis.**  
   Send one calm message asking for:
   - the current account ledger
   - what amount they show as unpaid
   - the date they say the payment became late
   - the late fee amount
   - the lease clause, loan term, or account rule they are relying on
   - a pause on additional late fees while the payment status is being checked

5. **If the bank confirms the payment truly failed, make one replacement payment to verified details.**  
   Use your usual portal, your usual payee details, or another trackable method the payee confirms through a trusted route. Add a clear memo such as “replacement rent payment” or “replacement mortgage payment” with the month and account number if appropriate, and save the confirmation.

6. **If this is a mortgage posting, application, or fee error, send a written notice of error.**  
   Use the servicer’s designated notice-of-error address if one is listed on your statement, coupon book, website, or written servicer notice. Include your name, loan number, payment date, payment amount, what you believe went wrong, the fees added, and what correction you are asking for. A mortgage servicer generally must acknowledge a proper written notice of error within 5 business days and usually respond within 30 business days for most payment-servicing errors, with some exceptions and possible extensions.

7. **Escalate only after you have your documents together or if fees keep growing.**  
   For a mortgage or loan, submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau if the company will not correct a clear servicing, posting, or fee problem. For rent, late-fee rules and grace periods vary by state and city, so contact a local tenant hotline, legal aid office, or housing agency if the landlord will not provide a ledger or the fee does not match your lease.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, refinance, sue, change banks, or change your whole payment system.
- You do not need a perfect case before you act; the first job is to verify the payment status, avoid paying the wrong person, avoid duplicate payment, and get the ledger in writing.
- You do not need to argue every issue at once; focus on this payment, this fee, and the next due date.

## Important reassurance
A bounced-payment notice does not automatically mean you did something wrong or that the fee is final. Clear bank records, a written ledger, and a calm written response give you the best chance to correct a posting problem and avoid paying twice.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation and reduce immediate harm. If you have received an eviction notice, foreclosure notice, lawsuit papers, or you cannot make the replacement payment, later decisions may need local legal, housing, mortgage, or financial help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, housing, or professional advice. Landlord-tenant rules vary by state and locality, and loan rights depend on the contract and the type of loan. Keep records, use verified contact routes, and use written dispute or complaint channels when the issue affects fees, credit reporting, eviction, or foreclosure risk.
