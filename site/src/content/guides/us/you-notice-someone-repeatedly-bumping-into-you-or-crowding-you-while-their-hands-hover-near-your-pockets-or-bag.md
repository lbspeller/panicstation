# What to do if… you notice someone repeatedly bumping into you or crowding you while their hands hover near your pockets or bag (USA)

## Short answer
Increase the physical distance between yourself and the individual immediately by moving toward a wall, staff member, or high-visibility area. Secure your belongings by bringing bags to the front of your body and keeping your hands over pocket openings.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pat your pockets or bag to "check" for your items while still in the crowd (this reveals exactly where your valuables are located).
- Do not stop in an isolated area, such as a stairwell or quiet side street, to inspect your belongings.
- Do not pull out your wallet or phone to count items while in the immediate vicinity of the suspicious activity.
- Do not engage in a verbal argument or attempt to physically restrain the person.
- Do not chase an individual if they move away quickly; prioritize your own safety and the security of your remaining items.
- Do not accept assistance from a stranger who offers to "help" you clean a stain or adjust your bag.

## What to do now
1. **Change your physical position to break the "crush" or contact.**
   - Step out of the flow of foot traffic immediately.
   - Move your back against a solid surface like a wall, pillar, or building facade.
   - If on public transit, move away from the doors and toward the center of the car or near the operator's booth.

2. **Secure all access points to your belongings without looking down.**
   - **Rotate any backpack or shoulder bag to the front of your chest.**
   - **Place your hand or arm firmly over the zippers or openings of your bag.**
   - **Keep your elbows tucked in close to your ribs to protect side pockets.**

3. **Move toward a "safe pause" location with active witnesses.**
   - **Identify the nearest uniformed staff, station agent, or security guard.**
   - **Enter a well-lit business or shop and stand near the service counter.**
   - **If you are with others, instruct them clearly: "Stay close to me, let's move into that store now."**

4. **Perform a discreet sensory check of your essentials.**
   - **Once in a secure spot, use your hands to feel for the presence of your phone, wallet, and keys.**
   - **Avoid laying items out on a table; keep them inside your bag or deep pockets during the check.**

5. **Contact emergency services if the threat is active or escalates.**
   - **Dial 911 if the person continues to follow you, threatens you, or if you witness a crime in progress.**
   - **For past thefts with no immediate danger, locate the local police department's 10-digit non-emergency number.**

6. **Lock all financial accounts if items are confirmed missing.**
   - **Use your mobile banking app to "freeze" or "lock" all credit and debit cards.**
   - **Check for and remove cards from digital wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) if your device was compromised.**
   - **Call your bank using the official number found on their website or your paper statements.**

7. **Initiate identity protection protocols if IDs were taken.**
   - **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create an official FTC Identity Theft Report.**
   - **Contact one of the three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a free one-year fraud alert.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the person's exact intent or gather evidence before moving to a safe area.
- You do not need to file a full police report in the first few minutes; securing your accounts and physical safety is the priority.
- You do not need to replace physical ID cards or licenses until you have completed the initial account freezes.
- You do not need to review security footage yourself; this is a task for law enforcement or transit security.

## Important reassurance
Physical crowding and "bumping" are documented tactics used to mask theft in high-traffic areas. It is a normal physiological response to feel startled or hyper-vigilant when your personal space is invaded. Taking immediate steps to move away and secure your items is a practical safety measure, regardless of whether a theft was actually completed.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected or attempted pickpocketing incident. It focuses on immediate physical safety and the prevention of financial fraud. Long-term recovery, such as replacing government documents or pursuing legal action, requires secondary steps not covered here.

## Important note
This information is for immediate situational stabilization. If you are in physical danger or the person is acting aggressively, contact 911 immediately. During federal government shutdowns, some services like IdentityTheft.gov may have limited functionality; if so, contact your local police department directly for guidance.
