# What to do if… police ask you to meet them to sign paperwork but will not explain what it is (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the request as potentially important. Before voluntarily meeting, answering questions, or signing anything, ask for details and speak with a lawyer. If you are already with officers, ask whether you are free to leave.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume an informal request means you are legally required to attend or sign.
- Do not sign a document you have not read and understood.
- Do not answer questions to “clear things up” before getting legal advice.
- Do not consent to a search or volunteer a phone passcode before getting legal advice.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, court order, or other formal document.
- Do not argue, run, or physically resist during an in-person encounter.
- If officers say they are acting under a warrant, do not physically resist.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the voluntary conversation.** Say:  
   “Before I agree to meet, answer questions, or sign anything, I need to understand the request and speak with a lawyer.”

2. **Ask whether this is voluntary or based on legal process.** Ask:  
   “Is this a voluntary request, or are you saying I am legally required to attend or sign something?”  
   “What is the exact name of the document?”  
   “Can you send me a copy for my lawyer to review?”  
   You can also ask how the officer describes the reason for the contact, but do not rely on that description before getting advice.

3. **Verify the contact independently.** End the call. Find the agency’s publicly listed number yourself, call it, and ask to be connected to the officer by name and badge number if one was provided.

4. **Contact a lawyer promptly.** Tell the lawyer that an officer asked you to meet and sign unexplained paperwork. Ask the lawyer to review any document and handle or arrange any voluntary meeting.

5. **If you are already with officers, ask whether you can leave.** Say:  
   “Am I being detained, or am I free to leave?”  
   If you are free to leave, leave calmly. If the answer is no or unclear, do not physically resist. Say:  
   “I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer.”  
   Then stop answering substantive questions.

6. **If they mention or show a subpoena, court order, warrant, or other formal document, ask for a copy if one is available.** Do not ignore a subpoena or court order. If officers are acting under a warrant, do not physically resist. Contact a lawyer promptly to check what the document requires and whether any deadline applies.

7. **Keep the basic record.** Save any voicemail, text message, email, or business card. Note the officer’s name, agency, badge number if provided, and the time of contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide your full account during an unexplained informal request.
- You do not need to decide whether to attend a voluntary meeting until you have details and legal advice.
- You do not need to voluntarily hand over documents, unlock a phone, or consent to a search to appear cooperative.
- You do not need to respond immediately because a voluntary meeting is described as “quick” or “routine”.
- Formal legal process should not be ignored, but you can ask a lawyer to help you understand the next step.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to slow down an unclear request. Asking whether a meeting is voluntary, requesting a copy of the document, verifying the caller, and getting legal advice are practical first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer, especially if you receive formal legal process, are detained, or are asked to answer substantive questions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or professional advice. Laws and procedures can vary by state and situation. Do not physically resist during an in-person encounter. Get legal advice about your specific circumstances.
