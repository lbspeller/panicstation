# What to do if… your bank account is marked dormant and you cannot make payments (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the bank through a trusted route and ask exactly what identity or activity step is needed to reactivate the account. Then protect any essential payments due today or in the next few days with another safe payment method while access is being restored.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “reactivate” links from unexpected texts or emails. Use the bank’s official app, the number on the back of your card, or the bank’s official website.
- Don’t keep retrying the same payment many times. Repeated attempts may create duplicate pending items, returned payments, or fees.
- Don’t pay anyone who offers to “restore access” to your bank account for a fee.
- Don’t share your Social Security number, ID photos, account number, or one-time passcodes unless you started the contact through an official bank channel and are using the bank’s secure process.
- Don’t assume “dormant” means your money is gone. Ask whether this is an inactivity control, a security or identity-verification hold, or a transfer to a state unclaimed property office.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the exact account status.** Ask the bank:
   - What status is on the account: dormant, inactive, restricted, closed, security hold, or sent to unclaimed property?
   - What triggered it: no customer-initiated activity, returned mail, missing verification, a security review, or something else?
   - What is blocked: debit card, ACH payments, bill pay, checks, wire transfers, online access, ATM withdrawals, or all transactions?

2. **Use a trusted contact route and ask for the fastest reactivation path.**
   - Call the number on the back of your debit card, use the official bank app, or go to the bank’s official website by typing the address yourself.
   - If the call came to you first, end it and call the bank back through a trusted number.
   - Ask whether reactivation can be done through secure online verification, by phone, or only in a branch.
   - If a branch visit is required, ask what ID and documents to bring and whether an appointment is needed.

3. **Ask when payments will work again.** Before ending the call, ask:
   - Whether the account will be usable immediately after verification or after a processing delay.
   - Whether you need to make a small customer-initiated transaction after reactivation.
   - Whether failed payments will retry automatically or need to be paid manually.
   - Whether any pending deposits, payroll payments, or benefit payments are affected.

4. **Stabilize essential bills first.** Make a short list of payments due now that could cause serious harm if missed, such as housing, utilities, insurance, childcare, phone or internet needed for work, or medication.
   - Contact each biller and say your bank account is temporarily restricted while the bank verifies or reactivates it.
   - Ask for a short extension, fee waiver, or pause on repeat autopay attempts.
   - Write down who you spoke to, the date, and any confirmation number.

5. **Use another safe payment method only for essentials.**
   - Pay urgent bills from another account you control, if you have one.
   - Use a trusted joint account only if you normally have permission to use it.
   - Use a credit card only if it is safe and affordable, and only for time-critical essentials.
   - Keep receipts so you can reconcile the account once access returns.

6. **Protect incoming money.**
   - If payroll, benefits, refunds, or other deposits are going into this account, contact the payer as soon as you can.
   - Ask whether the next deposit can be redirected, paused, reissued, or handled by an emergency process.
   - Do not close or abandon the dormant account until you understand what will happen to pending deposits.

7. **If the bank says funds were sent to the state, switch from “reactivate” to “claim.”**
   - Ask which state received the funds, the transfer date, and the amount transferred.
   - Search or claim through that state’s official unclaimed property office.
   - Be cautious with paid recovery services. State unclaimed property claims are usually designed so people can start the claim themselves through the official state process.

8. **Escalate only after you have the basic facts.**
   - Ask the bank for a case number or written explanation of what is needed to restore access.
   - If the bank is not responding or the explanation keeps changing, submit a complaint through the CFPB or the regulator for your bank or credit union.
   - Use the complaint route that matches your institution where possible. OCC handles complaints about national banks and federal savings associations; FDIC handles complaints involving FDIC-supervised banks; Federal Reserve Consumer Help can help identify or refer bank complaints if you are unsure; NCUA’s Consumer Assistance Center handles credit union disputes.

## What can wait
- Choosing a new main bank account.
- Deciding whether the bank acted fairly.
- Cleaning up old account habits, address updates, or reminders to keep the account active.
- Filing formal complaints if the bank is already giving you a clear same-day or next-step reactivation route.
- Reorganizing every autopay. First, protect the bills due now.

## Important reassurance
A dormant or inactive status is stressful because it can stop normal payments quickly, but it often means the bank needs a specific verification or activity step before the account can be used again. Your immediate job is to use trusted bank channels, get the exact reactivation step, and prevent urgent bills from snowballing while that happens.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment your account is blocked. Later decisions, such as changing banks, challenging fees, fixing billing fallout, or pursuing a formal complaint, may need help from the bank, the biller, a consumer agency, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Bank procedures vary by institution, account type, and state law. Use official contact channels, and if you are told your funds were transferred as unclaimed property, follow the official state claim process.
