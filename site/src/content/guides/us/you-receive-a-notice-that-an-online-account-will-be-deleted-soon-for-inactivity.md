# What to do if… you receive a notice that an online account will be deleted soon for inactivity (USA)
## Short answer
Do not use links, buttons, phone numbers, or attachments in the notice. Go directly to the service’s official website or app, sign in there, check whether the warning appears inside the account, and export anything important.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links or open attachments in the notice to “save your account.”
- Don’t call phone numbers or use “support chats” provided in the message.
- Don’t enter your password on a page opened from the notice.
- Don’t wait until the last day to export data, because exports and recovery can take time.
- Don’t make rapid security changes until you are sure you are on the real site or app.

## What to do now
1. **Treat the notice as untrusted until you verify it inside the real account.**  
   Open a new browser window or the official app. Type the service address yourself, use a bookmark you already trust, or open the app from your device, not from the message.

2. **Check whether the account is truly scheduled for deletion.**  
   After signing in, look in areas such as **Account**, **Security**, **Notifications**, **Account status**, **Data & privacy**, or **Subscription & billing**. If there is no matching warning inside the account, treat the message as suspicious.

3. **If you want to keep the account, use the account in a small reversible way.**  
   Provider rules vary. Signing in may be enough for some accounts, while others may count actions such as reading or sending an email, opening or saving a file, using the service, or confirming account details. Check the service’s own inactivity or account-status page if it shows one.

4. **Export or download important data now, even if you plan to keep the account.**  
   Look for wording such as **Download your data**, **Export**, **Transfer**, **Archive**, or **Backup**. Prioritise:
   - Photos and videos
   - Contacts and calendars
   - Messages or chats
   - Documents and notes
   - Purchase history, subscriptions, receipts, and licence keys

5. **Save the export in two separate places.**  
   Use two locations you control, such as a computer and an external drive, or two separate cloud accounts. Open a few exported files to confirm the download worked before you relax.

6. **Do a focused security check while you still have access.**
   - Confirm the recovery email and phone number are yours.
   - Turn on two-factor or multi-factor authentication if the service offers it.
   - Review recent sign-ins, connected devices, and connected apps.
   - Sign out of devices or sessions you do not recognise.
   - If you typed your password on a page reached from the notice, change it from the official site or app.

7. **If you cannot sign in, use only the provider’s official recovery path.**
   Use the official **Forgot password** or **Account recovery** flow from the real site or app. Take screenshots of the deletion deadline, account name, and any error messages. Avoid support links from the notice and avoid sponsored search results.

8. **If this is a work or school account, contact your admin or IT contact now.**  
   Work and school accounts may have organization-controlled retention, deletion, and recovery rules. An administrator may be the only person who can pause deletion, restore access, or confirm what data is retained.

9. **Report suspected phishing or cyber-enabled crime through official US routes.**
   - Report suspected scams or phishing to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If money was lost, an account was taken over, or you suspect cyber-enabled crime, consider reporting to the FBI’s IC3 at ic3.gov.
   - Mark the message as phishing or junk in your email provider.
   - If the account is tied to shopping, payment, or financial accounts, check recent orders, saved payment methods, delivery addresses, and security settings from the official sites or apps.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to leave the service forever.
- You don’t need a perfect backup system right now.
- You don’t need to clean up old files or organise the export during the deadline window.
- You don’t need to argue with support about policy before you have protected a copy of your data.
- You don’t need to change every account you own unless you reused the same password or see signs of account access you do not recognise.

## Important reassurance
A deletion notice can be real, and scammers can also copy this kind of warning to rush you. Slowing down, using the official site or app, and exporting your data first protects you without giving the message control over what you do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate data loss and account-compromise risk. Later decisions about formal disputes, complex recovery, business records, legal questions, or specialist security help may need provider support or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, cybersecurity, financial, professional IT, or other professional advice. Policies, timelines, exports, and recovery options vary by provider and account type, so rely on what you can confirm inside the official account and through official support channels.
