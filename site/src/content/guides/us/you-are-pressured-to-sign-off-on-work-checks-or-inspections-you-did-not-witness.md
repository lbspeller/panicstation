# What to do if… you are pressured to sign off on work, checks, or inspections you did not witness (USA)

## Short answer
Do not approve a record that would inaccurately say you performed, witnessed, or verified a check you did not witness. Ask what the sign-off certifies and request a truthful option, such as re-checking the item or recording that you reviewed records only, where the form allows.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign, initial, stamp, or click-approve a statement that you know is inaccurate.
- Do not backdate a record, change readings, swap names, or edit a log to create a misleading record.
- Do not let someone else use your login, credentials, stamp, or signature.
- Do not delete, destroy, or conceal records or messages about the request.
- Do not send accusatory messages or copy a large group into the dispute.
- Do not remove confidential originals or store workplace material somewhere you are not allowed to use.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before approving anything.** Say: “I cannot sign that I witnessed or verified something I did not see. I need to check what I can accurately certify.”
2. **Read the exact declaration.** Ask what your signature, initials, stamp, or click-approval would mean: performed, witnessed, verified, reviewed, received, or something else.
3. **Ask for the request in writing, where safe.** If the request was verbal, send a short factual note: “Confirming that I was asked to sign off the check dated [date and time]. I did not witness that check and need guidance on an accurate record.”
4. **Offer a truthful alternative.** Ask whether you can perform a new check within your role, who is authorised to complete the accurate record, whether the person who performed the original check can complete the appropriate entry, or whether an administrative review can be recorded without implying that you witnessed the work.
5. **Raise any immediate safety concern.** If the missing check affects equipment, vehicles, lifting gear, hazardous energy, chemicals, patient safety, or another urgent risk, tell the responsible manager or safety contact immediately and ask whether use should be paused until the condition is verified. OSHA describes a limited right to refuse dangerous work, but specific conditions apply. Check the official guidance promptly rather than assuming that the right applies automatically. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911 if emergency help is needed and use any site emergency procedure.
6. **Use an internal escalation route.** Contact the next manager, safety officer, quality or compliance contact, union representative, or ethics hotline. Keep the message neutral: “I am being asked to certify a check I did not witness. I need guidance on an accurate sign-off.”
7. **Make a dated timeline.** Note what you were asked to approve, who asked, when it happened, what you said, and what alternative you offered. Keep only copies of your own messages and documents you are permitted to retain.
8. **Get prompt advice if there is retaliation or an unresolved hazard.** Federal OSHA or an OSHA-approved State Plan may be the relevant route depending on your workplace and location. A retaliation complaint under Section 11(c) of the OSH Act generally must be filed within 30 days of the alleged retaliation. Other statutes can have different deadlines, so check the applicable route promptly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, sue, or make an accusation about someone’s motives.
- You do not need to prove fraud before refusing an inaccurate certification.
- A detailed review of every past record can wait unless you are assigned to do it.
- Do not delay prompt advice if there is an immediate hazard, retaliation, or a possible filing deadline.

## Important reassurance
Pausing before you sign is a reasonable response. You are keeping the record accurate and giving the responsible people a chance to correct the process.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your industry, your job duties, the type of inspection, your employer’s procedures, and whether specialist advice is needed.
