# What to do if… you receive urgent notice that water to your building will be shut off for emergency repairs (USA)

## Short answer
Confirm the exact shut-off window and immediately store a minimum of 1 gallon of water per person per day, then turn off high-draw appliances and your water heater to prevent mechanical damage.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait until the scheduled start time to fill containers, as crews often isolate lines earlier than stated.
- Don't run dishwashers, washing machines, or ice makers until you have verified the water is clear and the air is purged from the system.
- Don't leave faucets in the "on" position while waiting for service to return, as this can cause flooding if you are not present when pressure restores.
- Don't use hot water until the cold water has been flushed and runs clear; pulling sediment into the water heater can cause permanent damage.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the duration and scope of the interruption:** Check the notice for a contact number for the **utility provider or building management** to verify if the shut-off affects the entire building or just specific units.
   - Ask if the repair involves a "Boil Water Advisory" or if it is a simple mechanical isolation.
2. **Store a minimum of 3 days of water immediately:**
   - Use clean, food-grade containers to store **1 gallon per person per day** for drinking and basic food preparation.
   - Increase this amount for infants, pets, or individuals with medical conditions.
   - Fill a bathtub or large buckets with "grey water" specifically for **manual toilet flushing**.
3. **Isolate your water heater and sensitive appliances:**
   - Turn off the breaker for **electric water heaters** or turn **gas water heaters** to the "Pilot" setting to prevent the tank from dry-firing if it drains.
   - Shut off the valve to your **refrigerator ice maker** to prevent it from cycling while dry.
4. **Prepare for a "dry" hygiene routine:**
   - Place hand sanitizer and disinfecting wipes at every sink station.
   - Set aside a small basin and a jug of stored water for essential handwashing.
5. **Establish a communication plan with the landlord or manager:**
   - Send a written message (email or text) acknowledging the notice and asking for a projected "all clear" time.
   - If you have mobility issues or medical equipment requiring water, notify management now so they can provide bottled water if the outage is prolonged.
6. **Flush the system correctly when service returns:**
   - Run the **cold water tap in a bathtub** or a laundry sink (which usually lack aerators) for several minutes until the water is clear and the "banging" sound of air in the pipes stops.
   - Do not use any hot water or water-fed appliances until the cold water is perfectly clear.
7. **Check for local assistance if the outage exceeds 24 hours:**
   - Dial **211** to reach local community services that may provide emergency water stations or information on tenant rights in your specific jurisdiction.

## What can wait
- You don't need to resolve disputes over rent or habitability while the repair is active; focus on physical stabilization.
- You don't need to buy specialized water filtration systems; temporary storage in clean containers is sufficient for short-term repairs.
- You don't need to flush every sink and shower immediately; prioritize one high-flow fixture (like a tub) to clear the main lines first.

## Important reassurance
Temporary water shut-offs for repairs are a standard part of urban infrastructure maintenance. While the disruption is significant, taking these few isolation steps prevents the two most common complications: appliance damage and the consumption of dislodged sediment.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a localized, building-specific water interruption. It is not intended for regional drought management or long-term infrastructure failure.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or plumbing advice. Local health department regulations and tenant laws vary by state. Always follow the specific instructions provided by your municipal utility or local public health officials.
