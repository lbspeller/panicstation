# What to do if… your electric vehicle charge port will not close after charging (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start a normal trip until the charging cable is fully removed, the inlet area looks dry and undamaged, and there are no charging, high-voltage, or door warnings. If the charge door is loose, could flap, or the car shows a warning, use roadside assistance or towing instead of driving.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force the charge-port door shut.
- Do not put tools, metal objects, cloth, or fingers into the socket openings.
- Do not spray water, cleaner, lubricant, or compressed air into the inlet.
- Do not keep trying to charge if the inlet, latch, hinge, or connector area looks damaged or wet.
- Do not drive if the door could flap, detach, cover a light, cover a camera, cover a sensor, or distract you.
- Do not ignore dashboard alerts about charging, high voltage, moisture, the charge port, or a system fault.

## What to do now
1. Get to the safest pause you can. Stay out of traffic flow, park, set the parking brake, turn on hazard lights if you are near moving vehicles, and inspect only from a safe place.
2. Confirm charging has fully ended. Make sure the charging connector is completely removed. If the connector is stuck, stop pulling hard and use the vehicle manual, charging-station help button, automaker app, or roadside assistance.
3. Check the inlet area without inserting anything. Look for cracked plastic, a bent hinge, a broken latch, debris where the latch catches, moisture around the inlet, or a warning message on the dashboard or app. If you see moisture only around the outside edge, you can gently blot that outside edge with a dry cloth; do not push anything into the socket openings.
4. If your vehicle has an inner cap or inner cover, close it only if it moves normally. If it will not close, looks damaged, or traps moisture, do not force it.
5. Try one low-risk reset. Lock and unlock the car once, then try the charge door again. If your owner’s manual or vehicle screen gives a normal power-off and restart process, you can try that once.
6. Decide whether you need to move at all:
   - Do not drive if the inlet looks wet or damaged, the car shows a relevant warning, the connector is not fully removed, or any inner cap or cover will not close.
   - If the inlet looks dry and undamaged, there are no warnings, and you only need to leave a dangerous spot or clear a charging bay, move the minimum distance to a safer nearby place.
   - If the outer door is loose but the inlet is dry and undamaged, you may use a small amount of low-residue tape on clean exterior paint or plastic only for a short, low-speed move to a safer nearby place. Do not cover lights, cameras, sensors, license plates, or warning indicators.
7. If you are on a freeway shoulder, in a live lane, or anywhere you feel exposed, switch to roadside safety. Stay belted if leaving the car would put you closer to traffic. If you can safely get behind a barrier and away from traffic, do that. Call roadside assistance; call 911 if there is immediate danger, a crash, fire, injury, or you are stranded in a hazardous location.
8. Do not rely on other drivers seeing you. All states have Move Over laws for emergency vehicles with flashing lights, and some states also cover disabled vehicles with hazard lights, but coverage and driver behavior vary.
9. Get help from your automaker’s roadside assistance, a towing provider, or a qualified repair shop. Describe the problem as: “the EV charge port door will not latch after charging.”
10. If the problem seems recurring or safety-related, note the date, location, warning messages, photos of the door and inlet area, and any repair outcome. You can report a vehicle safety problem to NHTSA.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify whether the fault is the latch, actuator, hinge, sensor, or software right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a complaint today unless the car is repeatedly unsafe.
- You do not need to attempt lubrication, disassembly, cleaning inside the inlet, or a roadside repair.
- You do not need to continue the journey just because the problem looks cosmetic.

## Important reassurance
A stuck charge-port door is usually a repairable latch, hinge, actuator, or alignment problem. Slowing down is the right response because the immediate issue is avoiding electrical contact, loose parts, distraction, and roadside exposure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A lasting fix may need the owner’s manual, automaker support, roadside assistance, a dealer, a qualified repair shop, or a safety complaint route if the issue keeps happening.

## Important note
This is general information, not vehicle-specific mechanical, legal, emergency, or professional advice. Follow your owner’s manual, dashboard instructions, and emergency responders or roadside providers where they apply.
