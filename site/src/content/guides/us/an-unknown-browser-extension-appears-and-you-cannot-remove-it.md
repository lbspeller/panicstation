# What to do if… an unknown browser extension appears and you cannot remove it (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the affected browser on that device for sensitive logins. Treat activity in that browser as potentially exposed until you have checked whether the extension is managed legitimately, removed clearly unwanted software, and run a security scan.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t log in to email, banking, payroll, or medical portals from the affected device while you are checking it.
- Don’t follow pop-ups offering a cleanup download or a support phone number.
- Don’t install an extension remover or cleanup tool from an ad, redirect, or unfamiliar website.
- Don’t grant new permissions to the unknown extension.
- Don’t ignore messages such as “Managed by your organization” or “Installed by policy.” They are clues that need checking.
- Don’t remove applications you do not recognize unless you have checked what they are or asked for help.

## What to do now
1. **Pause sensitive activity on the affected device.** Close the browser. For an urgent login, use a different device you trust.
2. **Capture the details.** Take screenshots of the extension name, its permissions, any publisher information, and any message such as “Managed by your organization” or “Installed by policy.”
3. **Check whether management is expected.** If this is a work or school device, contact the IT or helpdesk team. If this is your personal device and the browser unexpectedly says it is managed, pause sensitive use and check the cause before continuing.
4. **Use the browser’s official recovery route.**
   - **Chrome:** Open Manage extensions and try Remove. If the issue continues, use Chrome’s Reset settings option. A Chrome settings reset is not a full reset and does not replace a device scan.
   - **Firefox:** Use Mozilla’s official steps for an add-on that cannot be removed normally.
   - **Edge:** Remove or disable the unknown extension if the control is available. Use Microsoft’s Edge troubleshooting steps, including a malware scan and repair if needed.
   - **Safari on Mac:** Open Safari, choose Settings or Preferences, choose Extensions, select the extension, and choose Uninstall.
5. **Review installed applications.** Look for applications installed or updated around the time the extension appeared. Uninstall only software you recognize as unwanted. If you are unsure, ask a trusted support person before removing it.
6. **Run the device’s built-in security scan.** On Windows, run a full scan in Windows Security. A Microsoft Defender Offline scan can be useful if you remain concerned about persistent malware. Save your work first because the offline scan restarts the computer. If BitLocker is enabled on the system drive, suspend BitLocker protection before running the offline scan, or ask a trusted support person for help.
7. **Secure exposed accounts from a different device you trust.** Change the passwords for sensitive accounts used in the affected browser, starting with email and any account showing suspicious activity. Turn on multi-factor authentication where it is offered. Check recent sign-ins where the service provides that option.
8. **Act promptly if money or account access is affected.** Contact the bank, card issuer, or service using its official app, website, or the number on the card. Report scams or fraud to the FTC. Suspected cyber-enabled crime, including account takeover or financial loss, can also be reported to IC3.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the extension’s exact origin before pausing sensitive use.
- You don’t need to decide about a factory reset as the first step.
- You don’t need to contact every service you use. Start with sensitive accounts used in the affected browser and any account showing suspicious activity.
- You don’t need to carry out advanced manual cleanup while you are overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
A blocked removal button does not by itself prove that someone has accessed your accounts. Pausing sensitive use, checking whether the browser is legitimately managed, reviewing installed applications, and running a security scan are practical first steps that buy time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the extension returns, the browser still says it is managed unexpectedly, or the device continues behaving strangely after the scan, hands-on help from your organization’s IT team or a trusted repair professional may be needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Use official websites and verified contact routes before entering personal information or installing software.
