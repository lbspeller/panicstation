# What to do if… you are told your passport number on a visa or permit does not match your current passport (USA)

## Short answer
Verify if the "mismatch" is simply a valid visa in an expired passport (which is legally acceptable if you carry both) or a clerical error in the record. If it is a confirmed error, contact a CBP Deferred Inspection site for I-94 issues or the issuing U.S. embassy for visa foil corrections.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not travel if a major biometric or biographical error has already been flagged by an airline.**
- **Do not attempt to peel, move, or alter the visa foil (stamp) from an old passport to a new one.**
- **Do not throw away the expired passport containing the valid visa foil.**
- **Do not pay private third-party "expeditors" to fix government immigration records.**
- **Do not use liquid paper or pens to "correct" printed passport numbers on any document.**

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which document or system contains the mismatch.**  
   **Ask the official or agent to show you if the error is on the physical visa foil (the sticker), your electronic I-94 admission record, or the airline's internal manifest.**  
   - If the error is on the visa foil, it is a Department of State issue.  
   - If the error is on the I-94 or entry record, it is a CBP issue.
2. **If your visa is valid but in an expired passport, carry both passports together.**  
   **The U.S. government permits travel with a valid visa in an expired passport as long as you also present a new, valid passport of the same nationality.**  
   - Ensure the expired passport is physically intact.  
   - Present both documents to the airline at check-in and to the officer at the port of entry.
3. **Contact the issuing U.S. embassy/consulate for visa foil (sticker) errors.**  
   **If the passport number printed on the visa foil itself is a typo or belongs to a document you never held, you must request a correction from the post that issued it.**  
   - Most embassies offer a "Visa Correction" web form for foils issued within the last 12 months.  
   - You typically do not need a new interview for a clerical correction.
4. **Use the CBP Traveler Communication Center (TCC) for electronic I-94 corrections.**  
   **For errors on your digital arrival record (I-94), submit an inquiry via the official CBP "Ask a Question" portal.**  
   - Select the topic "I-94 / Traveler Compliance" and the sub-topic "Issue with my I-94 / Travel History."  
   - Upload a clear scan of your passport biodata page and the visa foil.
5. **Contact a CBP Deferred Inspection Site for urgent or complex entry errors.**  
   **If you are already in the U.S. and the mismatch is affecting your legal status or work authorization, find the nearest Deferred Inspection Site on the official CBP list.**  
   - Many sites now accept correction requests via specific email addresses (e.g., Boston, Miami, Chicago).  
   - Provide your full name, DOB, correct passport number, and the date/port of your last entry.
6. **Coordinate with your sponsor if you are on an F, J, or H-1B visa.**  
   **Ask your Designated School Official (DSO) or Responsible Officer (RO) to verify that your SEVIS or petition record matches your current passport.**  
   - Sometimes the mismatch is triggered by an outdated passport number in the sponsoring agency's database.

## What can wait
- **You do not need to apply for a brand-new visa immediately.** Many foil typos can be corrected administratively without a full re-application.
- **You do not need to change your flight within the next hour.** If the error is caught at the airport, ask for a "supervisor review" first; they may have the authority to clear a minor typo if identity is verified.
- **You do not need to file a DHS TRIP (redress) application.** This is for persistent, repeated issues, not a one-time clerical correction of a specific record.

## Important reassurance
Passport number mismatches are common, especially following a renewal or when "Simplified Arrival" biometric systems link to an older travel document. These are generally regarded as clerical errors rather than fraud. Once the correct authority (CBP or State Department) is notified, they have standardized protocols to update the record.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for document mismatches. It does not cover situations involving name changes, dual-nationality conflicts, or visas that have been physically damaged or revoked.

## Important note
Admission to the United States is always at the discretion of the CBP officer at the port of entry. While carrying two passports is standard procedure for valid visas in expired books, any clerical error on a visa foil should be corrected before travel whenever possible to avoid "denied boarding" by airlines, who often have stricter automated checks than the border officers themselves.
