# What to do if… a station or stop closes suddenly and you need an accessible route to continue your journey (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safe place near the closure and ask transit staff or the agency’s official customer support for a confirmed accessible route or other accessible arrangement before travelling onward.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t take a detour that depends on stairs, a broken elevator, or an entrance you have not confirmed is open and accessible.
- Don’t board replacement service until you know that boarding, any transfer, and the exit point are accessible for you.
- Don’t let anyone separate you from essential mobility equipment or medical items to speed things up.
- Don’t assume a nearby station or stop is accessible at the entrance, platform, or time you need.
- Don’t leave a safe area based only on unconfirmed third-party directions.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the nearest safe public area.** Use a staffed booth, customer service desk, well-lit waiting area, or another safe place near the closure. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help, call 911.
2. **Contact the transit agency.** Start with staff or the vehicle operator. If no one is available, use the agency’s official service alerts, app, website, or customer support number.
3. **State what you need plainly.** Say: “I need an accessible route to continue my journey.” Ask:
   - “Which accessible route can I use right now?”
   - “Are the elevators, ramps, entrances, and transfer points on that route working?”
   - “Is there an accessible shuttle, replacement bus, or another accessible arrangement for this closure?”
4. **Name the feature that is unusable.** If an elevator, ramp, lift, entrance, or platform route is out of order, say so and ask what accessible alternative the agency can arrange.
5. **Confirm the exit before boarding.** If you are already on a vehicle that is being short-turned or unloaded early, tell the operator before you exit: “I need an accessible stop and an accessible path from the stop.”
6. **Ask about paratransit only as an available option.** If you are already eligible for ADA complementary paratransit, ask whether the agency has a same-day option for this disruption. Do not assume one is available. If you are not eligible or do not know, ask for an agency-arranged accessible route, shuttle, or other accessible arrangement.
7. **Ask for escalation if the first answer does not work.** Say: “That route is not accessible for me. Please connect me with a supervisor or accessibility contact who can help arrange a workable option.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about refunds, complaints, or detailed documentation while you are stranded.
- You do not need to work out longer-term paratransit eligibility or future travel plans right now.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation to work, family, or friends immediately. A brief safety update to a trusted person is enough if that would help.
- You do not need to choose the fastest route. Focus on a route you can actually complete safely.

## Important reassurance
A sudden closure can remove the accessible path you were relying on. Stopping to confirm each part of the replacement route is reasonable and can prevent you from being stranded somewhere harder to leave.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate disruption. Later decisions, such as a refund request, complaint, ongoing accommodation request, or paratransit eligibility question, may need help from the transit agency or a specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, transportation-planning, or other professional advice. Accessibility arrangements vary by transit agency and may change during a disruption. If a suggested route is unsafe or inaccessible for you, say so and ask for a different option.
