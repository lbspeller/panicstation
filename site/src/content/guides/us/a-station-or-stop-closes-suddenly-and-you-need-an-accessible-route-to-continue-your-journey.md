# What to do if… a station or stop closes suddenly and you need an accessible route to continue your journey (USA)

## Short answer
Find a secure, staffed, or well-lit location and contact the transit agency's customer service or dispatch immediately to request a "Reasonable Modification" for an accessible alternative.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt a detour that relies on stairs, steep grades, or unconfirmed "accessible" entrances.
- Don’t accept a transit plan that drops you at a location where you cannot exit the platform or station accessibly.
- Don’t allow staff or others to separate you from your mobility device, oxygen, or medical equipment to facilitate a "faster" transfer.
- Don’t assume the next bus or train will be accessible; verify the lift / ramp status with dispatch before it arrives.
- Don’t leave the station area until you have a confirmed, step-by-step accessible route or a promised shuttle.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the nearest safe, well-lit area such as a staffed booth, help point, or customer service desk.** Taking a moment to orient yourself helps ensure you do not miss official announcements or signage regarding the closure.
2. **Contact the transit agency’s dispatch or customer service line immediately using the fastest available method.** 
   - Use the "Help" intercom at the station if available.
   - Call the phone number listed on the agency's website, app, or posted service alerts.
3. **State clearly that you require a "Reasonable Modification" because your accessible route is blocked.** Using this specific phrase triggers ADA-mandated obligations for the agency to provide effective alternatives.
4. **Ask for an immediate accessible alternative if the next accessible vehicle is more than 30 minutes away.** Under FTA guidance, if a lift/ramp failure or station closure prevents your journey, the agency should provide alternative transportation (such as a shuttle or paratransit van) promptly.
5. **Verify the accessibility of every transfer point the agency suggests.** 
   - Ask: "Are the elevators and ramps currently operational at [Station Name]?"
   - Ask: "Is there a level path of travel from the vehicle to the street at the new drop-off point?"
6. **Tell the vehicle operator immediately if you are already on board and the route changes.** Inform them: "I can only exit at an accessible stop. Where is the nearest stop with a working lift / ramp / elevator where I can safely deboard?"
7. **Request an emergency paratransit trip even if you are not currently enrolled in a paratransit program.** Agencies have the discretion to use paratransit vehicles as an "interim" accessible service for any passenger stranded by a sudden facility failure.
8. **Escalate the request if the initial staff member cannot provide a solution.** Ask: "Who is the supervisor or ADA coordinator on duty who can authorize an accessible shuttle or alternate ride for me right now?"

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal ADA complaint or request a fare refund while you are still trying to complete your journey.
- You do not need to decide on long-term travel changes or paratransit applications until you are safely home.
- You do not need to explain the nature of your disability; you only need to state that the current route is inaccessible to you.
- You can wait to document the names of staff or vehicle numbers until you have reached a safe, accessible transfer point.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of urgency or panic when an essential accessible path is suddenly removed. Transit agencies are legally required to provide "interim" service when accessibility features fail, and insisting on a safe, usable route is a standard part of transit operations, not an imposition.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate transit disruptions in the USA. It focuses on securing an accessible continuation of your current journey and does not cover long-term legal remedies or permanent paratransit eligibility.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Individual transit agency policies and local infrastructure vary significantly. Always prioritize your physical safety; if a suggested detour appears hazardous or inaccessible, do not attempt it and request a different alternative from the agency.
