# What to do if… you notice someone waiting near your car and watching you approach (USA)

## Short answer
Stop approaching your car. Turn back to a staffed place, a checkout, a lobby, or a visible group of people, and ask for help leaving safely. Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep walking toward your car to “get it over with” or cut between parked cars.
- Don’t confront, argue, or move closer to film the person.
- Don’t unlock your car while someone is close enough to rush the door.
- Don’t get into your car if someone is standing close to the door, window, or path out.
- Don’t accept a stranger’s offer to help you check your vehicle or walk you to it.
- Don’t drive while too shaken to control the car safely.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance immediately.** Change direction and go back into the nearest staffed place, such as a store, lobby, café, security desk, reception area, or checkout. Aim for light, people, and visible cameras.
2. **Ask for an escort, not advice.** Tell staff clearly: “Someone is waiting by my car and watching me. Can security, a parking attendant, or a staff member walk with me or help me leave safely?”
3. **If you feel threatened right now, call 911.** Be ready to give your exact location, the lot or garage level, the nearest entrance, what you are seeing, a basic description of the person or vehicle, and where you are staying safe.
4. **Delay reaching your car until you have support.** Wait inside or near staff until the person leaves, an escort is with you, or security or law enforcement tells you what to do next.
5. **When you approach the car with support, pause before opening it.**
   - Look around the car and nearby vehicles from a short distance.
   - Look inside your car before opening the door.
   - Keep your key ready so you are not fumbling at the door.
   - If anyone moves closer, blocks your path, or something feels wrong, turn back immediately.
6. **If you get into the car, secure the door first.** Get in, lock the doors, start the engine, and leave only if you can do so safely.
   - If someone approaches the door or window, do not open the door or roll the window down.
   - If you are blocked in, stay locked inside. If it seems safe, use your horn or lights to draw attention and call 911.
7. **Once you are safe, report it in the way that fits the location.**
   - For a campus, hospital, workplace, mall, or managed lot, report it to security or public safety so they can check the area and any available cameras.
   - For a non-emergency police report, use your local police department’s non-emergency number or online reporting if available. If it becomes urgent at any point, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you are “overreacting” right now.
- You do not need to identify the person, investigate, or collect evidence.
- You do not need to post online or warn others publicly while you are still unsettled.
- You do not need to drive home immediately if you feel shaky; you can stay in a staffed place until you are steadier.

## Important reassurance
Backing away when something feels wrong is a sensible safety choice. You are allowed to choose distance and help over politeness, speed, or proving that your concern is justified.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes. Later decisions, such as formal reports, venue complaints, or camera requests, can wait until you are safe and may need location-specific or specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, mental health, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.
