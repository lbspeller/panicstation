# What to do if… you develop new chest pain that gets worse when you breathe in (USA)

## Short answer
New chest pain that increases with breathing (pleuritic pain) can indicate a serious condition like a pulmonary embolism or pneumothorax. If the pain is sudden, severe, or accompanied by shortness of breath or dizziness, **call 911 immediately**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "test" the pain by exercising, running, or climbing stairs.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are feeling lightheaded, faint, or short of breath.
- Do not take any anti-inflammatory or pain medication (like ibuprofen) until you have been evaluated by a clinician.
- Do not ignore the symptom because you can find a comfortable position; localized "comfort" does not rule out a hazard.
- Do not wait for several hours to see if the pain "settles down" on its own.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and sit in a comfortable, upright position.** Keeping the torso upright can make breathing easier and helps you stay alert while waiting for help.
2. **Call 911 immediately if you experience any of the following "red flag" symptoms:**
   - The pain is sudden, severe, or unexplained.
   - You are short of breath or breathing much faster than usual.
   - You feel dizzy, lightheaded, or like you might faint.
   - You notice swelling, redness, or pain in one of your legs (calf or thigh).
   - You are coughing up blood or pink-tinged mucus.
   - The pain radiates to your jaw, neck, back, or arms.
3. **Follow dispatcher instructions regarding aspirin.** If you are alert and the dispatcher or a medical professional advises it, you may be told to chew and swallow one adult-strength (325mg) or four low-dose (81mg) aspirin, provided you have no allergy and no history of bleeding disorders.
4. **Loosen any tight clothing around your neck, chest, or waist.** This reduces the effort required to breathe and can help you feel less restricted during a panic-likely moment.
5. **Identify your recent risk factors to tell emergency responders:**
   - Recent long-distance travel (flights or car rides over 4 hours).
   - Recent surgery, especially on the legs, hips, or pelvis.
   - Pregnancy or having given birth in the last 6 weeks.
   - Current treatment for cancer or use of hormone replacement / birth control.
   - Any history of blood clots (DVT or PE).
6. **Gather your identification and a list of medications if you can do so without overexerting yourself.** If you are alone and waiting for an ambulance, unlock your front door so responders can enter quickly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause of the pain (such as "pleurisy" vs. "clot") before seeking help.
- You do not need to check your own vitals using home gadgets or smartwatches; professional equipment is required for accuracy.
- You do not need to notify your primary care physician or employer until you have reached a medical facility.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of dread or sharp anxiety when your breathing is restricted by pain. This physical reaction is your body's way of alerting you to a problem. Emergency departments are equipped to quickly screen for the most serious causes of this specific type of pain.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or a formal medical evaluation.

## Important note
Chest pain that changes with respiration is a complex symptom that requires professional diagnostic tools like ECGs, blood tests (D-dimer), or imaging. Always prioritize the instructions of 911 dispatchers and on-site medical personnel.
