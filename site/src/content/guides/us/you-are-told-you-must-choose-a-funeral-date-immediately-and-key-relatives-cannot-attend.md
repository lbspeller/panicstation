# What to do if… you are told you must choose a funeral date immediately and key relatives cannot attend (USA)

## Short answer
You are not legally required to schedule a full funeral service immediately; you can separate the legal "disposition" (burial or cremation) from the "service" (memorial) to allow time for family to travel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign a full-service contract or pay a large deposit while feeling rushed or under pressure.
- Don’t accept claims that embalming is "required by law" without asking for the specific state statute (it is rarely required except for certain transport or public viewings).
- Don’t agree to a "package deal" before seeing the General Price List (GPL).
- Don’t feel obligated to hold the service before the burial or cremation occurs.
- Don’t let multiple people communicate with the funeral home; this creates conflicting instructions that providers may use to justify "needed" immediate decisions.

## What to do now
1. **Request a 24-hour communication pause for family consultation.**  
   **State clearly:** “We are not ready to set a service date. Please provide the next available dates for disposition only, and we will contact you tomorrow morning.”

2. **Obtain the General Price List (GPL) via email or in person.**  
   **Under the FTC Funeral Rule, you have the right to receive an itemized price list you can keep.** 
   - If you are on the phone, they must give you price information.
   - If you are in person, they must give you the GPL before you discuss any specific arrangements.

3. **Separate the legal disposition from the ceremonial service.**  
   **Ask for the price of "Direct Cremation" or "Immediate Burial."** 
   - These are specific, lower-cost options defined by federal law that do not include a formal viewing or service.
   - Selecting this now handles the time-sensitive physical needs while leaving the service date open for when relatives can attend.

4. **Verify the status of the "Disposition Permit" or "Burial-Transit Permit."**  
   **Ask the funeral director: "Has the death certificate been certified by the doctor/coroner, and has the county issued the disposition permit?"** 
   - The funeral home cannot proceed with burial or cremation until these legal steps are done.
   - If these are not yet complete, there is no physical way to "choose a date" that is guaranteed, giving you natural leverage to wait.

5. **Establish a single "Authorizing Agent" to handle the funeral home.**  
   **Formally designate one person as the point of contact and put this in writing (email) to the funeral home.** 
   - This prevents the funeral home from calling different relatives to push for a decision.
   - Ensure this person understands they can say "I need to check with the family and call you back."

6. **Inquire about refrigeration fees vs. embalming requirements.**  
   **If you need to delay the physical burial for more than 24-48 hours, ask for the daily refrigeration rate.** 
   - Most states allow refrigeration as an alternative to embalming. 
   - This buys you days or even weeks of time for travel without making irreversible chemical choices.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to choose a casket, urn, or vault today.
- You do **not** need to finalize the obituary or guest list.
- You do **not** need to decide on flowers, music, or speakers.
- You do **not** need to pay for anything other than the "Basic Services Fee" and "Transfer of Remains" to start the process.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of urgency is often driven by the funeral home's scheduling logistics, not by law. It is normal to feel overwhelmed when logistics collide with grief. Taking 24 hours to step back and wait for written prices is a protective, rational action, not a sign of indecision.

## Scope note
This guide addresses immediate pressure from funeral providers regarding scheduling and contracts. It does not cover specific religious requirements (e.g., Islamic or Jewish traditions requiring rapid burial) or complex estate litigation.

## Important note
The FTC Funeral Rule applies nationwide, but specific "Right of Disposition" laws (who has the legal power to decide) and permit timelines vary by state. If a provider claims a "legal requirement" for a specific date or service, ask them to provide the state regulation code in writing.
