# What to do if… you are pressured to agree to sexual acts you do not want as a “compromise” to avoid conflict (USA)

## Short answer
You are not required to "compromise" on sexual activity to manage a partner’s emotions or avoid an argument. Move to a separate space immediately and contact a confidential support line to help stabilize your safety.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to any sexual act simply to end persistent questioning, prevent an outburst, or stop "silent treatment."
- Do not attempt to negotiate or provide a "better" reason for saying no while you are feeling scared, overwhelmed, or trapped.
- Do not accept threats—such as ending the relationship or sharing private information—as a debt that must be paid with sexual compliance.
- Do not stay in a room where your exits are blocked or where your phone has been taken from you.
- Do not blame yourself for complying or "going along with it" as a way to stay safe; this is a common survival response known as fawning or freezing.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate physical pause in the interaction.** **State a clear, non-negotiable boundary and move to another room or outside if possible.**
   - "I am stopping now."
   - "I am going into the other room."
   - "I need you to step away from me."
2. **If you feel you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.** **Request emergency assistance if the person is becoming violent, preventing you from leaving, or if you fear for your life.**
3. **Connect with an outside person who is not involved in the situation.** **Call or text a trusted friend or family member and ask them to stay on the line with you until you are in a safe location.**
4. **Secure your essentials to maintain your independence.** **Ensure you have your phone, car keys, and identification on your person so you can leave the premises without delay if the pressure escalates.**
5. **Contact a confidential specialist service for guidance on coercion.** **Reach out to the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) or the National Domestic Violence Hotline for support that does not require you to involve the police.**
   - Call RAINN at 800-656-HOPE (4673) or text "HOPE" to 64673.
   - Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 800-799-SAFE (7233) or text "START" to 88788.
6. **Seek a medical evaluation if you desire care or evidence preservation.** **You can visit an emergency department and request a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) / Sexual Assault Forensic Examiner (SAFE) without being obligated to file a police report.**
7. **Preserve communication records if it is safe to do so.** **Keep copies of texts or emails involving threats or pressure, but only if the other person does not have access to your device and seeing these records won't cause you further distress right now.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship or "fix" the conflict today.
- You do not have to decide whether to report the coercion to the authorities or label it as a crime.
- You do not need to explain your actions or your "no" to the person who pressured you.
- You do not need to justify your reaction to friends or family members right now.

## Important reassurance
Choosing the path of least resistance to avoid conflict or harm is a survival strategy, not a sign of true consent. Feeling confused, numb, or "guilty" for saying yes under pressure is a normal reaction to an abnormal and coercive situation. You deserve to have your boundaries respected without fear of consequences.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps only. Long-term safety planning, legal options, and emotional recovery should be addressed with the assistance of the specialist advocates listed above.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for legal or medical advice. If you are experiencing thoughts of self-harm or intense emotional distress, you can call or text 988 to reach the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline 24/7.
