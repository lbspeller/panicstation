# What to do if… your work authorisation or security clearance is suddenly marked under review (USA)

## Short answer
Clarify your status as a witness, participate truthfully using first-hand observations, and maintain strict confidentiality regarding the interview details to avoid interference claims.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete or alter any evidence including emails, Slack messages, text threads, or personal notes related to the incident.
- Do not discuss the investigation or your potential testimony with coworkers or other potential witnesses.
- Do not speculate on motives, repeat office rumors, or guess at facts you did not personally see or hear.
- Do not record the interview surreptitiously; most private-sector policies and many state laws prohibit recording without all-party consent.
- Do not sign any summary or statement that contains inaccuracies or words you did not use.
- Do not refuse to participate without legal or union counsel, as "failure to cooperate" is often a fireable offense in at-will employment.

## What to do now
1. **Request written confirmation of your status in the investigation.** **Before the interview begins, ask the investigator to clarify if you are being interviewed strictly as a third-party witness or if your own conduct is under review.** 
   - Ask for the general scope of the inquiry (e.g., "behavior in the March 12 meeting") to help you prepare.
   - If they refuse to clarify your status, note this in your own records.
2. **Determine if you have a legal right to representation under Weingarten.** **If you are a union-represented employee and reasonably believe the interview could lead to your own discipline, you must clearly state your request for a union representative.**
   - In 2026, these rights generally do not extend to non-union private-sector employees.
   - If you are unionized, say: "If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, I respectfully request that my union representative be present."
3. **Review your own records for a factual timeline.** **Use only documents you already have legitimate access to—such as your own calendar or sent emails—to refresh your memory on dates and locations.**
   - Do not attempt to access restricted files or "dig" into company systems you do not normally use.
   - Distinguish between what you saw directly and what someone else told you.
4. **Adhere to the "First-Hand Facts Only" rule during questioning.** **Limit your answers to direct observations—what you saw, what you heard, and what you did.**
   - If you do not remember a detail, say "I do not recall" rather than guessing.
   - If a question is vague, ask for clarification (e.g., "Are you asking about the verbal exchange or the follow-up email?").
5. **Comply with lawful confidentiality instructions.** **Follow the investigator's instructions to keep the interview contents confidential if they cite a legitimate business reason, such as protecting evidence or preventing retaliation.**
   - Note that under the NLRA, employers cannot stop you from discussing your own wages, hours, or working conditions with coworkers, but they can generally mandate silence regarding an active, sensitive investigation.
6. **Request to review and correct the interview notes.** **At the conclusion of the meeting, ask to read the summary or notes taken by the investigator to ensure your statements were captured accurately.**
   - If you disagree with a phrasing, ask for a correction before the meeting ends.
   - If they provide a formal statement for signature, take the time to read every word; do not feel pressured to sign "on the spot."
7. **Document the interview for your own private files immediately afterward.** **Write down the names of everyone present, the start and end times, the main questions asked, and any specific documents you were shown.**
   - Keep this record on a personal device or in a physical notebook at home, not on a company computer.
   - If you later realize you made a factual error (like a wrong date), send a brief, written correction to the investigator.
8. **Monitor for and report any signs of retaliation.** **Keep a log of any adverse changes to your work environment—such as sudden schedule shifts, loss of duties, or hostile treatment—that occur after your participation.**
   - Federal law (EEOC and OSHA) prohibits retaliation against witnesses participating in harassment, discrimination, or safety investigations.
   - Note that OSHA retaliation complaints (Section 11(c)) often have a very strict 30-day filing window.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you need a lawyer immediately unless you are told you are the subject of the investigation.
- You do not need to "pick a side" or worry about how your testimony affects a coworker’s career; your obligation is to the facts.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your supervisor if they are not the ones conducting the interview.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard part of professional life to be called as a witness in workplace matters. Being interviewed does not mean you have done something wrong or that your job is in jeopardy. Most investigations are routine compliance measures designed to protect the organization and its employees.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for witnesses in US-based workplace investigations. It does not cover situations where you are the primary subject of an investigation, criminal matters, or specialized public-sector "Loudermill" hearings.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, union contracts, and corporate policies vary significantly by state and industry. If the investigation involves serious allegations like fraud, physical violence, or systemic discrimination, or if you fear imminent termination, consult with an employment attorney or union representative immediately.
