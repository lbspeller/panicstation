# What to do if… your work authorisation or security clearance is suddenly marked under review (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the exact notice or trigger in writing, then separate the issue into the right lane: I-9, E-Verify, immigration/work authorization, or security clearance/access. Do not access systems, sites, documents, or duties you have been told are paused.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, sign a separation paper, or give a written statement while you are panicked.
- Do not keep doing classified, sensitive, export-controlled, or restricted work if your access has been paused.
- Do not move work files, sensitive materials, screenshots, notices, or system messages to personal devices or personal accounts.
- Do not ignore an E-Verify mismatch or miss the response date shown on the notice.
- Do not give HR a bundle of extra personal documents; for I-9 or E-Verify, use the formal process and provide what is requested for that step.
- Do not post about the review online, in group chats, or in informal workplace channels.

## What to do now
1. **Ask which process is causing the review.**
   - Ask HR: “Is this an I-9 reverification, an E-Verify case result, an immigration-document question, or an internal audit?”
   - Ask security: “Is my clearance eligibility under review, or is this a local access, badge, system, or contract decision?”
   - Ask for the notice, case result, access instruction, or written summary before you respond in detail.

2. **If this is an E-Verify mismatch or tentative nonconfirmation, follow the notice.**
   - Ask for the Further Action Notice and read the date on it.
   - You generally must tell your employer whether you will take action to resolve the mismatch by the 10th federal government working day after E-Verify issued the mismatch.
   - While the mismatch process is still open, the employer generally should not terminate, suspend, delay training, withhold or lower pay, or take other adverse action because of the mismatch alone.
   - If you are sent home, locked out, threatened, or have hours cut, write down what happened and ask HR to confirm the reason in writing.

3. **If this is I-9 reverification, keep it narrow and documented.**
   - Ask HR whether the request is because a work-authorization document is expiring, because of rehire, or because of an internal audit.
   - You generally get to choose which acceptable document or documents to present from the I-9 Lists of Acceptable Documents; the employer should not demand one specific document if another acceptable option is available.
   - Keep a copy or photo of what you submitted, the date you submitted it, and the name of the person who received it.

4. **If this is a security clearance or access review, use the security channel first.**
   - Contact your Facility Security Officer if you are a cleared contractor, or your agency security officer or HR contact if you are a federal employee or applicant.
   - Ask whether your eligibility, clearance, badge, system access, site access, or specific duties are suspended, restricted, or only under review.
   - Ask what work you may do right now, what you must stop doing, and whether any forms, updated information, or interview scheduling are needed.
   - For DCSA-related matters, status information is usually routed through authorized security, HR, military, recruiter, or FSO contacts rather than directly to the individual.

5. **Protect your work status for the next few days.**
   - Ask HR or security for written instructions on whether you should report to work, work remotely, perform non-sensitive duties, wait for reassignment, or record leave.
   - If you are locked out of systems or buildings, ask how to handle timesheets, missed work, pay reporting, and urgent handoffs.
   - Do not try to bypass badge, account, network, document, or access controls.

6. **Make a simple paper trail.**
   - Save the notice, case result, emails, access messages, dates, names, and what you submitted.
   - Write a short factual timeline for yourself: what happened, who told you, what you asked, what you gave them, and what deadline is next.
   - Keep the timeline calm and factual; do not speculate about motives or fault.

7. **Get targeted help if the request goes beyond the basic process.**
   - If you have a union, employee representative, employee legal benefit, or trusted adviser, ask for process guidance before signing or submitting anything unusual.
   - If the issue involves immigration status, an expired document, a pending application, or a request for documents beyond the formal I-9 or E-Verify step, consider speaking with an immigration attorney before sending more information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, move, change employers, or explain the situation to coworkers.
- You do not need to write a long personal statement unless a formal process specifically asks for one.
- You do not need to solve every future clearance, immigration, or employment issue today; focus on the next notice, deadline, and written instruction.

## Important reassurance
“Under review” can mean a records mismatch, reverification, access pause, audit issue, or routine security follow-up. Staying inside the official process, meeting the next deadline, and avoiding workarounds can prevent a fixable issue from becoming harder to unwind.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilize the situation, avoid irreversible mistakes, and keep communications in the right channel. Later decisions, formal responses, appeals, immigration filings, clearance adjudication, or employment disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, employment, security-clearance, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Deadlines, rights, access rules, and work status can depend on the exact notice, employer, agency, contract, and role. If you are uncertain whether you may work or access sensitive material, pause that work and follow written HR or security instructions.
