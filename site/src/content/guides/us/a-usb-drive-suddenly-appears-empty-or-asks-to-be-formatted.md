# What to do if… a USB drive suddenly appears empty or asks to be formatted (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before making changes to the USB drive. Do not format, erase, initialize, repair, or copy new files onto it yet, because keeping the original unchanged can reduce the risk of making recovery harder.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click **Format**, **Initialize**, **Erase**, or **Repair** when the missing files matter.
- Do not create a new volume or copy new files onto the USB drive to test it.
- Do not run repair tools on the original drive before choosing a recovery approach.
- Do not keep reconnecting the drive if it repeatedly disconnects, becomes unusually hot, or appears physically damaged.
- Do not keep experimenting if the drive reports zero bytes.
- Do not connect an unknown or untrusted USB drive to additional computers just to test it.

## What to do now
1. **Disconnect the drive carefully.**  
   If the computer offers an eject option, use it. Unplug the USB drive and set it aside.

2. **Decide whether one connection check is reasonable.**  
   If this is your trusted personal USB drive and it is not unusually hot, physically damaged, or repeatedly disconnecting, connect it once using a different USB port without a hub. If an adapter or cable is involved, try a different known-good one.  
   - If it opens normally, copy the most important files to a separate safe location first.
   - If it still appears empty or unformatted, or reports zero bytes, stop testing it.

3. **On Windows, inspect without changing anything.**  
   Open **Disk Management** and locate the USB drive by its size. Note whether it appears as **RAW**, **Unallocated**, **Unknown**, **Not Initialized**, or with the expected capacity but no accessible files.  
   - Do not initialize it, format it, or create a new volume.

4. **On macOS, inspect without repairing or erasing.**  
   Open **Disk Utility**, choose **View > Show All Devices**, and select the USB device. Note its size and whether a volume appears beneath it.  
   - If the missing files matter and you do not have another copy, do not run **First Aid** yet. First Aid checks for and repairs errors, so choose a recovery approach before using it on the original drive.

5. **Keep the original drive unchanged while you choose the next step.**  
   If the files are important, consider a reputable data-recovery service. Ask how the provider will avoid unnecessary changes to the original drive. A recovery specialist may recommend working from a copy rather than the original.

6. **Stop reconnecting the drive when warning signs appear.**  
   If the drive repeatedly disconnects, becomes unusually hot, or has visible damage, unplug it and seek professional recovery help if the files matter. If it reports zero bytes, stop experimenting and consider professional recovery help.

7. **Use your organization’s reporting route when needed.**  
   If the USB drive contains workplace, customer, student, medical, or other confidential organizational data, follow your organization’s incident-reporting process or contact its IT or security team. Avoid testing the drive on additional organizational computers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether the USB drive is permanently broken.
- You do not need to run repair commands or recovery software today.
- You do not need to redesign your backup setup in this moment.
- You do not need to keep reconnecting the drive to look for a quick fix.

## Important reassurance
An empty or unformatted message does not by itself prove that the files are gone. Pausing before formatting, repairing, or writing new data gives you time to choose a safer next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from an experienced technical specialist or a reputable data-recovery service, especially if the files are important or the device may be physically failing.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional IT, legal, forensic, security, or data-recovery advice. Follow your organization’s requirements when a device contains confidential or regulated data.
