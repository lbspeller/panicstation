# What to do if… you realise you do not know where a person who died kept important documents (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the property and the mail immediately, then systematically check the home, contact their known professional advisors (attorney, tax preparer, or bank), and query the local Probate / Surrogate's Court for existing filings.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t discard or shred any mail, even junk mail, as it often contains account numbers, policy IDs, or clues to asset locations.
- Don’t attempt to guess passwords or use "remembered" logins to access accounts; this can trigger fraud locks and legal complications.
- Don’t distribute any property or files to family members for "safekeeping" until a legal representative is formally authorized.
- Don’t assume the will must be located in the home; it may be held by the drafting attorney or a national will registry.
- Don’t share photos of sensitive documents (Social Security cards, death certificates, or bank statements) via text or social media to coordinate help.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the physical residence and gather all visible document containers.**  
   Lock all doors, collect spare keys, and move folders, envelopes, or file boxes into one central, controlled room to prevent items from being misplaced during the initial search.

2. **Create a chronological document log to track all findings and attempts.**  
   Record every location searched, every document found, every phone call made to institutions, and any case numbers provided by court clerks.

3. **Order at least 10 certified copies of the death certificate through the state or local vital records office.**  
   Most financial institutions, government agencies, and courts will require a certified copy (not a photocopy) to discuss account details or release records.
   - You may use official third-party services like VitalChek for faster remote ordering if you cannot visit the office in person.

4. **Visit a local Post Office in person to manage or forward the deceased’s mail.**  
   To forward mail, the USPS requires an in-person request with a government-issued ID and a certified copy of your Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration (court documents proving you are the authorized executor or administrator).
   - A death certificate alone is generally insufficient to change a mailing address for a deceased person.

5. **Search the home specifically for business cards or letterhead from a law office or tax professional.**  
   Contact any law firms found to ask if they drafted a will, hold the original document, or have an estate plan on file.

6. **Contact the Probate Court or Surrogate's Court in the county where the person resided.**  
   Ask the clerk if a will has already been filed or if an estate case has been opened under the deceased person's name.

7. **Notify the Social Security Administration (SSA) of the death by phone or in person.**  
   Call 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) between 8:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. local time to ensure benefits are stopped and to prevent identity fraud.
   - Do not attempt to report a death to the SSA via email or online as these methods are not accepted.

8. **Inquire with banks about "supervised box opening" procedures if a safe deposit box key is found.**  
   Many states allow an interested party to petition the court for a limited order to open a safe deposit box solely to search for a will or burial instructions before the estate is formally opened.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify every single asset or debt in the first 48 hours.
- You do not need to settle disputes over sentimental items while the legal documents are still missing.
- You do not need to close digital accounts or social media profiles immediately unless they are linked to urgent financial obligations.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for documents to be disorganized or "missing" immediately following a death. Official records such as deeds, titles, and bank statements are almost always replaceable or discoverable through the mail and court records once you have been granted legal authority to act.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and document recovery. Estate laws, probate procedures, and safe deposit box access rules vary significantly by state; later steps will likely require consultation with a probate attorney or the court clerk.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you suspect foul play, unauthorized document removal, or find multiple conflicting wills, pause all actions and consult a qualified probate attorney in the relevant jurisdiction.
