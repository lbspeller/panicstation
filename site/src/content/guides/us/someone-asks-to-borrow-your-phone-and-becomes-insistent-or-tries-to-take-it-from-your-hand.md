# What to do if… someone asks to borrow your phone and becomes insistent or tries to take it from your hand (USA)

## Short answer
Maintain physical possession of your device, immediately increase the distance between yourself and the individual, and move toward a populated or staffed area. If the individual attempts to touch you or the device, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand your phone to a stranger even if they claim there is an emergency.
- Don’t unlock your phone or enter your passcode while a suspicious person can see the screen.
- Don’t let the individual stand between you and your exit path or a public space.
- Don’t follow or chase the person if they successfully take the device and flee.
- Don’t engage in an argument or offer long explanations for why you are saying no.
- Don’t allow yourself to be led to a secondary location, such as an alley, car, or quieter street.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the device immediately and adjust your stance.**  
   Put the phone in a secure, internal pocket or bag rather than holding it in your hand. Step back to create at least six feet of space and stand with your side toward the person to make yourself a smaller target.
2. **State a clear and final boundary.**  
   Use a firm, neutral voice to say: "No, I cannot help you." Repeat this exactly once if they persist, then immediately stop talking and begin moving.
3. **Move toward a safe refuge point.**  
   Walk directly into the nearest open business, government building, or lobby with visible security or staff.
   - If no buildings are open, move toward a group of other adults or a well-lit, busy intersection.
4. **Draw public attention to the situation if the pressure increases.**  
   If the person follows you or continues to demand the phone, speak loudly so others can hear: "I do not know you. Stop following me."
   - If you feel an immediate physical threat, point to a specific bystander and say: "You in the blue shirt, call 911. This person is trying to rob me."
5. **Contact 911 if the person makes physical contact or you feel unsafe.**  
   If you are unable to call yourself, ask a staff member at a business to call for you. State your location first, then describe the situation as an attempted robbery or harassment.
6. **Remotely lock the device if it is taken or compromised.**  
   If the device is no longer in your possession, use another person's phone or a computer to access your tracking service.
   - For Apple devices: Use iCloud.com/find to enable "Mark as Lost."
   - For Android devices: Use the Google Find Hub app or website to "Secure Device."
7. **Protect your primary digital accounts.**  
   Change the password to your primary email account first, as this is often used for password resets. Then update passwords for banking, payment apps, and social media.
8. **Contact your mobile carrier to suspend the service.**  
   Ask the carrier to disable the SIM / eSIM and add the device's IMEI number to the national blacklist to prevent it from being used on other networks.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal police report for a non-physical interaction.
- Restoring data from backups or purchasing a replacement device.
- Reviewing low-sensitivity accounts like streaming services or retail loyalty programs.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled, guilty, or anxious when someone uses social pressure or aggression to demand your property. These tactics are designed to make you hesitate. Choosing your physical safety and the security of your data over a stranger's request is a correct and necessary action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active or imminent personal safety threat involving a mobile device. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or legal proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. Always prioritize your physical safety over the recovery of personal property. If a crime is occurring or you are in danger, contact 911 immediately.
