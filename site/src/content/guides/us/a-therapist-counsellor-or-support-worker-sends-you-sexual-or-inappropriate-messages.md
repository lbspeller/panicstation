# What to do if… a therapist, counsellor, or support worker sends you sexual or inappropriate messages (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer pause and stop replying. You do not have to manage this directly with the person who sent the messages.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep replying to smooth things over, argue, or try to get an admission.
- Do not arrange a private meeting to discuss the messages while you feel unsafe or pressured.
- Do not confront them because you feel you owe them an explanation.
- Do not post the messages publicly while you are overwhelmed.
- Do not force yourself to decide whether to report anything right now.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance.**  
   Stop replying. If you may want the existing thread later and it feels safe to do so, save it once before putting it away. Mute or block the person if that feels safer. You can stop contact without sending a final message. If you prefer to send one clear line, keep it brief: “Do not contact me again.”

2. **Tell one safe person.**  
   Choose someone steady and supportive. You can ask them to stay with you in person or on the phone while you take the next step.

3. **Reach out for specialist support if that would help.**  
   If the messages felt sexual, coercive, or unsafe, RAINN may be able to offer support or referrals:
   - Call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**
   - Text **HOPE** to **64673**
   - Use the online chat through the RAINN hotline page

4. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger or need emergency medical help.**

5. **Use 988 if you are in an immediate emotional crisis or at risk of harming yourself.**  
   Call or text **988**, or use the 988 online chat.

6. **Ask for another clinician or support worker if you need ongoing support.**  
   If the person works for an organization, contact its manager, supervisor, or main contact. You can keep the request simple: “I need a different person and do not want direct contact with this person.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to contact the organization, a relevant state licensing board if the person is licensed, or law enforcement.
- You do not need to write a detailed account today.
- You do not need to confront the person or seek closure.
- You do not need to make long-term care decisions while you are overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, frozen, embarrassed, or unsure does not mean you did anything wrong. The person providing care or support is responsible for maintaining appropriate boundaries.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: creating distance, getting support, and preserving your options. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you are in an immediate emotional crisis or at risk of harming yourself, call or text 988 or use the 988 online chat.
