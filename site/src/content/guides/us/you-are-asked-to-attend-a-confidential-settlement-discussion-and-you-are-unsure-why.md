# What to do if… you are asked to attend a confidential settlement discussion and you are unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the request as a formal, competitive internal selection process and immediately request the job description, evaluation criteria, and a written explanation of the process before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or walk out in the moment; wait until you have the process details in writing.
- Do not sign any new documents, waivers, or severance agreements during the initial meeting or interview.
- Do not assume your past performance guarantees a pass; treat this as a first-time interview with a new employer.
- Do not refuse to participate or ignore the invitation, as this can be documented as a performance failure or job abandonment.
- Do not share sensitive company information or internal grievances on social media or with competitors.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written follow-up to the invitation to clarify the process.** If the request was verbal, email HR or the manager to confirm: "Per our conversation, I am confirming the interview for the [Role Name] role; please provide the formal job description and the specific criteria being used for evaluation."
2. **Request a list of the interviewers and the decision timeline.** Ask who will be on the panel and how many rounds are expected, as well as the deadline for the final selection decision.
3. **Download and save copies of your current job description and recent reviews.** Move these to a personal device or print them if permitted by company policy, as access to internal systems may change during a reorganization.
4. **Create a factual log of the events leading to this request.** Note dates, times, participants, and exactly what was said regarding your current status and the "new" role.
5. **Request reasonable accommodations for the interview if needed.** Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), you may request adjustments to the interview format (such as extra time or a specific communication method) if a medical condition or disability requires it.
6. **Ask specifically if this is part of a federally regulated layoff.** If your company has 100 or more employees, ask HR: "Is this process part of a mass layoff or plant closing that triggers federal WARN Act notice requirements?"
7. **Coordinate with colleagues using factual statements only.** While private-sector employees generally have the right to discuss working conditions under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), avoid using company-owned chat software for these discussions and stick to objective observations.
8. **Treat the interview as a "reset" of your professional record.** Prepare a one-page summary of your current contributions and metrics to present during the interview, regardless of how long you have worked with the interviewers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to stay with the company long-term until you see the new contract or offer.
- You do not need to hire an attorney immediately unless you are asked to sign a legal waiver or release of claims.
- You do not need to confront management about the fairness of the situation until the formal process is defined.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your external network or clients.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard reaction to feel startled or confused when asked to re-interview for duties you already perform. This process is often a structural requirement of a reorganization or merger and does not necessarily mean your previous work was poor. Focusing on the technical requirements of the "new" role is the most effective way to stabilize your position.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps to take within the first 48 hours of being asked to interview for an existing role. It is focused on harm reduction and process clarification, not long-term legal strategy or career coaching.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment in most of the United States is "at-will," meaning either party can end the relationship for any legal reason. If you believe you are being targeted due to race, age, gender, disability, or another protected characteristic, or if you are being asked to waive your rights, consult a qualified employment attorney.
