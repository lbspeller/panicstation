# What to do if… you are asked for a customs reference number and cannot find the confirmation (USA)

## Short answer
Do not guess the number or reply through an unverified message. Verify the request through an official carrier, broker, or retailer channel, ask which exact identifier is needed, and use a shipment detail you trust to ask for the record to be located or re-sent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click a payment, login, or document-upload link in an unexpected text or email.
- Do not send your passport, Social Security number, bank details, or payment-card details to an unverified contact.
- Do not invent a customs reference number or reuse one from another shipment.
- Do not assume that a tracking number, air waybill, invoice number, broker reference, and customs entry number are interchangeable.
- Do not delete the request, shipment emails, receipts, or portal messages while you are checking what happened.

## What to do now
1. Save the request and note who sent it, when it arrived, and the exact wording used for the missing number.

2. Verify the request independently. Open the carrier’s official website or app yourself, use the retailer account where you placed the order, or call a phone number from a trusted source. Do not use a link or phone number from the unexpected message.

3. Ask the verified contact to name the exact identifier they need and where it should appear. Ask whether they want an entry number, a reference shown on entry-summary documentation, a broker reference, a carrier reference, a tracking number, or an air waybill.

4. Search your email, deleted items, spam folder, retailer account, and verified carrier or broker portal for the sender name and these terms: `customs`, `entry`, `entry summary`, `7501`, `broker`, `clearance`, `invoice`, `tracking`, and `air waybill`.

5. If a broker or carrier handled clearance, contact that organisation through its verified official channel. Give the shipment identifier you already trust and ask whether they can identify the required reference or re-send the original request. Share only the minimum information they say is needed through that verified channel.

6. If the request claims to come directly from CBP, asks for urgent payment, or asks for sensitive information through an unexpected call, text, or email, stop responding through that route. Use the FTC resources below to check the message and report suspected fraud.

7. Keep a brief record of the verified contact, the date, the identifier they asked for, and any case or ticket number they provide.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to dispute a charge or make a complaint.
- You do not need to reconstruct every customs document at once.
- You do not need to send sensitive documents until you have verified who is asking and what is actually required.

## Important reassurance
A missing confirmation does not mean you need to guess, pay immediately, or send documents through an unfamiliar link. A verified carrier, broker, retailer, or official support channel may be able to clarify which reference is needed and how to recover it.

## Scope note
These are first steps for checking the request, recovering the correct identifier, and avoiding unnecessary disclosure. A complex import or compliance issue may need help from a licensed customs broker or official CBP guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, customs brokerage advice, financial advice, or professional advice. The correct identifier and recovery process can depend on the shipment, the filer, and the organisation asking for the information.
