# What to do if… you see someone attempting to enter a shared building behind residents without permission (USA)

## Short answer
Do not grant access; step back to allow the door to close and lock, then move to a secure location and notify building security or call 911 if you feel threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hold the door open or use your credentials to let the person in.
- Don’t enter confined spaces like elevators or stairwells with an unknown person.
- Don’t physically block the doorway or attempt to touch or detain the individual.
- Don’t engage in an argument or prolonged conversation with the person.
- Don’t follow the person into the building to see where they are going if they gain entry.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your physical safety by creating distance from the entry point.** Move away from the door and allow it to fully latch and lock. If you are already inside, move toward a well-lit, populated area like a lobby desk or toward a secondary locked door rather than heading to your private unit.
2. **If you are outside the building, wait for the person to leave before using your key.** Stand in a visible, public area or return to your vehicle until the entrance is clear and the person has moved away from the building perimeter.
3. **Use a single, neutral boundary statement if you must communicate.** From a safe distance, say "I cannot let you in; please use the intercom or contact the person you are visiting." Once said, stop speaking and move away.
4. **Identify the person to building management or security as soon as you are safe.** Contact the on-site concierge, security desk, or property manager immediately to report that an unauthorized entry attempt has occurred.
5. **Call 911 if the person is aggressive, uses force, or if you feel in immediate danger.** Provide the dispatcher with the building address, a description of the person, and their current location or last known direction of travel.
6. **Note physical descriptions of the person only if it is safe to do so.** Focus on height, build, clothing, and any distinguishing marks, but do not stop to take photos or videos if it requires staying near the individual.
7. **Report any equipment failures to the building office immediately.** If the door did not close properly, the latch failed, or the self-closing mechanism is broken, flag this as an urgent security maintenance request.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person actually lives in the building or is a guest.
- You do not need to prove the person is a trespasser.
- You do not need to confront the person to explain building policy.
- You do not need to decide on long-term security improvements or lobby upgrades right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of social pressure or awkwardness when refusing to hold a door. In a secure building, following access protocols is a standard safety measure, not an act of rudeness. Your priority is maintaining the security of the shared living space.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate encounter with unauthorized entry. It does not cover long-term legal actions for trespassing or internal building management policies.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you believe a crime is occurring or you are at risk, contact emergency services at 911.
