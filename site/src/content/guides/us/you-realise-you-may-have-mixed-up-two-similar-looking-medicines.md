# What to do if… you realise you may have mixed up two similar-looking medicines (USA)

## Short answer
Stop taking any further doses immediately and contact **Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222** or use the **webPOISONCONTROL** online tool for a specific risk assessment. If you are having trouble breathing, have collapsed, or are experiencing a seizure, call **911** now.

## Do not do these things
- Do **not** take the "correct" medicine to compensate or take an extra dose to "even it out."
- Do **not** take your next scheduled dose until you have received professional advice.
- Do **not** throw away any packaging, bottles, or loose tablets involved in the mix-up.
- Do **not** attempt to induce vomiting or use home remedies like charcoal unless specifically directed by a clinician.
- Do **not** eat or drink anything other than small sips of water until Poison Control advises otherwise.
- Do **not** drive yourself to the hospital if you feel dizzy, confused, or unusually drowsy.

## What to do now
1. **Immediately secure all involved medication containers and labels.** **Place the bottles or blister packs in a well-lit area to prepare for identification.**
2. **Identify the exact names and strengths of the medicines you believe were mixed.** **Check the "Active Ingredients" section of the labels and look for any distinctive markings (imprints) on the remaining tablets.**
   - Note the quantity missing from each container.
   - Note the exact time you took the dose.
   - If a pill organizer was used, keep it intact to show the sequence of doses.
3. **Check the person for emergency "red flag" symptoms.** **Call 911 immediately if you observe any of the following:**
   - Difficulty breathing or wheezing.
   - Loss of consciousness or inability to wake up.
   - Seizures or uncontrollable shaking.
   - Swelling of the face, lips, or tongue.
   - Severe confusion or hallucinations.
4. **Contact Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 for a free, confidential assessment.** **Provide the specialist with the medicine names, the suspected dose taken, and the person’s age and weight.**
   - If the person is asymptomatic and you prefer an online interface, use the **webPOISONCONTROL** tool.
   - Be prepared to list any other medications taken in the last 24 hours.
   - Mention any known kidney or liver conditions.
5. **Follow the instructions provided by the Poison Control specialist exactly.** **If they recommend a medical evaluation, bring all physical medication containers with you to the emergency department.**
6. **Stay in the company of another adult for at least two hours if possible.** **Avoid using alcohol or any other sedating substances while you monitor for new or worsening symptoms.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to determine if the error was "your fault" or how it happened right now. 
- You do **not** need to reorganize your entire medication cabinet while waiting for medical guidance.
- You can wait to report the error to the **FDA MedWatch** or **ISMP MERP** programs until after the immediate medical risk has been resolved.

## Important reassurance
Medication mix-ups are a frequent occurrence due to look-alike packaging and similar drug names. Taking the step to seek professional triage before symptoms appear is the most effective way to prevent a minor error from becoming a serious medical event.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for accidental medication confusion. It does not replace clinical judgment or specialized toxicology advice. Some medications (such as blood thinners, heart rhythm drugs, or insulin) require immediate professional intervention regardless of how you feel.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Always prioritize instructions from emergency services (911) or a certified Poison Control specialist.
