# What to do if… a process server tries to hand you legal papers at your home or workplace (USA)

## Short answer
Accept the papers if it is safe to do so, keep every page, and note the court name, case number, and any date, deadline, or hearing date shown. Verify the case through an official court website or clerk’s office. Missing a required response may have serious consequences.

## Do not do these things
- Do not throw the papers away or assume that refusing them ends the matter.
- Do not pay anyone at the door or in the lobby.
- Do not give bank details, card details, passwords, or your Social Security number to make the situation go away.
- Do not argue about the case or explain your side to the process server.
- Do not sign a separate acknowledgement, waiver, or other document at the door unless you understand it and choose to sign.
- Do not let embarrassment at work push you into quick promises or emotional calls.
- Do not post the papers online. They may contain personal information.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   At home, you can pause before opening the door fully. At work, move to a private area if one is available.

2. **Accept the papers if it is safe to do so.**  
   You do not need to debate the case. You can ask for the server’s name and company if they are willing to provide them. If they do not answer questions, continue by reviewing the papers.

3. **Keep every page together.**  
   Keep the envelope if there is one. Photograph the pages that show the court name, the parties, the case number, and any date, deadline, or hearing date.

4. **Write down what happened.**  
   Note the date, approximate time, location, and any name or company details the server provided.

5. **Identify the document type without trying to solve the case immediately.**  
   Look for headings such as **Summons**, **Complaint**, **Petition**, **Subpoena**, **Notice**, or **Notice of Hearing**. Different documents require different next steps.

6. **Verify the case through an independent official route.**  
   Use an official court website or call the clerk’s office using contact details from an official court website. For a federal case, start with the official PACER Find a Case page. The specific federal court is the best place to check the most current case information. Do not rely only on a phone number, email address, or payment instruction printed on an unofficial cover sheet.

7. **Treat any shown date as important.**  
   Put any response date or hearing date in your calendar. Contact a local attorney, legal aid organization, or bar association lawyer-referral service promptly. If you cannot reach legal help immediately, ask the court clerk where to find the court’s official instructions and keep trying to get local legal guidance.

8. **Keep workplace disclosure limited.**  
   You can keep the explanation to a manager or HR brief: you received legal papers and need a short private break to note dates and make a call.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to dispute, settle, negotiate, or make any payment.
- You do not need to call the other party immediately.
- You do not need to explain the details to coworkers, friends, or family right now.
- You do not need to understand every page before you verify the case and note any dates.

## Important reassurance
Receiving papers can feel alarming, but you do not have to solve the whole situation at the door or in the lobby. The useful first steps are to keep the papers, note any dates, verify the court information independently, and get local help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right next step depends on the document type, the court, the case, and the rules in your state. Later decisions may need help from a local attorney or legal aid organization.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws, procedures, and deadlines vary by state, court, and case type.
