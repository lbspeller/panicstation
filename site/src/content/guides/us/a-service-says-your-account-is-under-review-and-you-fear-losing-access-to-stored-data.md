# What to do if… a service says your account is under review and you fear losing access to stored data (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid repeated login attempts that trigger security lockouts, document all error messages or "under review" banners, and immediately use the official "Download Your Information" tool from a known, trusted device.

## Do not do these things
- Do not spam login attempts, password resets, or 2FA verification codes, as this often triggers permanent automated security bans.
- Do not delete the account, remove recovery emails, or change phone numbers in an attempt to "reset" the review process.
- Do not engage with third parties or social media "recovery experts" claiming they can unlock accounts for a fee; these are common scams targeting users in active crises.
- Do not wipe your primary devices or uninstall the service's app until you have verified if local cached copies of your data exist.
- Do not submit multiple conflicting appeal tickets; maintain a single, consistent case thread with the service.

## What to do now
1. **Minimize the risk of triggering further automated security blocks.**
   - Access the account only from one trusted device and a familiar home or work network.
   - If you are currently logged in on any device, stay logged in and do not use "log out of all sessions" features, as you may not be able to log back in while the review is pending.

2. **Capture a complete record of the current account status and restrictions.**
   - Screenshot the "account under review" banner, any mention of specific policy violations, timestamps, and any provided Case ID or reference number.
   - Save the full text of any automated emails received regarding the review, including the sender's address and the date/time received.

3. **Attempt a manual data export through the service's internal tools immediately.**
   - Search for settings labeled **"Download Your Information," "Export Data," "Takeout," or "Portability."**
   - If the system allows it, prioritize exporting the most critical data first (e.g., "Photos" or "Documents") rather than a full archive, which may time out or be blocked mid-process.
   - If the export is denied, screenshot the specific error message, as this is evidence of a potential denial of access rights.

4. **Submit a single, factual appeal or request for review.**
   - Use the official appeal link provided in the notice and state clearly that you require access to your stored personal data.
   - Include any reference numbers and a brief, neutral explanation if you believe the review was triggered by an error (e.g., a recent trip or a new device login).

5. **Identify and secure local copies of your data that may still be on your device.**
   - Check "synced" folders on your computer or the "Recently Deleted" or "Offline" folders in mobile apps.
   - Copy any found files to an external drive or a different, functioning cloud service immediately.

6. **Invoke state-specific privacy rights if you are a resident of California or other regulated states.**
   - If you reside in California, submit a "Right to Access/Know" request under the CCPA/CPRA, specifically requesting all data held since January 1, 2022.
   - Use the business’s designated privacy email or web form found in their "Privacy Policy" rather than the standard customer support chat.
   - Document the date and time you submitted this request; under California law, businesses generally have 45 days to respond to a verifiable request.

7. **Report "dark patterns" or deceptive recovery practices to federal authorities.**
   - If the service makes it intentionally difficult to export data or provides "broken" recovery links, file a report with the **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)**.
   - Mention if the service uses "dark patterns"—design choices that trick or frustrate you into giving up on your data access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you will continue using this service in the future.
- You do not need to worry about "cleaning up" or organizing the data; the only priority is moving it to a safe location.
- You do not need to seek legal representation immediately; official appeal channels and state privacy requests are the standard first steps.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic when a service restricts access to years of files or photos. Most "under review" statuses are automated security flags intended to protect you from unauthorized access; staying methodical and avoiding "panic-clicking" gives you the best chance of a successful resolution or data recovery.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital data access during an account review. It does not cover long-term data management, legal litigation against tech companies, or recovery from active identity theft.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Data access rights in the United States are currently a "patchwork" of state laws; your specific rights depend on your state of residence and the type of service provider. If your account contains sensitive financial or health data, different federal regulations (like GLBA or HIPAA) may apply to how that data must be handled.
