# What to do if… someone repeatedly asks for sexual “favours” in exchange for help, rides, or introductions (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance and minimize one-on-one contact with the individual. Contact a confidential sexual assault support service to discuss safety planning and understand your rights regarding "quid pro quo" harassment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to sexual requests to resolve the tension or "pay back" a perceived debt.
- Do not meet the person in private or enclosed spaces (like a car or home) to discuss the behavior.
- Do not feel obligated to explain your "no" or justify why you are refusing the requests.
- Do not delete digital evidence such as texts, emails, or voicemails, even if they are upsetting.
- Do not assume the behavior is harmless because it has not involved physical contact yet.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize physical distance.** End current interactions immediately and move to a public or populated area.
2. **Minimize one-on-one contact.** Avoid situations where you are alone with this person, including accepting rides or private meetings.
3. **Preserve a record of all requests.** Save screenshots of messages, log the dates and times of verbal requests, and keep any voicemails or emails.
   - Note any witnesses who may have been nearby during verbal exchanges.
4. **Identify alternative resources.** Locate other sources for the help, transportation, or introductions you need to reduce the person's leverage over you.
5. **Set a written boundary (if safe).** Use text or email to state that the sexual requests must stop and that you only wish to interact on a professional or platonic basis.
6. **Disclose to a trusted third party.** Tell a friend, family member, or colleague about the situation so someone else is aware of the behavior.
7. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline for confidential support.** Use these verified 2026 contact methods for RAINN:
   - **Call:** 800-656-HOPE (4673)
   - **Text:** "HOPE" to 64673
   - **Online Chat:** rainn.org
8. **Request "supportive measures" at school or university.** If this involves an educational institution, contact the Title IX Coordinator to request safety measures (like schedule changes or no-contact orders) without being required to file a formal complaint.
9. **Report workplace harassment to HR or a supervisor.** If the person has authority over your job, document the "quid pro quo" (this-for-that) nature of their requests.
10. **File a report for housing-related coercion.** If the requests are tied to your housing, contact the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) Sexual Harassment in Housing Initiative.
11. **Call 911 for immediate threats.** Contact emergency services if you are being followed, threatened with physical harm, or feel in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to have "perfect" evidence to seek support or supportive measures.
- You do not need to confront the person face-to-face about their behavior.
- You do not need to figure out the long-term impact on your career or education right now.

## Important reassurance
Responses like freezing, being overly polite, or laughing off a request are common survival mechanisms and do not mean you consented or encouraged the behavior. Needing help with a ride or a professional introduction is never an invitation for sexual pressure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. Long-term legal, professional, or academic resolutions should be handled with the guidance of a specialist or advocate.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Hotline services provide confidential support, but some professionals (like teachers or certain HR staff) may be "mandatory reporters" depending on your jurisdiction and age.
