# What to do if… you receive conflicting information about where a person who died is being held (USA)

## Short answer
Obtain a single case reference number and confirm which specific agency has physical custody (Hospital Decedent Affairs vs. County Medical Examiner/Coroner) before authorizing any transport or payment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call multiple departments simultaneously, as this can generate conflicting internal logs.
- Do not travel to a facility until a staff member confirms the person is physically present and identifies the specific room or unit.
- Do not pay deposits or sign funeral service contracts until you have verified the "release status" of the remains.
- Do not assume a "curtain-pull" identification is required; many offices now use digital photograph verification for safety and dignity.
- Do not rely on information from nursing staff or police officers once the remains have been moved; only Decedent Affairs or the Medical Examiner's office can confirm current custody.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a single written contact log.** **Record the full legal name, date of birth, and date of death of the deceased.**
   - Include a section for "Reference Numbers" (Police Report #, Medical Record #, or ME Case #).
   - For every call, log the time, the facility name, and the specific person’s name and extension.
2. **Identify the Legally Authorized Person (LAP) under state law.** **Verify who holds the legal Right of Disposition in your specific jurisdiction.**
   - The hierarchy typically follows: 1. Designated Agent (in writing), 2. Surviving Spouse, 3. Majority of Adult Children, 4. Parents, 5. Siblings.
   - Only this person can legally authorize the release of the body to a funeral home.
3. **Contact the Hospital Decedent Affairs office directly.** **If the death occurred in a medical facility, bypass the nursing unit and ask for the "Morgue Manager" or "Bereavement Coordinator."**
   - Ask: "Is the decedent physically in the hospital morgue or have they been transferred to the county?"
   - Ask for the "MRN" (Medical Record Number) if you do not have it.
4. **Call the County Medical Examiner or Coroner if the death was sudden or unattended.** **Provide the name or police incident number and ask for the "Investigations Desk."**
   - Ask: "Do you have custody of this person, or are they still at the hospital pending your pickup?"
   - Ask: "Has a Case Number been assigned?" (This number is the primary way to track the person between agencies).
5. **Confirm if there is a "Security Hold" or "Release Hold."** **Inquire if the body is being held for identification, autopsy, or a law enforcement investigation.**
   - If a hold exists, ask who the "Case Investigator" is and what their direct phone number is.
   - Ask: "Is the identification confirmed, or do you require a photograph verification from the next of kin?"
6. **Request the specific "Release Requirements."** **Ask exactly what paperwork is needed to move the remains to a funeral home.**
   - Standard requirements usually include a "Release of Remains" form signed by the next of kin.
   - For 2026 transfers, ensure you ask about the "Burial-Transit Permit" status.
7. **Cross-reference transfer times if locations conflict.** **If one facility says "they left" and the other says "not here," ask for the name of the transport company and the "Log Out" time.**
   - Bodies are often in transit via private contractors; there may be a 2–4 hour window where neither facility has eyes on the remains.
8. **Direct your chosen funeral home to perform a "Courtesy Check."** **Give the funeral director the case number and ask them to verify physical custody location before they dispatch a vehicle.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on burial versus cremation while custody is being confirmed.
- You do not need to finalize the obituary or service details.
- You do not need to pay "body storage" fees until the facility provides a written receipt of custody.
- You do not need to travel to the Medical Examiner's office for identification unless they specifically request an in-person appointment.

## Important reassurance
Inconsistency often occurs during the "transfer window" between a hospital and the county morgue. These two systems use different software and reference numbers. Once you have the Medical Examiner Case Number, the location usually becomes clear within one business hour as the log is updated.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate steps to locate remains and confirm custody in the USA. It does not cover the legalities of death certificates, probate, or funeral costs, which vary by state.

## Important note
This is for informational purposes and is not legal advice. Facility protocols vary by county. For deaths in law enforcement custody (jail/prison), 2026 regulations in states like California and Arizona may require an independent medical examiner oversight; ask the facility for the name of the independent oversight body if this applies.
