# What to do if… you receive bills or debt collection notices addressed to someone who has died (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay from your own money or agree that you are responsible. Put the bill aside for the estate’s personal representative, and notify the sender that the person has died so future contact is directed to the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay “to keep it from going to collections” unless you are sure you are authorized to pay from estate funds.
- Do not sign anything that says you are the responsible party, guarantor, or account holder unless you have confirmed that is true.
- Do not give your Social Security number, bank details, date of birth, or card information to an unfamiliar caller.
- Do not assume you are personally responsible because you are a spouse, child, relative, or emergency contact.
- Do not promise payment before checking whether you were a co-signer, joint account holder, surviving spouse with state-law responsibility, or legally responsible for estate administration.
- Do not argue about the debt in the first contact; keep the contact brief, factual, and in writing where possible.

## What to do now
1. **Make a simple bills log.** For each bill or notice, write down the sender, date received, amount, account or reference number, deadline shown, and whether it looks like a utility, rent, insurance, medical, credit, or collection notice.

2. **Keep the bill, envelope, and any messages together.** Do not throw them away, even if the bill looks wrong. Put them in one folder so the estate’s personal representative can see what arrived and when.

3. **Work out who the personal representative is, if anyone is already known.**
   - If there is a will, it may name an executor.
   - If there is no will, a probate court may appoint an administrator or personal representative.
   - A person named in a will may still need authority under the state probate process before acting for the estate.
   - If you are not the person handling the estate, pass the bills to that person and avoid acting in your own name.

4. **Tell the original sender that the person has died.** Use a calm, factual message: the person’s name, the date of death, the account number, and the name and contact details of the personal representative if known. Ask them to mark the account as deceased and direct future estate correspondence to the personal representative.

5. **If a debt collector contacts you and you are only a relative, friend, emergency contact, or helper, set a narrow boundary.** Say that you are not the person handling the estate and that you are not agreeing to pay. If you know who is handling the estate, give only that contact information; do not discuss the debt itself.

6. **If you are in an allowed contact category, ask for written debt information.** This may include a surviving spouse, parent of a minor who died, legal guardian, lawyer, confirmed successor in interest, executor, administrator, or personal representative. Ask for the collector’s name and mailing address, the creditor’s name, the amount claimed, and written validation information. Asking for information does not mean you are agreeing to pay from your own money.

7. **If you are the personal representative, keep handling estate-only.** Ask where formal estate correspondence should be sent, keep estate money separate from your own money, and do not pay bills until you understand what must be kept active short-term and what should wait for the estate process.

8. **Treat pressure as a warning sign.** Stop and verify before responding if someone demands same-day payment, asks for gift cards or wire transfers, refuses to send written information, threatens arrest, or asks for your personal financial information.

9. **Use a written stop-contact request if contact is overwhelming.** You can tell a debt collector in writing that you do not want further contact, and keep a copy. This does not erase the debt or stop a collector from contacting the estate or giving notice of a specific action they plan to take.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today which debts will be paid, disputed, delayed, or left unpaid.
- You do not need to negotiate with collectors in the first days after the death.
- You do not need to close every account immediately; first, stop accidental personal liability and get bills into the estate workflow.
- You do not need to know probate priorities today, because those rules vary by state and can be checked when the immediate pressure has passed.

## Important reassurance
Bills can arrive quickly because automated billing systems keep running after a death. Slowing down, refusing personal payment pressure, and routing bills to the estate is a normal and reasonable first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they help you avoid accidental personal responsibility, reduce pressure, and connect bills to the estate process. Later decisions about probate, debt priority, disputes, and state-specific responsibility may need help from a probate lawyer, consumer lawyer, legal aid office, or other qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. State laws and your specific facts matter. If you are unsure whether you are personally responsible because of a joint account, co-signed loan, spouse situation, estate role, or court appointment, avoid paying or signing until you have checked your position.
