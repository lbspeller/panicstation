# What to do if… you lose your visa, residence permit, or immigration status card while abroad (USA)

## Short answer
Find a secure location, report the loss to the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate, and apply for the specific travel documentation (emergency passport, boarding foil, or new visa) required to board your return flight.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to travel using a U.S. passport once it has been reported lost or stolen as it is immediately invalidated in international databases.
- Don't use third-party "visa expeditors" or agents claiming they can replace a green card or visa from outside the U.S.; only U.S. embassies and consulates can issue these documents.
- Don't assume a digital photo or photocopy of your document is sufficient for boarding a commercial carrier.
- Don't delay reporting the loss to the U.S. government, as this leaves your identity vulnerable to misuse.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure location and conduct a final search.**  
   Check all bags, safe boxes, and recent locations. If you suspect theft, immediately change passwords for any banking or email accounts accessible via your mobile device if it was also taken.
2. **Compile a digital folder of your remaining identification and status proof.**  
   Gather any of the following if available:
   - Passport biographic page photo
   - Visa foil or Green Card photo (front and back)
   - I-94 arrival record (retrievable from the CBP website or CBP One app)
   - USCIS receipt notices or approval letters
   - Current flight itinerary
3. **If a U.S. citizen: Report the lost passport and apply for a replacement at the nearest U.S. embassy.**  
   **You must appear in person to submit Form DS-11 (Passport Application) and Form DS-64 (Statement Regarding a Lost or Stolen Passport).**
   - If you have urgent travel within 24–48 hours, request an "emergency limited-validity passport."
   - For after-hours life-or-death emergencies, contact the embassy's duty officer via their main phone line.
4. **If a Green Card holder: File Form I-131A for carrier documentation.**  
   **You must pay the $575 filing fee online through the USCIS website before attending an embassy appointment.**
   - This process is for Lawful Permanent Residents (LPRs) who have been outside the U.S. for less than one year (or two years with a Reentry Permit).
   - Bring your payment receipt, original passport, and a passport-style photo to the consulate.
5. **If a Nonimmigrant Visa holder: Email the embassy that issued the original visa.**  
   **Notify the specific Consular Section that issued your visa that the document is lost or stolen.**
   - Provide your full name, date of birth, and passport number.
   - You generally cannot "replace" a visa; you must apply for a new one, pay the fees, and attend a new interview.
6. **Retrieve your I-94 record if you are a foreign visitor.**  
   **Access the official CBP I-94 website or the CBP One mobile app to print your most recent entry record.**
   - If your record is "Not Found," contact a CBP Deferred Inspection Office for assistance.
7. **Obtain a local police report.**  
   **Visit a local police station to report the theft or loss and request a physical copy of the report.**
   - While not always mandatory for the embassy, this is often required for insurance claims and may be requested by consular officers or airlines.
8. **Contact your airline carrier directly.**  
   **Notify the airline of your situation before arriving at the airport.**
   - Ask specifically what documentation they require to allow you to board (e.g., a Boarding Foil, Transportation Letter, or Emergency Passport).

## What can wait
- You do not need to file Form I-90 to replace a physical Green Card while abroad; this form can only be filed once you are physically present in the United States.
- You do not need to replace secondary ID cards (state driver's licenses, work badges) until you return home, unless they are your only remaining form of photo ID.
- You do not need to finalize insurance claims or police investigations today.

## Important reassurance
U.S. embassies and consulates deal with lost travel documents daily and have established protocols for verifying your identity and status. While the process involves fees and in-person appointments, there is a clear legal pathway to secure the documentation needed to return to the United States.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individuals currently outside the U.S. who lack necessary entry documentation. It does not cover long-term immigration litigation or complex status abandonment issues.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Entry into the U.S. is always at the discretion of Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officers at the port of entry. Always verify current fees and appointment procedures on the official website of the specific U.S. embassy or consulate in the country where you are located.
