# What to do if… you lose access to a passwordless login because the passkey device is gone (USA)

## Short answer
Lock the missing device remotely and contact your mobile carrier to blacklist the line, then use the service’s official account recovery flow from a trusted computer or still-signed-in device.

## Do not do these things
- Do not follow login links from unsolicited texts or emails claiming your device was found; these are often phishing attempts targeting people who recently lost devices.
- Do not erase or wipe a device that is currently your only signed-in "lifeline" to an account, as this may permanently remove your last recovery route.
- Do not share one-time codes, backup codes, or screenshots of security settings with anyone claiming to offer "support" or "recovery services."
- Do not repeatedly attempt to log in after seeing a rate-limit or lockout message, as this can extend the security freeze on your account.
- Do not create a temporary new account using the same email address in an attempt to contact support, as this can create identity conflicts during the recovery process.

## What to do now
1. **Remotely lock or secure the missing device immediately using another computer or tablet.** 
   - **For Apple devices:** Sign in to iCloud.com/find or use the Find My app on another Apple device to **Mark as Lost**.
   - **For Android devices:** Go to google.com/android/find and select **Secure Device** to lock it and sign out of your Google account.
   - **For Windows devices:** Log in to your Microsoft account and use the **Find my device** feature to lock the hardware.

2. **Contact your mobile carrier to suspend service and blacklist the device's IMEI.** 
   - Ask the representative to "suspend the line" to prevent unauthorized calls or SMS-based recovery attempts.
   - Request that they "blacklist the IMEI number" to prevent the device from being activated on any US cellular network.

3. **Identify and protect your "lifeline" sessions on other hardware.** 
   - Check if you are still signed in to the affected accounts on a home computer, laptop, or tablet.
   - If you have an active session, navigate to **Security / Account Settings / Passkeys** and:
      - Revoke the passkey associated with the missing device.
      - Add a new passkey using the device you are currently on.
      - Generate and save a new set of "Backup Codes" or "Recovery Codes" immediately.

4. **Initiate the official account recovery process from a trusted browser.** 
   - Use a private or incognito window on a computer you have used successfully before.
   - Navigate directly to the provider’s official sign-in page and select **"Forgot password?"**, **"Try another way"**, or **"Account recovery"**.
   - Follow the prompts to use alternative verification methods such as a recovery email, a trusted phone number, or a pre-configured recovery contact.

5. **Utilize recovery contacts or recovery keys if previously configured.** 
   - **Apple users:** If you set up an **Account Recovery Contact**, contact that person and ask them to generate a recovery code for you.
   - **Google users:** If you have **Backup Codes** printed or stored elsewhere, enter one of these when prompted for your passkey.

6. **File a local police report if you believe the device was stolen.** 
   - Obtain a report number, as this is often required by insurance providers and some financial institutions to verify identity theft claims.
   - Provide the police with the device's serial number or IMEI if available.

7. **Report significant financial loss or identity theft to federal authorities.** 
   - If your personal data was misused to open accounts, visit **IdentityTheft.gov** to create a formal FTC recovery plan.
   - If the device loss resulted in significant financial fraud or a major cybercrime, file a report at **IC3.gov** (the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center).

## What can wait
- You do not need to replace all your digital hardware or "reset" your entire digital life today.
- You do not need to recover access to low-priority accounts (streaming, social media) until your primary email and financial accounts are secured.
- You do not need to decide on a new security strategy or "ecosystem switch" while you are in the immediate recovery phase.

## Important reassurance
Losing a passkey device is a known risk in modern security, and most major providers (Apple, Google, Microsoft) have built-in recovery paths specifically for this scenario. While the process may take time—sometimes involving a mandatory security wait period of several days—following official channels is the most reliable way to regain access. You are not at fault for a device being lost or stolen, and recovery is a standard procedure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm prevention for users who have lost their primary passkey device. It does not cover technical troubleshooting for syncing issues or long-term security architecture.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional technical advice. Account recovery protocols are subject to change by service providers. If you suspect your financial accounts are at risk, contact your bank using the official number on the back of your card or their verified website immediately.
