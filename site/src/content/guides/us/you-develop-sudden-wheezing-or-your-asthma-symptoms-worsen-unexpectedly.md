# What to do if… you develop sudden wheezing or your asthma symptoms worsen unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Sit upright and use your **rescue (quick-relief) inhaler immediately** as directed by your asthma action plan. If symptoms do not improve within minutes or if you have any emergency signs, **call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie flat on your back — sit upright to keep your airway open.
- Do not wait to see if the medicine works if you cannot speak in full sentences or your lips are turning blue.
- Do not exceed the dosage on your prescription label or action plan without emergency medical supervision.
- Do not leave the person having an attack alone.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are struggling to breathe — call 911.
- Do not use a "maintenance" or "controller" inhaler (usually a disc or non-aerosol) for immediate relief unless it is specifically part of a SMART / MART therapy plan.

## What to do now
1. **Sit upright immediately and try to remain steady.**  
   Avoid lying down; leaning forward slightly with your hands on your knees (tripod position) may make breathing easier. Loosen any restrictive clothing around your neck or chest.

2. **Use your rescue inhaler right away according to your asthma action plan.**  
   If you do not have a plan, follow the instructions on the prescription label, which typically involve 2 to 4 puffs every 20 minutes for up to an hour.  
   - Shake the inhaler well before use.  
   - Use a spacer if you have one available to ensure the medicine reaches your lungs.  
   - If you suspect an allergic reaction (hives, swelling, or known exposure) is causing the attack, use an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) first if prescribed.

3. **Call 911 immediately if you show any emergency "Red Zone" signs.**  
   Do not wait for your inhaler to work if you experience:  
   - Difficulty walking or talking due to shortness of breath.  
   - Lips, skin, or fingernails looking blue, gray, or pale.  
   - Skin sucking in around your ribs or neck when you breathe (retractions).  
   - Extreme gasping or nostrils flaring.  
   - Confusion, extreme drowsiness, or fainting.

4. **Monitor your response to the rescue medication over the next 15 minutes.**  
   If your breathing does not become significantly easier or if your peak flow remains below 50 percent of your personal best, seek emergency care.

5. **Contact your primary clinician or asthma specialist today once breathing is stabilized.**  
   Even if the rescue inhaler worked, an unexpected worsening means your maintenance treatment may need to be adjusted to prevent a more severe relapse.

6. **Identify and move away from the immediate trigger if possible.**  
   If you are near smoke, strong fumes, cold air, or animals, move to a different environment with cleaner, temperate air while you recover.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause of the flare-up right this second.
- You do not need to worry about scheduling follow-up tests or long-term medication changes until your breathing is fully stabilized.
- Paperwork and insurance details can be handled after you are under the care of a medical professional.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic when your breathing changes suddenly. This physical reaction is common and expected. By sitting up and using your rescue medicine now, you are taking the most effective first step toward regaining control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute asthma flare-up. It is not a replacement for a clinical diagnosis or a long-term management strategy.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not professional medical advice. Asthma can be life-threatening. If you are in doubt about the severity of your symptoms, always default to seeking emergency medical care by calling 911.
