# What to do if… you notice water seeping through a basement wall (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as an electrical-safety problem first: stay off wet flooring and away from outlets, cords, appliances, and the electrical panel near the wet area. Only shut off power if you can do it from a dry, safe location.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t step into a wet basement area if water may be touching outlets, cords, appliances, extension cords, the electrical panel, or plugged-in equipment.
- Don’t touch the electrical panel or flip breakers if you would have to stand on a wet or damp surface to reach it.
- Don’t use fans, a dehumidifier, a pump, or a shop vacuum in or near the wet area until power is safe and any wet electrical equipment has been checked if needed.
- Don’t tear into drywall, insulation, paneling, or the foundation wall while water is actively seeping.
- Don’t ignore foul odors, gurgling drains, toilet backups, or sewage-like water.
- Don’t delay urgent safety steps just to take photos.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Keep children and pets away. Stay on dry flooring. Move yourself away from the wet wall, wet cords, outlets, appliances, and anything plugged in nearby.

2. **Handle power only from a dry place.** If you can turn off the affected circuit or main power without standing on a wet or damp surface, do that. If you would have to cross the wet area, do not touch the panel; call an electrician. From a dry place, you can also call your electric utility for emergency guidance.

3. **Check for an inside water source without entering the wet area.** Look from a safe spot for a leaking water heater, laundry hose, supply pipe, utility sink, condensate line, or appliance. If it looks like plumbing and you can safely reach the shutoff, close the nearest shutoff or the main water valve. If you cannot reach it safely, call an emergency plumber.

4. **Check the outside only if it is safe.** Look for overflowing gutters, a downspout dumping water against the foundation, water pooling against the wall, or soil sloping toward the house. Do not climb, dig, or work near electrical equipment during a storm.

5. **Limit spread without using powered equipment.** Move electronics, cardboard boxes, textiles, and stored items up off the floor and away from the wet wall if you can do that without stepping into the wet area. Put towels or absorbent pads only where you can place them from dry flooring.

6. **Document what you can safely see.** Take photos or video of the seepage point, damp area, cracks or stains, nearby outlets or appliances, and the floor path of the water. Note the time and recent rain or storm conditions.

7. **Contact the right help before it becomes standing water.**
   - Contact your insurer, insurance agent, landlord, property manager, or condo association as applicable, and ask what emergency mitigation they want documented.
   - Call a plumber if you suspect an internal pipe, appliance, water heater, or drain problem.
   - Call your local sewer or wastewater utility, or an emergency plumber experienced with sewer backups, if you notice sewage-like water, toilet backup, gurgling drains, or strong foul odors.

8. **If you have a sump pump, be cautious.** Only check it if you can reach it from a dry, safe area and you are confident the outlet and cords are not wet. If not, leave it alone and tell the electrician, plumber, insurer, or mitigation contractor that you have one.

9. **Step back and escalate if conditions worsen.** Leave the area if seepage becomes pooling, you hear buzzing or see sparks, you smell gas, the wall or floor cracks, bulges, or shifts, or you feel unsafe. If there is an immediate threat to life, call 911 from a safe place. If you smell gas, leave first and call emergency services or the gas utility from outside.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether you need foundation repair, waterproofing, drain tile, grading work, or a new sump system.
- You do not need to start demolition while water is still actively entering.
- You do not need to run drying equipment until the water source is controlled and electrical safety is clear.
- You do not need to decide the exact cause right now; it is enough to separate obvious plumbing signs from rain, groundwater, drainage, or sewer warning signs.

## Important reassurance
Catching seepage before it pools gives you more room to act safely. The immediate goal is to avoid shock, sewage exposure, slipping, and unnecessary damage while you get the right help involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about plumbing repair, drainage, drying, mold cleanup, insurance, or foundation work may need help from qualified specialists.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-prevention. It is not legal, medical, financial, insurance, engineering, electrical, plumbing, or other professional advice.
