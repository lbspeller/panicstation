# What to do if… you receive a notice that you must attend a meeting about a complaint involving your child and you are shocked (USA)

## Short answer
Identify which agency is calling the meeting (CPS, school, or police), request a written summary of the allegations, and obtain legal advice before signing documents or giving a detailed statement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice; missing an initial meeting can be interpreted as a lack of cooperation or an admission of risk.
- Do not contact the person you believe made the report to argue or confront them.
- Do not "prep" your child by giving them specific answers or asking them to hide information.
- Do not post about the notice, the allegations, or the agency on social media.
- Do not sign a "safety plan" or any "voluntary" placement agreement in the moment without a lawyer reviewing the long-term custodial implications.
- Do not assume a school meeting cannot lead to a CPS or police report; treat all formal meetings with the same caution.

## What to do now
1. **Take a 10-minute pause to ground yourself before responding.**
   **Shock often triggers a "defend and explain" response that leads to oversharing.** 
2. **Verify the identity of the person and agency sending the notice.**
   **Look up the official agency phone number independently (do not use only the number on the letter) to confirm the sender's role.**
   - Determine if the agency is Child Protective Services (CPS/DCF/DSS), local law enforcement, or the school district.
   - Ask for the specific name and supervisor of the person calling the meeting.
3. **Request the specific "meeting type" and a written summary of concerns.**
   **Ask if this is a "Family Team Meeting," an "Investigative Interview," or a "Disciplinary Hearing."**
   - If the agency refuses to provide written allegations, take notes on exactly what they say over the phone.
4. **Clarify if the meeting or your participation is court-ordered.**
   **Ask: "Is there a court order or subpoena requiring my attendance today?"**
   - If there is no court order, the meeting is likely "voluntary," though refusing may lead the agency to seek a court order. 
5. **Invoke your right to have an attorney or support person present.**
   **Inform the caseworker or administrator that you will be bringing an advocate or legal counsel to the meeting.**
   - If they state you cannot have a lawyer, ask for the specific written policy that excludes counsel.
6. **Protect your child's rights regarding interviews.**
   **State clearly that you do not consent to your child being interviewed without you or your attorney present.**
   - Be aware that in some states, CPS may interview children at school without prior parental notice; verify your state's specific 2026 statutes on "exigent circumstances" for child interviews.
7. **Address school-specific notification rights.**
   **If the meeting involves school-based gender identity or medical changes, reference current federal and state requirements for parental notification.**
   - Under 2026 legal standards (including Mirabelli v. Bonta rulings), parents generally have a right to be informed of significant changes to their child's records or presentation at school.
8. **Create a factual log of all communications.**
   **Start a document listing every call, letter, and visit, including the date, time, and name of the official.**
   - Keep copies of any evidence that contradicts the complaint (e.g., medical records, attendance logs, or emails).
9. **Prepare a "deferral" script for the meeting.**
   **If you are asked a question you are not ready for, use a neutral phrase to buy time.**
   - Example: "I want to be accurate, so I need to check my records and consult with counsel before answering that."

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to agree to long-term "services" or "counseling."
- You do not need to explain your entire family history or parenting philosophy in the first phone call.
- You do not need to notify your employer or your child's extracurricular coaches right now.
- You do not need to "prove your innocence" immediately; focus first on understanding the specific legal process.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an official notice regarding your child is a high-stress event, and feeling shocked or defensive is a standard physiological response. A notice is a starting point for an assessment or investigation; it is not a final judgment. Taking a methodical, rights-focused approach is the most effective way to protect your family.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the 24–48 hours after receiving an unexpected notice. It does not cover specific state-by-state trial procedures or long-term dependency court strategy.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Parental rights and CPS procedures vary significantly by state. If the allegations involve potential criminal charges or the immediate removal of a child, prioritize speaking with a qualified family defense or criminal defense attorney.
