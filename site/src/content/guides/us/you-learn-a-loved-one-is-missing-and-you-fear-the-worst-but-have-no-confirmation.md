# What to do if… you learn a loved one is missing and you fear the worst but have no confirmation (USA)

## Short answer
**Call law enforcement immediately to file a missing person report.** There is no "24-hour waiting period" in the United States, and reporting early is the most critical factor for a successful recovery or resolution.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait for a specific time limit to pass; police can and should take reports immediately if the disappearance is out of character.
- Don't assume an adult's "right to disappear" prevents you from reporting serious safety concerns or medical risks.
- Don't post unconfirmed rumors, "death announcements," or specific sensitive identifiers (like home addresses or medical diagnoses) on social media.
- Don't pursue leads or confront suspects yourself; this can compromise the investigation or put you in danger.
- Don't delete any digital evidence, including call logs, recent texts, "last seen" timestamps, or location-sharing history.
- Don't pay anyone claiming to be a "private locator" or "ransom agent" without law enforcement approval, as scams target families in crisis.

## What to do now
1. **Contact law enforcement immediately.**
   - **Call 911** if there is evidence of violence, a medical emergency, a child is involved, or you believe the person is in immediate physical danger.
   - Otherwise, call your **local police or sheriff’s non-emergency line** to file a formal missing person report.
   - Ask for the **Case Number**, the **Officer’s Name**, and the **NCIC (National Crime Information Center) number** once it is generated.
2. **State specific "At-Risk" factors clearly.**
   Tell the officer if the person is a minor, an older adult, has dementia / cognitive impairment, requires life-sustaining medication (e.g., insulin), is in mental health distress, or has been threatened by a domestic partner. High-risk status changes how law enforcement prioritizes the case.
3. **Verify NCIC entry for anyone under 21.**
   Under federal law (Suzanne's Law), law enforcement **must** enter anyone under the age of 21 into the NCIC Missing Person File within two hours of the report. Confirm with the officer that this entry has been made.
4. **Compile a "Police-Ready" information packet.**
   Gather the following details immediately for the investigator:
   - Legal name, aliases, and a very recent, clear color photograph.
   - Physical description (height, weight, hair/eye color, tattoos, scars, or piercings).
   - Clothing last seen in and details of their vehicle (make, model, color, license plate).
   - Last known location, time of contact, and a list of frequent hangouts or workplaces.
5. **Request institutional and forensic checks.**
   Ask law enforcement to check local **hospitals, jails, and the Medical Examiner / Coroner’s office**. Due to privacy laws, these institutions may not provide information directly to you, but they will coordinate with investigating officers.
6. **Preserve DNA and scent evidence.**
   Locate items used solely by the missing person, such as a **toothbrush, hairbrush, or unwashed clothing**. Place these in clean paper bags (not plastic) and do not touch them with your bare hands; law enforcement may need them for DNA profiling.
7. **If the missing person is a child, contact NCMEC.**
   After filing the police report, call the **National Center for Missing & Exploited Children at 1-800-THE-LOST (1-800-843-5678)**. They provide technical assistance and can help coordinate national visibility.
8. **Designate a single family spokesperson.**
   Choose one person to be the primary point of contact for law enforcement. This prevents fragmented information and ensures you do not miss critical updates from the investigator.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to hire a private investigator or launch a public media campaign in the first few hours.
- You do not need to notify every extended family member or acquaintance immediately; focus on the police and immediate "last seen" contacts first.
- You do not need to handle their financial accounts, cancel leases, or move their belongings until law enforcement provides clear guidance.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a paralyzing sense of dread or confusion when a loved one is missing. By moving through these structured steps—reporting, documenting, and preserving evidence—you are giving law enforcement the best possible tools to find them. Taking action is a way to manage the uncertainty of the first few hours.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and reporting in the first hours of a disappearance. Long-term investigative procedures and legal requirements vary by state and local jurisdiction.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and is not legal advice. Reporting a missing person does not guarantee an immediate search; law enforcement prioritizes cases based on "at-risk" criteria and local department policies.
