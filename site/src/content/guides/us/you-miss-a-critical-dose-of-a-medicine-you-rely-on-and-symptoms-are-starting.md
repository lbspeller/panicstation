# What to do if… you miss a critical dose of a medicine you rely on and symptoms are starting (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your pharmacist or prescribing clinician immediately for a professional dosing decision. If you experience severe symptoms like chest pain, trouble breathing, or seizures, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take a double dose to "catch up" unless a healthcare professional specifically instructs you to.
- Do not take an extra dose if you are uncertain whether you already took the scheduled one.
- Do not use alcohol, OTC sedatives, or other medications to mask emerging symptoms.
- Do not wait for the next scheduled dose if symptoms are worsening or involve vital functions.

## What to do now
1. **Identify emergency triggers for immediate intervention.** Call **911** if you experience:
   - difficulty breathing or chest pain
   - a seizure or uncontrolled shaking
   - sudden confusion or fainting
   - a severe allergic reaction (swelling of face/tongue, hives)
   - any symptom that is rapidly worsening or prevents you from standing.
2. **Stop physical activity and find a safe place to sit or lie down.** This prevents injury from falls if the missed dose causes dizziness, weakness, or sudden changes in blood pressure or heart rate.
3. **Contact your pharmacist or prescriber immediately for medication-specific instructions.** 
   - Call your **pharmacy** first; pharmacists can provide immediate clinical guidance on whether to take the dose now or wait.
   - If the pharmacy is closed, call your **prescriber's office** and request the on-call clinician or the urgent nurse line.
   - State clearly: the name of the medication, the strength, the time the dose was missed, and the specific symptoms you are currently feeling.
4. **Follow the "missed dose" instructions on the official medication label or insert if you cannot reach a clinician.** 
   - Most labels specify a timeframe (e.g., "Take as soon as remembered unless it is within 4 hours of the next dose").
   - If you have an established "Action Plan" for this specific condition (e.g., an Asthma Action Plan or Diabetes Management Plan), follow the "Symptom Return" section exactly.
5. **Use Poison Control for guidance if you are unsure how much you took or if you took an extra dose by mistake.** Call **1-800-222-1222** or use the **webPOISONCONTROL** online tool. They provide free, confidential expert guidance 24/7 on potential toxicity or dosing errors.
6. **Request an emergency supply if the missed dose is due to an empty prescription.** 
   - Ask the pharmacist about "emergency refill" protocols (sometimes called Kevin's Law in certain states).
   - Pharmacists may be able to dispense a 72-hour or small emergency supply of maintenance medications while waiting for doctor authorization.
7. **Document your timeline for the clinician.** 
   - Write down the time of the last confirmed dose.
   - List the time symptoms started and what they are.
   - Note any other substances (food, other meds) taken since the missed dose.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine why the dose was missed (e.g., fixing a routine or alarm) right now.
- You do not need to make long-term changes to your treatment plan until you are stabilized.
- You do not need to finish non-essential tasks or chores while symptomatic; these can wait until a clinician confirms you are safe.

## Important reassurance
Missing a medication dose is a common occurrence that healthcare professionals are trained to manage daily. Panic is a standard physiological response when symptoms return, but professional guidance from a pharmacist or 911 operator can provide a concrete path to stabilization.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate minutes and hours following a missed dose with emerging symptoms. Long-term medical management and dosing adjustments must be handled by your primary healthcare provider.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Professional clinical judgment from a pharmacist or doctor is required for all medication dosing decisions.
