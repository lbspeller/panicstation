# What to do if… you are told a prepaid funeral plan exists but cannot find the provider details (USA)

## Short answer
Keep any new funeral purchases limited and reversible where possible while you look for a payment, contract, or policy trail. Use the official NAIC locator if the arrangement may have been funded through life insurance, and check official state unclaimed-property searches as an additional route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay a fee to a private locator or anyone promising a quick recovery.
- Don’t share the deceased person’s Social Security number, ITIN, or documents with unsolicited callers or through links you did not independently verify.
- Don’t assume the missing arrangement was one specific type of product. It may have been a preneed contract, a trust-funded arrangement, or an insurance-funded arrangement.
- Don’t delay care of the remains or other time-sensitive arrangements while you search. When dealing with a funeral home, ask for itemized prices and keep choices limited where possible.
- Don’t decide that anyone was scammed just because the paperwork is missing.

## What to do now
1. **Start one notes page.** Write down the deceased person’s full legal name, prior names, date of birth, date of death, last address, previous states of residence, and the name of the person who mentioned the plan.
2. **Do a first pass for paperwork.** Check estate papers, files, old mail, scanned documents, email, and text messages that you are entitled to review. Search for terms such as `funeral`, `preneed`, `pre-need`, `burial`, `cremation`, `policy`, `premium`, `trust`, and `beneficiary`.
3. **Look for a payment trail.** Check any statements you are entitled to review for payments to a funeral home, cemetery, insurer, or unfamiliar company. If you need help identifying a payment, contact the bank or card provider and ask what documentation is required before they can discuss the account.
4. **Use the official NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator if insurance may be involved.** The free tool is for possible life insurance policies and annuity contracts. It asks for details from the death certificate, including the deceased person’s Social Security number or ITIN, legal name, date of birth, and date of death. Submit sensitive details only through the official NAIC route.
5. **Check official unclaimed-property routes.** Search each state where the person lived or did business. Use an official state search or the NAUPA gateway. NAUPA also links to a multi-state search for participating states. Treat this as an additional check, not a complete search for every possible arrangement.
6. **Follow the clue to the relevant state office.** If the trail points to insurance, use the official NAIC directory to contact the state insurance department. If it points to a funeral home, cemetery, preneed contract, trust, or escrow arrangement, ask the relevant official state licensing or consumer-protection office which agency oversees it and whether a verification route exists.
7. **Contact any named business through independently verified details.** Ask the funeral home, cemetery, insurer, or other named provider to search under the deceased person’s legal name, prior names, and previous addresses. Ask for the response in writing where possible.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether anyone was scammed.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint before you have gathered the basic names, dates, documents, and payment clues.
- You do not need to settle every funeral choice while the funding question is unclear. Keep decisions limited and reversible where possible.

## Important reassurance
Missing paperwork does not by itself show that the arrangement is gone or that anyone was scammed. Checking the payment trail, the official NAIC locator, and state routes can narrow the next step without forcing every decision at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the relevant state office, the provider, or a qualified specialist, depending on the type of arrangement and the state involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Only share sensitive personal information through official channels that you independently verify and initiate.
