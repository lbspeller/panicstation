# What to do if… police ask to take a quick look inside your home as part of an inquiry (USA)

## Short answer
Do not consent to entry or a search just because the request sounds informal. Keep the door closed, verify who is contacting you, ask whether they have a warrant, and clearly say that you do not consent; if officers enter or say they will enter, do not physically resist.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door wide, step aside, or act in a way that could make it unclear whether you invited entry.
- Do not agree to a search just to end the conversation.
- Do not physically block, push, or interfere with officers if they enter or say they have authority to enter.
- Do not guess, joke, or volunteer details about the inquiry at the door or on the phone.
- Do not voluntarily unlock doors, rooms, or containers just to speed things up.
- Do not argue with another resident if they respond differently.

## What to do now
1. **Create a clear boundary.** Speak through the closed door if officers are outside. If the contact is by phone or text, do not agree to a visit or entry during that conversation.
2. **Verify the contact.** Ask for the person’s name, badge number, department or agency, and a brief explanation of why they are contacting you. If the contact is by phone or text, independently find the agency’s public phone number and call it to confirm the contact.
3. **Ask whether they have a warrant.** If they say yes, ask them to hold it up to a window or slide it under the door if that is practical. Look for the address, the place described, and the items or people described. Do not try to resolve every legal question at the door.
4. **State non-consent clearly.** Say: “I do not consent to entry or a search.” You do not need to explain your reason.
5. **Speak calmly with other residents.** If another resident is present, state your own non-consent clearly and ask them not to invite entry. Rules about shared authority and another resident’s response can be fact-specific, so tell a lawyer exactly who was present and what each person said.
6. **If officers insist on entering, do not physically resist.** Say: “I do not consent, but I am not resisting.” Keep your hands visible, avoid sudden movements, and do not argue about legal exceptions in the moment.
7. **Make a record when it is safe.** Write down the time, names, badge numbers, agency, what was said, where officers went, and what they handled or took. Ask for a copy of any warrant and a receipt or inventory for property taken. Procedures can vary, so ask how to obtain paperwork if it is not provided.
8. **Use clear words if officers detain or arrest you.** Say: “I am going to remain silent. I want a lawyer.” Do not answer questions beyond identifying information that local law may require.
9. **Contact a lawyer as soon as you safely can.** Tell the lawyer what happened, whether officers showed a warrant, whether anyone agreed to entry, and whether anything was taken.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to give a full statement or answer detailed questions.
- You do not need to work out every possible exception to the warrant requirement at the door.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to challenge what happened.
- You do not need to prove that you are being helpful or cooperative by agreeing to a quick look.

## Important reassurance
An informal request for a quick look can create pressure to answer immediately. It is reasonable to pause, keep the boundary clear, state that you do not consent, and get legal advice.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Questions about a warrant, an entry without consent, another resident’s response, detention, arrest, or property taken can be fact-specific and may need a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Search-and-seizure rules can vary by state and situation, and there are exceptions to warrant requirements in some circumstances. Prioritize safety, state non-consent clearly, avoid physical resistance, and seek legal advice promptly.
