# What to do if… a partner pressures you to have sex as a condition for basic affection, attention, or kindness (USA)

## Short answer
You do not owe sexual access in exchange for basic kindness or affection. Create physical distance from the pressure, prioritize your immediate safety, and connect with a confidential advocate for support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in sexual activity "to make the pressure stop" if you feel coerced, afraid, or trapped.
- Do not feel required to provide a list of reasons for saying no; your lack of consent is sufficient.
- Do not stay in a conversation where your boundaries are being treated as a negotiation or debate.
- Do not let a partner isolate you or take your communication devices during or after a refusal.
- Do not assume that being in a relationship or marriage makes this behavior acceptable or "not serious."

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause immediately.** Move to a different room, step outside, or go to a public space where you can breathe and think without being monitored or pressured.
2. **State your boundary clearly once and stop the interaction.** Use a direct statement such as "I am not having sex, and I need you to stop asking."
3. **Keep your essentials with you.** Ensure you have your phone, keys, and wallet nearby in case the situation escalates and you need to leave the premises quickly.
4. **Contact a confidential specialist for support.** Reach out to **RAINN (National Sexual Assault Hotline)** to talk through what is happening and learn about local resources:
   - Call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**
   - Text **HOPE** to **64673**
   - Use the **online chat at rainn.org**
5. **Contact domestic violence support if this is part of a pattern of control.** The **National Domestic Violence Hotline** provides safety planning and support without requiring you to report to police:
   - Call **800-799-SAFE (7233)**
   - Text **START** to **88788**
6. **If you believe your device is being monitored, take precautions.** Use a "Private" or "Incognito" browser window, clear your history after searching for help, or use a trusted friend's phone or a public library computer.
7. **Identify a safe person to talk to.** Reach out to a trusted friend or family member and ask if they can stay on the phone with you or if you can go to their home for the night.
8. **Call 911 if you are in immediate physical danger.** If your partner is physically blocking you from leaving, threatening you, or if you fear for your safety, treat it as an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to end the relationship.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a police report tonight.
- You do not need to find the "perfect" words to describe your experience.
- You do not need to convince your partner that their behavior is harmful.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured or coerced into sex by an intimate partner is a violation of your autonomy. If you have "gone along with it" in the past to keep the peace or avoid a partner's anger, that is a common survival response and does not mean you consented or that the behavior is your fault.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction only. It focuses on ending the immediate pressure and finding confidential support. Long-term safety planning should be done with the help of a professional advocate.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and is not legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, contact 911. Confidential hotlines are the safest way to get personalized safety planning.
