# What to do if… someone in a public place is hovering close and watching your phone or bag (USA)

## Short answer
Move toward staff, security, or other people while bringing your phone and closed bag close to your body. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger or need immediate police assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep scrolling while someone is close enough to reach your phone.
- Don’t open your bag or wallet in the open to check whether something is missing.
- Don’t confront, argue with, follow, or chase the person.
- Don’t set your bag down or hang it on a chair while you deal with the situation.
- Don’t hand your phone to a stranger who offers help with directions, translation, a photo, or an online search.
- Don’t block the person’s path or make physical contact.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your phone and bag.**  
   Stop scrolling. Put your phone away unless you are using it to call for help. Close your bag and hold it in front of your body. Keep your wallet, keys, and phone out of easy reach.

2. **Move toward a safer pause.**  
   Walk into a staffed store, move to a staffed counter, approach security, or stand near other people. In a transit area, look for an employee, staffed booth, or help point.

3. **Change position without stopping to work out the person’s intent.**  
   Step sideways, change direction, move toward a staffed counter, or place a barrier between you and the person if one is available. The aim is to create distance and limit access to your belongings.

4. **Reduce access from behind.**  
   Keep your bag in front of you. Avoid standing where someone can remain directly behind you while you are distracted. A wall, pillar, counter, or group of people may help you create space.

5. **Use a brief boundary only if it feels safe.**  
   Say, “Please step back,” then continue moving toward staff or other people. You do not need to explain or continue the interaction.

6. **Ask for help and report the situation at the right level.**  
   Tell venue staff or security what you noticed. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger or the situation requires immediate police assistance. If there is no emergency, contact the local law enforcement agency where the incident happened. The local agency may have a non-emergency phone number or online reporting option.

7. **Check for missing items only after reaching a safer place.**  
   Check for your phone, wallet, keys, and cards near staff or security rather than rummaging through your bag in the open. Do not chase anyone. If a payment card is missing, contact the card issuer promptly once you are safe.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide immediately whether the behavior was suspicious, rude, or criminal.
- You don’t need to give a perfect description before moving to a safer place.
- You don’t need to confront the person or work out whether anyone else was involved.
- Account changes, insurance questions, and a detailed written report can wait until you are safe and able to focus.
- If a payment card is missing, reporting it to the issuer should happen promptly once you are safe.

## Important reassurance
You are allowed to create space and move toward help before you know exactly what is happening. Securing your belongings and leaving the immediate area is a calm response to uncertainty.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and stabilize the moment. Later decisions, such as reporting a theft, replacing items, securing accounts, or seeking support after a crime, may need additional help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, or other professional advice. Call 911 for an emergency requiring immediate assistance. Local reporting procedures vary, so ask venue staff or the local law enforcement agency for the appropriate non-emergency route when the situation is not urgent.
