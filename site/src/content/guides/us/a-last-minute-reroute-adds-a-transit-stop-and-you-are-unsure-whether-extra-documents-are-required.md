# What to do if… a last-minute reroute adds a transit stop and you are unsure whether extra documents are required (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the entry requirements for the new transit country immediately; if the stop is in the US, you must have a valid ESTA, B-1 / B-2 visa, or C-1 transit visa, as there is no "sterile transit" without clearing immigration.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you can stay in the "sterile" gate area during a US stop — everyone landing in the US must be legally admissible and clear border control.
- Do not wait until you reach the connection airport to check documents, as airlines will deny boarding at your current location if you lack the required authorization.
- Do not rely on travel forums or third-party blogs for visa rules; use only official government domains (.gov) or the airline's verified "Timatic" check.
- Do not accept a reroute with a "self-transfer" or "separate tickets" unless you are certain you have the right to enter the transit country and clear customs.
- Do not attempt to board if the gate agent flags a document deficiency — this can lead to being stranded in a third country without support.

## What to do now
1. **Document every detail of the new itinerary.** **Take screenshots of the updated flight numbers, arrival and departure airports, and the exact duration of the layover.**
2. **Determine if the transit requires physical entry into the country.** **Check if you are required to change airports, stay overnight, or collect and re-check baggage, all of which mandate full immigration clearance.**
3. **Verify your US admissibility status immediately if the stop is on US soil.** **Confirm you have an approved ESTA (current fee is $40.27) or a valid B-1 / B-2 or C-1 visa, as the US does not allow transit without these documents for most nationalities.**
4. **Use the official Mobile Passport Control (MPC) app to prepare for the arrival.** **Download the CBP MPC app to submit your travel information digitally, which can significantly reduce the time spent in the immigration queue during a tight connection.**
5. **Confirm baggage handling procedures with the gate agent.** **Ask explicitly if your bags are "checked through" or if you must retrieve them at the first US port of entry to pass through customs before re-dropping them.**
6. **Request a formal document check from the airline staff.** **Ask the agent to run your passport and the new itinerary through their compliance system (Timatic) and provide a verbal or written confirmation that you are "cleared to fly."**
7. **Evaluate the risk of a last-minute ESTA application.** **If you do not have authorization and the 72-hour window has passed, ask the airline to reroute you through a country with "sterile transit" or visa-free rules for your nationality instead of waiting for a pending approval.**
8. **Secure a written record of the reroute reason.** **Obtain a printout or email from the airline stating that the change was involuntary, which may assist with claims for visa fees or alternative flight costs later.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to update your final destination hotel or transport until you have confirmed you can board the transit leg.
- You do not need to research the local laws of the transit country beyond the immediate immigration and customs requirements.
- You do not need to pay for expedited visa services from third-party sites; official channels are the only way to ensure valid entry.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when an airline changes your path without your input. These document requirements are technical and binary; once you have the "cleared to fly" confirmation from the airline's system, the uncertainty is resolved.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unexpected transit requirements. It does not cover long-term visa applications, complex immigration appeals, or specific requirements for refugee travel documents or diplomatic passports.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or immigration advice. Entry requirements are subject to change based on nationality, travel history, and current government policy. Airlines have the final authority to deny boarding based on their interpretation of document requirements.
