# What to do if… you are told your resignation notice period has been changed after you handed it in (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid immediate verbal or written admissions of fault; instead, request all allegations and questions in writing and secure a specific window to review records before providing a factual, non-speculative response.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a narrative or guess at causes you cannot personally verify.
- Do not use phrases like "this was my fault" or "I am responsible" in an attempt to de-escalate the immediate pressure.
- Do not delete, modify, rename, or "clean up" any logs, emails, or files related to the incident.
- Do not export company data to personal email accounts, cloud storage, or physical drives for "safekeeping."
- Do not use company-owned devices to research legal advice or personal employment rights.
- Do not sign any "interview summaries" or "stipulations of fact" that contain inaccuracies or incomplete context.

## What to do now
1. **Request all inquiries and allegations in writing.**  
   **If asked for a verbal response, state that you want to ensure accuracy and request the specific questions be sent via email.**
   - Ask for the specific timeframes, systems, and data types involved in the allegation.
   - Ask for a clear deadline for the written response.

2. **Secure a fact-checking window.**  
   **Formally request a defined period (e.g., 24 to 48 hours) to review your own calendars, sent emails, and task logs.**
   - Do not agree to a "now or never" response if it requires verifying technical details you cannot recall under pressure.

3. **Preserve evidence in its original location.**  
   **Stop any automated deletion or archiving protocols you have control over regarding the relevant files.**
   - If you believe critical logs are at risk of being overwritten, notify the IT or Security department in writing that these records must be preserved for the investigation.

4. **Verify the reporting path.**  
   **Check your employee handbook or intranet for the official "Incident Reporting" or "Whistleblower" procedure.**
   - Route your factual observations through the formal security or privacy office rather than just your immediate supervisor if they are the one making the allegation.

5. **Draft a minimalist factual statement.**  
   **When providing the initial response, include only externally verifiable events.**
   - Include the date and time you became aware of the incident.
   - List the specific actions you took (e.g., "I accessed the file at 2:00 PM as part of the X project").
   - State clearly which information you have not yet been able to verify.

6. **Invoke representation rights if applicable.**  
   **If you are a union member, clearly state your request for a representative before any investigatory interview begins.**
   - If you are not in a union, be aware that while NLRB 2026 priorities aim to extend "Weingarten rights" to all employees, your employer may currently deny a request for a coworker to attend; document the request and the denial in writing.

7. **Differentiate between company and personal legal counsel.**  
   **Remember that company attorneys represent the interests of the corporation, not you personally.**
   - If the situation involves potential legal liability, seek an independent consultation with an employment attorney before signing a formal affidavit.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or accept a disciplinary deal during the first meeting.
- You do not need to determine the legal "severity" of the breach or the required regulatory notifications; that is the role of the Privacy Office.
- You do not need to "fix" the security vulnerability yourself unless you are part of the authorized remediation team.
- You do not need to defend your general character or work history; keep the focus on the specific data event.

## Important reassurance
An "immediate" demand for an explanation is a standard corporate risk-management tactic and does not automatically mean your employment is terminated. Taking a brief pause to ensure your statement is factually accurate is a protective measure for both you and the organization's eventual regulatory reporting.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization during the first few hours of an allegation. It does not cover formal HR grievance procedures, litigation strategy, or long-term career planning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employee rights in the US vary significantly by state (especially in "at-will" jurisdictions), specific employment contracts, and collective bargaining agreements. If you are asked to misrepresent facts or destroy evidence, seek independent legal counsel immediately.
