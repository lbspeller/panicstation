# What to do if… your low-beam headlights stop working and you realise before setting off in the dark (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive on public roads after dark or in low-visibility conditions without functioning low-beam headlights. Abandon your current driving plans and secure alternate transportation or wait for daylight to resolve the fault.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to drive using only Daytime Running Lights (DRLs) or parking lights; they do not provide sufficient illumination or rear-end visibility for night driving.
- Don't use high beams as a constant substitute for failed low beams, as this blinds other drivers and is illegal in most states when following or meeting traffic.
- Don't assume you can finish a trip before it gets "too dark" if the sun is already setting.
- Don't attempt to "jiggle" wiring or perform complex electrical diagnostics in a dark parking lot or near active traffic.
- Don't drive the vehicle to a repair shop at night; wait for daylight or use a towing service.

## What to do now
1. **Acknowledge that the vehicle is currently legally unroadworthy for night use.** **Federal and state laws (such as FMVSS 108) require functioning low-beam headlights from sunset to sunrise and during periods of reduced visibility.** Even if high beams work, you cannot safely or legally complete a standard trip without low beams.

2. **Verify the failure is not a simple switch or setting error.** **Turn the headlight control from "AUTO" to the manual "ON" position to rule out a sensor malfunction.** 
   - Check if the dashboard illumination or "headlights on" indicator is active.
   - Walk to the front of the car to confirm both lamps are physically dark.
   - Check if the high beams function when the lever is pulled; if they work, the issue is likely isolated to the low-beam bulbs, fuses, or relay.

3. **Treat a double-failure as a systemic electrical issue.** **If both low beams failed at the exact same time, it is statistically more likely to be a fuse, relay, or switch problem than two bulbs burning out simultaneously.** Stop cycling the switch repeatedly, as this can cause further heat damage to a failing relay or wiring.

4. **Perform a single, safe check of the lighting fuse if you have the manual.** **Locate the fuse box (usually under the hood or dash) and identify the fuse labeled for "Low Beam," "Headlamp," or "Lo-Beam."** 
   - Only attempt a replacement if you have the exact same amperage spare fuse.
   - If the new fuse blows immediately upon turning the lights on, stop immediately; there is a short circuit that requires a professional.

5. **Secure an alternate method of transportation for your immediate needs.** **Use a rideshare service, public transit, or a different vehicle rather than risking a "lights out" drive.** If you are away from home, contact roadside assistance (e.g., AAA or your insurance provider) to arrange a tow to a safe location or repair facility.

6. **Relocate the car only if it is currently in an unsafe or illegal position.** **Only move the vehicle within the immediate parking lot or driveway to a secure spot.** If you must move it a very short distance on a public road to reach safety, do so only if there is still enough ambient light to be seen clearly by others.

7. **Plan for a daytime repair or mobile mechanic.** **Schedule a technician to look at the car during daylight hours when visibility for repairs is high.** If you plan to DIY the bulb replacement, wait until morning to ensure you do not drop or break the delicate glass components in the dark.

## What can wait
- You don't need to know why the lights failed tonight; the cause (bulbs vs. wiring) is irrelevant to the immediate safety decision.
- You don't need to find an open mechanic immediately if the car is parked in a safe, legal spot.
- You don't need to worry about long-term electrical costs until a basic bulb and fuse check is completed in daylight.

## Important reassurance
Finding a safety failure right before a trip is frustrating, but realizing it *before* you are on a dark highway is a successful safety check. Choosing not to drive is a controlled decision that prevents a high-risk emergency later.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on immediate hazard mitigation and decision-making for a pre-trip lighting failure. It does not provide technical repair instructions for specific vehicle makes or models.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional mechanical advice. Vehicle lighting laws vary by state, including specific "wipers on, lights on" mandates and sunset/sunrise buffers. Always consult your vehicle's owner manual for fuse and bulb specifications.
