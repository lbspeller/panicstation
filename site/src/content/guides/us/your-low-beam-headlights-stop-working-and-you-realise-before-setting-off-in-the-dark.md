# What to do if… your low-beam headlights stop working and you realise before setting off in the dark (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start a night trip with failed low-beam headlights. Change the travel plan, get help, or wait for daylight rather than trying to make public-road driving work with broken lighting.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rely on daytime running lights, parking lights, or fog lights as a substitute for working headlights.
- Don’t use high beams as a workaround for failed low beams; they can glare into other road users and are restricted in many situations.
- Don’t start the trip hoping you can “beat the sunset.”
- Don’t keep driving around to test the fault on public roads after dark.
- Don’t attempt electrical work in the dark, in traffic, or anywhere you cannot stand safely.
- Don’t replace a fuse with a higher-amperage fuse.

## What to do now
1. **Treat this car as not available for night driving right now.**  
   State rules vary, and official state driver handbooks may require headlights during darkness, poor visibility, and weather conditions that require wipers. If the journey involves darkness or poor visibility, switch plans.

2. **Check that the low beams are actually selected.**  
   While parked, turn the headlight control fully to “ON” instead of relying on “AUTO.” Check the dashboard indicator, then briefly test low beams, high beams, hazard lights, and turn signals so you know what is and is not working.

3. **Look from outside the car if it is safe.**  
   Stand away from traffic, ask another person to operate the switch, or face the car toward a wall or garage door. Confirm whether both low-beam headlights are out, only one side is out, or the lights are dim or flickering.

4. **If both low beams are out, assume a shared fault until checked.**  
   Two bulbs can fail, but both low beams failing at once may also be a fuse, relay, switch, connector, or wiring issue. Do not repeatedly flip switches or tap wiring to force the lights on.

5. **Only do a simple manual-based check.**  
   Use the owner’s manual to locate the low-beam fuse if it is easy and safe to reach. If a fuse is blown and you have the correct spare, replace it only with the same amperage; if it blows again or you are unsure, stop and get help.

6. **Choose a help route that avoids night driving.**  
   Call roadside assistance, your insurer’s assistance line, a mobile mechanic, a repair shop, or someone who can give you a ride. Say plainly: “My low-beam headlights are not working and I have not started the trip.”

7. **If the car needs moving, keep it off public roads where possible.**  
   Move it only within a driveway, garage, parking lot, or private property if that is safe. If it is in a genuinely unsafe position, use hazard lights, keep the movement as short as possible, and prioritize getting it to a safe stopped place or getting professional help.

8. **Use different transport for tonight.**  
   Take another vehicle with working lights, get a ride, use a taxi or rideshare, use public transit, or delay the trip.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact electrical fault tonight.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop immediately if the car can stay parked safely.
- You do not need to decide whether it is a bulb, fuse, relay, switch, or wiring fault before you have arranged safe transport.
- You do not need to solve the whole journey problem with this car right now.

## Important reassurance
Catching this before setting off is a good stop point. The immediate problem is not fixing the car perfectly; it is avoiding a dark-road trip without the lighting other drivers expect to see.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Vehicle repair, state-specific legal questions, insurance issues, or roadside recovery decisions may need help from a qualified technician, roadside provider, insurer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, mechanical, insurance, or professional advice. Headlight rules and enforcement vary by state and situation; if you cannot confidently meet the lighting requirements for the conditions, do not drive until the problem is fixed.
