# What to do if… you are asked to identify personal items for return but are unsure what belongs to whom (USA)

## Short answer
Do not guess or confirm ownership while you are unsure. Ask the facility or agency to pause the handoff if its policy allows, keep the items in its custody for now, and explain its written identification and release process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take a mixed bag to sort out later if you are unsure what belongs to whom.
- Do not sign a statement confirming ownership if you cannot verify it.
- Do not post photographs of identification, keys, cards, prescriptions, or personal documents on social media.
- Do not discard, distribute, or give away uncertain items.
- Do not agree to a rushed handoff without asking for any deadline and the written policy.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a pause.** Say: “I am not sure which items belong to whom. Please pause the handoff and tell me your written process for identifying and releasing personal items.”
2. **Ask for a written itemized list.** Ask whether the holder can provide a list of the items, the current holding location, and a reference number. For valuables or easily confused items, ask whether distinctive details are available.
3. **Ask whether photographs or a viewing appointment are available.** If the holder cannot share photographs, ask whether you can view the items without taking them while they remain in its custody.
4. **Identify only what you are confident about.** Note any unfamiliar or missing items. Use recent photographs, receipts, or a description from someone who saw the items recently.
5. **Ask who may receive the items.** Rules can vary by state, organization, and circumstances. Ask which identification or documents are required and what happens if more than one person makes a claim.
6. **Flag sensitive items and act on anything missing.** Ask how any wallet, identification document, bank card, key, phone, or prescription is being secured and recorded. If a bank card is missing or may be in the wrong hands, contact the issuer promptly through a verified contact route. If an identification document or personal information appears missing or exposed, use the official replacement or identity-theft guidance in the resources below. If a document appears to belong to someone else, tell staff and leave it with them.
7. **Record the contact.** Write down the date, the office or department, the name of the person you spoke with, any reference number, any deadline, and what was agreed. If a medical examiner, coroner, or law enforcement agency holds the items, ask that office for its written personal-effects release process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to divide up belongings now.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements today.
- You do not need to decide what to keep, donate, or discard.
- You do not need to solve every estate-related task before asking for a pause.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to stop when you are unsure. A careful pause can prevent an avoidable mix-up and give the holder time to explain the correct process.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about competing claims, estate administration, or unresolved missing items may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state and by organization. Ask the holder to follow its written policy and explain any identification or documents it requires.
