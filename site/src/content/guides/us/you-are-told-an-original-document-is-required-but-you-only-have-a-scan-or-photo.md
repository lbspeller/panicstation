# What to do if… you are told an original document is required but you only have a scan or photo (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the requester to confirm in writing whether they need the physical original, an original digital file, or an issuer-certified copy. If the document will be used abroad, also ask whether an apostille or authentication certificate is required after you have the correct document form.

## Do not do these things
- Do not alter the content of the photo or scan, or crop away seals, stamps, signatures, or the reverse side.
- Do not assume a notarized photocopy becomes an original or an issuer-certified copy.
- Do not add a notarization to an original government-issued record unless the official instructions for that document specifically require the step.
- Do not detach staples, seals, or attached certificates from an official document.
- Do not send your only original until you have checked the official submission method and return arrangements.
- Do not pay a third-party service until you know the accepted document form, the destination country, and the authority responsible for the next step.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the exact requirement in writing.**  
   Ask: “Do you need the physical original, the original digital file issued by the authority, or an issuer-certified copy?” If the document will be used abroad, ask whether an apostille or authentication certificate is also required.

2. **Identify the issuer and the document type.**  
   Write down who issued the document, such as a state or county vital-records office, a court clerk, a school registrar, a state agency, or a federal agency. Follow the written requirements for the specific application, appointment, or submission.

3. **Request the official replacement or certified copy.**  
   Contact the issuing office and ask what replacement or certified-copy option it provides. Ask whether an expedited option is available and whether the document can be sent to your current location.

4. **Treat notarization as a separate question.**  
   A notarized photocopy is not the same as an issuer-certified copy. Only use a notarized-copy route when the requester confirms in writing that it is acceptable for this document type.

5. **For use abroad, check the destination-country route.**  
   Apostilles are generally used for countries in the 1961 Hague Apostille Convention. Authentication certificates are generally used for countries outside that Convention. For a state-issued document used in a Hague Convention country, check the issuing state’s apostille route. For a state-issued document used outside the Convention, the official route generally includes authentication by the issuing state before submission to the U.S. Department of State. Federal documents follow the U.S. Department of State route. Use the official instructions for the document issuer and destination country.

6. **If an appointment or submission is imminent, ask about the next workable step.**  
   Say: “I currently have only a photo or scan. I am requesting the correct document now. Can you confirm whether there is an interim option, a rescheduling process, or a reference number I should keep?”

7. **Keep the essential records together.**  
   Save the photo or scan, the written requirement, your replacement request, payment receipt, delivery information, and any reference number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a paid service before you know the accepted document form and the correct authority.
- You do not need to decide whether notarization, an apostille, or an authentication certificate is useful until the requester and official instructions clarify the requirement.
- You do not need to solve every later administrative step now. First request the correct document form.

## Important reassurance
A photo or scan may still help you identify the document and start a replacement request. The next useful step is to get the accepted document form stated clearly and contact the issuer.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilize the situation, avoid irreversible mistakes, and route the request to the correct issuing authority. Later decisions may require specialist help depending on the document, destination country, and requesting organization.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal or other professional advice. Requirements vary by document type, destination country, requesting organization, and issuing authority. Follow the requester’s written instructions and use official issuing agencies and official apostille or authentication channels.
