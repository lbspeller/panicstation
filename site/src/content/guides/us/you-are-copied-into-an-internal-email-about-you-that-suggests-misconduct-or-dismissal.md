# What to do if… you are copied into an internal email about you that suggests misconduct or dismissal (USA)

## Short answer
Do not enter an isolated or private location alone if you feel unsafe. Request to move the meeting to a public, visible, or recorded setting immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not follow a supervisor or colleague into an isolated area such as a stairwell, basement, parked vehicle, or off-site private residence.
- Do not allow the door to be locked or the windows to be covered if you choose to remain in the room.
- Do not record the conversation secretly if you are in a "two-party consent" state, as this may be a criminal offense or lead to immediate termination.
- Do not hand over your mobile phone, bag, or car keys if requested.
- Do not sign any documents or formal statements while feeling physically or psychologically pressured.
- Do not delete evidence of the meeting request, such as calendar invites, text messages, or emails.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a populated area immediately.** If you are being led toward an isolated location, stop walking and remain in a visible area with other employees or cameras present.
2. **State a specific alternative location.** **Use a neutral, professional tone to propose a safer environment.**
   - "I am happy to have this discussion in the break room or the lobby."
   - "I would prefer to meet in a conference room with the door open."
   - "Let's move this to a video call so I can take notes effectively."
3. **Invoke your right to a safe work environment.** **Refer to the employer’s obligation to provide a workplace free from recognized hazards.**
   - State: "I do not feel safe in that location and am requesting a visible space per company safety policy."
4. **Request a witness if you are in a union.** **If you are a union member and believe the meeting is investigatory, explicitly request a representative.**
   - Say: "If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, I respectfully request that my union representative, officer, or steward be present."
   - If you are not in a union, be aware that you generally do not have a federal legal right to a witness, but you can still request HR presence as a company policy matter.
5. **Create a written trail of the refusal.** **Send a brief email or message to the person requesting the meeting and carbon copy (CC) HR.**
   - Include: "I am available to meet now in [Public Location] or via [Video Platform]. I declined the meeting in [Private Location] because I did not feel safe there."
6. **Identify and record sensory cues.** **Note specific observations that contributed to your feeling of being unsafe.**
   - Physical aggression or looming.
   - Smell of alcohol or chemicals.
   - Explicit threats or erratic behavior.
   - Unusual time or place (e.g., after-hours in a darkened wing of the building).
7. **Contact HR or Security.** **Use an internal emergency line or panic button if your workplace provides one.** Inform them that you are being pressured into an unsafe one-on-one situation.
8. **Exit the premises if the threat is immediate.** **Leave the building and go to a public place if you believe physical harm is imminent.**
9. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.** **Contact emergency services if you are being physically blocked from leaving or are being threatened with violence.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal OSHA or EEOC complaint in the next hour.
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or hire an attorney immediately.
- You do not need to explain the "why" of your safety concerns in detail to the person making you feel unsafe.

## Important reassurance
Choosing to meet in a visible location or requesting HR presence is a standard procedural step in many organizations. Hesitation or a "gut feeling" regarding physical safety in isolated areas is a common physiological response designed to prevent harm.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate workplace safety concerns. It does not cover long-term litigation, whistleblower protections, or comprehensive labor law.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Workplace rights regarding witnesses (Weingarten rights) apply primarily to unionized employees under current 2026 NLRB rulings. Private sector non-union employees generally do not have a federal right to a coworker's presence in a meeting, though they may still refuse locations that pose a physical safety risk under the OSHA General Duty Clause.
