# What to do if… police ask you to provide access to your vehicle data, dash system, or connected car app (USA)

## Short answer
State clearly that you do not consent to a search, do not provide any passcodes or biometric unlocks, and ask for a lawyer if the officer insists on access without a warrant.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a phone passcode, pattern, or biometric unlock (fingerprint/face) for any car-related app.
- Do not delete dashcam files, format SD cards, or wipe app logs after a police request has been made.
- Do not physically resist if an officer seizes a device or vehicle; state your lack of consent verbally and handle the issue through legal channels.
- Do not attempt to explain or interpret data (such as speed or location) to the officer during the stop.
- Do not sign any "Consent to Search" forms or "Property Release" waivers without legal review.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify the nature of the encounter.** Ask: "Officer, are you asking for my consent or are you ordering me to provide this access?"
2. **Verbally refuse consent for all digital and physical searches.** Say: "I do not consent to a search of my vehicle, my phone, or my digital data."
3. **Trigger "Lockdown Mode" or "SOS Mode" on your smartphone immediately.** 
   - On most devices, holding the power button and a volume button simultaneously for several seconds disables biometric unlocking and requires a manual passcode.
4. **Identify the authority for the request.** Ask: "Do you have a warrant or a court order authorizing this search?"
   - If a warrant is presented, observe what specific device (phone, dashcam, EDR) or data it names.
5. **Invoke the right to remain silent and the right to counsel.** If the officer continues to pressure you for access, say: "I am going to remain silent and I want to speak to a lawyer."
6. **Stop interacting with the device once seized.** If an officer takes your phone or pulls the SD card from a dashcam, do not offer technical assistance or passwords.
7. **Secure dashcam footage once the encounter ends.** If you are permitted to leave with your device, ensure the footage is backed up to a secure, private location without altering the original file metadata.
8. **Request a property receipt if items are seized.** If the officer takes a dashcam, SD card, or phone, ask for a written receipt that includes the officer’s name, badge number, and agency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to provide data to your insurance company or the vehicle manufacturer right now.
- You do not need to determine if the police can obtain the data directly from the service provider (e.g., OnStar, Tesla, FordPass) via a subpoena.
- You do not need to review the footage or data logs yourself while at the scene.
- You do not need to provide a statement about your driving behavior or "black box" data until you have consulted a lawyer.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when an officer makes a technical or digital request. Declining a search and asking for a warrant is a standard legal response that protects your rights; it does not constitute an admission of guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial roadside or face-to-face interactions regarding vehicle data. It does not cover long-term legal strategies for challenging warrants or responding to manufacturer data breaches.

## Important note
Rights regarding biometric unlocks (face/fingerprint) vary by jurisdiction; passcodes generally have stronger Fifth Amendment protection. This guide provides general information and is not legal advice.
