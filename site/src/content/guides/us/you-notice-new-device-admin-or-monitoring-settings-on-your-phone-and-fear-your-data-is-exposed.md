# What to do if… you notice new device admin or monitoring settings on your phone and fear your data is exposed (USA)

## Short answer
If you are in immediate danger, call 911 from the safest phone available. Otherwise, do not rush to “clean” the phone; use a safer device to contact support and make your next moves there, because changes on the suspected phone may alert the person monitoring it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person you suspect through that phone.
- Do not immediately delete apps, revoke admin access, or factory-reset the device if that could increase danger or remove information you may want support with later.
- Do not rely on incognito mode, deleted history, hidden folders, or private browsing to keep your activity safe on a device you do not trust.
- Do not use the suspected phone for safety planning, legal questions, intimate-image concerns, or private messages about leaving.
- Do not enter new passwords, backup codes, recovery codes, or intimate disclosures on the device you do not trust.
- Do not assume this is “just tech stuff” if it feels frightening, sexual, coercive, or controlling.

## What to do now
1. **Shift sensitive activity to a safer device if you can.** Use a trusted friend’s phone, a work device, a library computer, or another device the suspected person cannot access. Use that safer device for support, account changes, and anything involving intimate images or personal safety.

2. **Contact a domestic violence advocate from the safer device.** The National Domestic Violence Hotline can help you think through tech abuse, phone monitoring, stalking concerns, and safer options without pressuring you to report. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

3. **Make a simple record of what you noticed.** Write down the date, time, exact wording of any alert, the name of any unfamiliar app, and what changed. Keep it simple. You do not need to investigate the phone deeply.

4. **Treat the phone as possibly visible to the other person.** For now, avoid using it for new passwords, new secret accounts, conversations about leaving, searches about abuse or stalking, or messages about intimate images.

5. **From the safer device, secure the accounts that unlock everything else.** Start with your main email, then cloud photo storage, messaging apps, your wireless account, banking, and any account containing intimate images, backups, or shared albums. Change passwords, review logged-in devices, and remove devices you do not recognize only from the safer device.

6. **Review active sharing and linked access from the safer device where possible.** Check location sharing, family-sharing features, shared photo libraries or albums, cloud backups, smartwatch or tablet links, and recovery phone numbers or email addresses. Turn off only what you can safely change without increasing risk right now.

7. **If you use an iPhone with iOS 16 or later, consider Apple Safety Check when you are in a safer pause.** Safety Check can help you review or stop sharing information and check who has account or device access. Do not use it while someone may be watching you or able to react immediately.

8. **If you use Android, look for unfamiliar high-access apps only if it feels safe.** In Settings, you may be able to check apps, permissions, special access, or device admin apps. Menu names vary by phone and Android version. You do not have to remove anything immediately for your concern to be real.

9. **Think about the wireless plan as well as the phone.** If the other person controls or shares the wireless account, they may have access or control beyond the handset. From a safer device, check whether an advocate, carrier, or trusted support person can help you ask about separating your line safely.

10. **If intimate images or intimate data may be involved, keep the next step support-focused.** You do not need to decide right now whether to report, send takedown requests, replace the phone, or tell anyone else. An advocate can help you separate immediate safety from later choices.

11. **If you may want to report later, avoid changing the phone more than necessary until you have spoken to safe support.** That might be an advocate, lawyer, law enforcement contact, or another support person you believe is safe and not linked to the suspected abuser. A short factual record is enough for now.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to report to police, leave the relationship, replace the phone, move carriers, send takedown requests, or check every app and account. You also do not need to solve the technical side all at once.

## Important reassurance
Technology-facilitated monitoring is a known abuse tactic. It is common to second-guess yourself, feel suddenly exposed, or worry that no one will take it seriously. You are allowed to prioritize safety and support over proving exactly how it happened.

## Scope note
This is first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, device examination, image takedowns, carrier separation, protective orders, or long-term digital security may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not personal legal, technical, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. In the USA, phone monitoring by a partner or ex may be part of stalking, harassment, domestic abuse, image-based abuse, or another legal issue, depending on the facts and state law. The safest first move is usually to use a safer device, speak with an advocate, and avoid tipping off the person who may be monitoring you.
