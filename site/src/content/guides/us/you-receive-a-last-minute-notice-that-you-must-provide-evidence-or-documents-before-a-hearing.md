# What to do if… you receive a last-minute notice that you must provide evidence or documents before a hearing (USA)

## Short answer
Locate the case number on the notice and immediately contact the Clerk of Court to confirm the filing method while preparing a written motion for an extension or continuance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a phone call to the clerk or a voicemail for the judge’s assistant changes the official deadline.
- Do not fail to appear at the hearing even if you have not complied with the document request (unless you have a signed order from the judge excusing you).
- Do not alter or recreate documents to make them look like originals if the originals are missing.
- Do not file documents containing full Social Security numbers, bank account numbers, or names of minors without redacting them according to Rule 5.2.
- Do not file papers with the court without also sending a copy to the opposing party or their attorney.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the case number and the specific court branch from the header of the notice.**
   - Locate the "Case No." or "Docket No." at the top of the document.
   - Note the specific division (e.g., Family Court, Small Claims, District Court).

2. **Call the Clerk of Court’s office for that specific jurisdiction immediately.**
   - Ask for "Procedural Instructions" only (clerks cannot give legal advice).
   - Ask: "What is the accepted method for filing emergency motions for a continuance or extension of time today?"
   - Ask: "Does the judge for this case have 'Standing Orders' for discovery or evidence deadlines posted on the court website?"

3. **Verify the specific rules for extensions based on the court type.**
   - If in Federal Court: Use **Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 6(b)** for civil cases or **Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 45(b)** for criminal cases.
   - If in State/Local Court: Search the court website for "Local Rules" regarding "Motions for Continuance."

4. **Draft a "Motion for Extension of Time" or "Motion for Continuance" immediately.**
   - Include the case caption (names and case number) at the top.
   - State exactly why you cannot comply (e.g., "Received notice less than 48 hours before deadline," "Third-party records holder requires 14 days to process").
   - Request a specific new date or a specific number of days for the extension.

5. **File the motion using the court’s approved method (E-filing, email, or physical drop-off).**
   - Save the confirmation receipt or time-stamped email immediately.
   - If filing in person, bring an extra copy and ask the clerk to "Date-Stamp" it for your records.

6. **Serve the other side and file a "Certificate of Service."**
   - Send the motion to the opposing attorney (or party if they are unrepresented) via the method required by local rules.
   - File a separate one-page document with the court stating: "I hereby certify that on [Date], I served a copy of this motion on [Name] by [Method of Delivery]."

7. **Submit any documents you currently have available as a "Partial Production."**
   - File what is ready now with a cover sheet explaining that additional documents are forthcoming upon the court's approval of the extension.

8. **Contact any third-party record holders in writing.**
   - Send an urgent email or fax to the hospital, employer, or agency holding the records.
   - Keep a copy of this request to show the judge you are acting with "due diligence."

9. **Attend the hearing as scheduled unless you receive a written order granting a postponement.**
   - Bring a physical folder containing the notice, your filed motion, your proof of service, and any partial evidence you were able to gather.

## What can wait
- You do not need to finalize your entire legal argument or trial strategy right now.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator or expert witness before addressing this immediate deadline.
- You do not need to respond to the merits of the case if the current deadline only concerns document production.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a short-notice demand is a standard procedural hurdle in litigation. The court's primary concern is usually "due diligence" and "good cause." By filing a formal motion and communicating with the clerk immediately, you are following the correct legal protocol to protect your rights.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for stabilizing a last-minute document or evidence deadline. Later decisions regarding the case strategy or the admissibility of evidence may require professional legal assistance.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Court rules vary significantly by state and local jurisdiction. If you face the loss of liberty, property, or parental rights, you should seek urgent counsel from a licensed attorney or a legal aid society in your area.
