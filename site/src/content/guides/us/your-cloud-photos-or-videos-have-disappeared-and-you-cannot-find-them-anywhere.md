# What to do if… your cloud photos or videos have disappeared and you cannot find them anywhere (USA)

## Short answer
Stop doing anything that could delete, clean up, or sync more changes. Check the cloud service in a browser, look in Recently Deleted, Trash, Hidden, Archive, and shared areas, then secure the account if anything looks suspicious.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t run a cleanup, delete batches, bulk-move files, or use third-party optimizer apps while you are panicking.
- Don’t empty Trash, Bin, Recycle Bin, or Recently Deleted.
- Don’t factory reset your phone, erase a device, or reinstall photo apps as your first move.
- Don’t grant new permissions to unfamiliar recovery apps or browser extensions.
- Don’t sign out of every device immediately if you still need one device to see what is accessible.
- Don’t assume “not in the main photo feed” means “permanently gone.”

## What to do now
1. **Pause anything that may be making it worse.**
   - Stop deleting, organizing, freeing storage, or changing sync settings for now.
   - If one phone or computer appears to be actively syncing deletions, disconnect that device from the internet or pause syncing if the service offers that option.
   - Use a different trusted device or browser to check the cloud account.

2. **Check the cloud service on the web, not only in the app.**
   - Open the service in a browser: iCloud Photos, Google Photos, or OneDrive.
   - Confirm the account name and email address shown on the page.
   - If you have more than one Apple Account, Google account, or Microsoft account, check each likely account once.

3. **Look in the places where missing items often still are.**
   - Check Recently Deleted, Trash, Bin, or Recycle Bin.
   - Check Hidden or locked photo areas.
   - Check Archive in Google Photos.
   - Check Shared Albums, shared libraries, partner sharing, and OneDrive shared folders.
   - Search for a known month, place, person, file type, or filename pattern such as `IMG`, `DSC`, `MOV`, or `MP4`.

4. **Check whether backup or sync changed.**
   - For Apple Photos, check whether iCloud Photos is on for the device where the photos were expected.
   - For Google Photos, check that you are in the right Google account and whether Backup is on for the device or folder that originally held the items.
   - For OneDrive, check Camera Upload if you used it, and unlock Personal Vault before checking the Recycle Bin or searching for vault items.

5. **Restore anything you find before doing more investigation.**
   - In Apple Photos, items in Recently Deleted are usually recoverable within 30 days.
   - In Google Photos, backed-up deleted items may stay in Trash for 60 days, and some non-backed-up items may stay there for 30 days.
   - In OneDrive personal accounts, deleted items in the Recycle Bin are usually removed after 30 days.
   - Restore a small sample first if the service lets you, then confirm the files return where expected.

6. **If many OneDrive files changed suddenly, check rollback options.**
   - If you have Microsoft 365, use OneDrive’s official “Restore your OneDrive” feature to review recent activity and roll back file and folder actions within the last 30 days.
   - Do not use this until you understand that it can undo later file changes too.
   - If you are using a work or school OneDrive, contact your organization’s IT support before making a large restore.

7. **Secure the account if anything looks wrong.**
   - Review recent sign-ins, unknown devices, recovery email changes, password reset messages, and sharing changes.
   - Change the password from a trusted device.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication if it is not already on.
   - Secure the email account used for password resets.
   - Remove unfamiliar app permissions, sessions, shared links, or devices using the provider’s official account security page.

8. **If identity theft may be involved, document the basics and use official routes.**
   - Take screenshots of suspicious sign-ins, password reset emails, security alerts, and deletion activity before closing them.
   - If a bank, card issuer, insurer, or provider may be affected, contact that organization directly using a trusted route.
   - Report identity theft and get a recovery plan through IdentityTheft.gov.
   - Call 911 only for immediate danger.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to change cloud services, reorganize your library, buy more storage, deduplicate files, or build a new backup system. Those choices can wait until you have checked the web account, recovery areas, sync settings, and account security.

## Important reassurance
Missing cloud photos are often not gone forever. They may be in a different account, a web-only view, Recently Deleted, Trash, Hidden, Archive, a shared area, Personal Vault, or a device folder that stopped backing up.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and recover what is still available. Later decisions may need help from the cloud provider, your device maker, workplace IT, an identity theft resource, or a qualified data recovery professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional IT, legal, forensic, financial, or security advice. Cloud services and recovery windows can change, so use the provider’s official help pages and avoid irreversible actions while you check.
