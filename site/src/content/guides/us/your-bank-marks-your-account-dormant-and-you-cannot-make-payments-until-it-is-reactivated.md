# What to do if… your bank marks your account dormant and you cannot make payments until it is reactivated (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately via an official number to verify if the funds are still at the bank or have been transferred to the state (escheated). If the bank still holds the funds, perform a customer-initiated transaction or provide required ID to reactivate; if escheated, you must file a claim with the state treasurer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links in "account reactivation" texts or emails; these are common phishing vectors.
- Do not attempt the same failed transaction multiple times, as this may trigger additional fraud blocks or duplicate fees once the account is active.
- Do not pay a third-party "recovery" service to get your money back from the state; state unclaimed property claims are free.
- Do not assume automated interest payments or bank fees count as "activity" that resets the dormancy clock.
- Do not ignore "due diligence" mailers from your bank; these are legal warnings that escheatment is imminent.

## What to do now
1. **Call the bank using the number on the back of your debit card or an official statement.**
   - **Ask specifically if the account is "inactive" (bank-held) or "escheated" (state-held).**
   - **Request a list of specific documents needed for identity verification if a branch visit is not possible.**
2. **Perform a customer-initiated transaction if the bank confirms the account is merely inactive.**
   - **Transfer a small amount (e.g., $1.00) between accounts or make a small deposit to reset the activity timer.**
   - **Ask the representative to confirm on the call that the "dormant" flag has been removed from the system.**
3. **Identify and contact billers for any payments due in the next 48 hours.**
   - **Notify them that your account is temporarily restricted and request a 5-day grace period to avoid late fees.**
   - **Switch urgent autopayments (mortgage, rent, insurance) to a different account or a credit card temporarily.**
4. **Determine if "due diligence" letters were sent to an old address.**
   - **Verify your current mailing address with the bank to ensure you receive future legal notices.**
   - **Ask the bank to waive any "dormancy fees" or "account maintenance fees" that accrued while the account was restricted.**
5. **Contact your employer's payroll department if you have an incoming direct deposit.**
   - **Ask if the current payroll run has already been processed to your dormant account.**
   - **Provide an alternative account number for the next pay cycle if reactivation will take more than 24 hours.**
6. **Check the official state unclaimed property database if the bank confirms the account is closed.**
   - **Search the website of the State Treasurer or Unclaimed Property Administrator for the state where you lived when the account was opened.**
   - **Initiate a formal claim through the ".gov" site; you will typically need to upload a photo of your ID and proof of social security number.**
7. **File a formal complaint if the bank refuses to release funds it still holds.**
   - **Submit a report to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).**
   - **Contact the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) if it is a national bank, or the NCUA for credit unions.**

## What can wait
- Closing the account permanently (focus on access first).
- Updating all non-urgent subscription services (e.g., streaming apps).
- Investigating the bank's internal policies for why they triggered dormancy.
- Ordering a new debit card unless the bank confirms the current one is permanently deactivated.

## Important reassurance
Dormancy is a consumer protection measure designed to prevent unauthorized access to "abandoned" funds; it is not a seizure of your money. While the block on payments is immediate, the funds remain legally yours. If the bank still holds the money, reactivation is usually a matter of verifying your identity or making a single transaction.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization of a blocked account. It does not cover the long-term recovery of complex assets like safe deposit boxes or stock certificates, which follow different state-specific timelines.

## Important note
Banking regulations regarding dormancy and escheatment vary by state law. Most states require 3 to 5 years of inactivity before funds are moved. Always use verified contact methods found on your physical bank card or official monthly statements to avoid fraud.
