# What to do if… you receive an email saying your “case number” requires urgent action but you cannot verify the sender (USA)

## Short answer
**Do not click any links, open attachments, or reply to the message.** Verify the claim by contacting the alleged agency or court directly using a phone number found on an official `.gov` website.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, open PDF attachments, or scan QR codes to "view your file" or "see the warrant."
- Do not call phone numbers provided in the email or use the "reply-to" function to ask questions.
- Do not provide personal identifiers such as your Social Security Number, date of birth, or bank details to "confirm your identity."
- Do not pay any "bond," "fine," or "processing fee" via cryptocurrency, gift cards, wire transfers, or payment apps like Zelle or Venmo.
- Do not assume the email is legitimate because it features a realistic court seal, law enforcement badge, or an actual judge's name.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and isolate the message.** Your immediate priority is to prevent your device from running malicious code or your accounts from being compromised.
   - If you have already clicked a link or opened an attachment, immediately disconnect your device from the internet (turn off Wi-Fi and mobile data).
2. **Preserve a record of the communication.** **Take a screenshot of the full email, including the sender's email address and any mentioned "case number."**
   - Save this image to a separate device if possible.
   - Do not forward the email to others, as this may spread malicious links; use the screenshot for sharing or reporting.
3. **Verify through official government channels only.** **Find the official website for the court or agency named in the email using a search engine (look for the `.gov` domain).**
   - **For Federal Courts:** Use the "Court Finder" on `uscourts.gov` to find the Clerk of Court’s phone number for the specific district mentioned.
   - **For Police or Sheriffs:** Search for the official municipal or county website and call their non-emergency main line.
   - **For Federal Agencies (FBI / US Marshals):** Call the local field office listed on their verified `.gov` sites.
4. **Ask the official representative specific verification questions.** **Request the Clerk or Officer to check your name against their active systems.**
   - Ask: "Do you serve initial summons or warrants via email?" (Note: Federal courts do not serve initial summons by email).
   - Ask: "Is there an active case or warrant associated with my name?"
5. **Run a quick red-flag check.** **Identify if the email contains any of the following indicators of a scam:**
   - The sender’s email address ends in a generic domain like `@gmail.com`, `@outlook.com`, or a misspelled variation of a government site (e.g., `.org` instead of `.gov`).
   - The email demands immediate payment to avoid "imminent arrest."
   - The message asks you to keep the matter "confidential" or tells you not to contact a lawyer.
6. **Report the impersonation attempt.** **File a report with federal authorities to help track the scam campaign.**
   - Visit **ReportFraud.ftc.gov** to report the phishing attempt.
   - For scams impersonating federal courts or agencies, file a complaint at **IC3.gov** (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center).
7. **Secure your digital accounts if you interacted.** **If you entered a password or shared financial data, take immediate protective steps.**
   - Change passwords for the affected accounts from a clean, internet-connected device.
   - Contact your bank or credit card issuer using the number on the back of your card if you shared payment info or sent money.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire a lawyer before verifying if the case exists.
- You do not need to respond to the sender to "clear your name."
- You do not need to prove the email is a scam to the sender.
- You do not need to decide how to handle a legal case until its existence is confirmed by an official clerk.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden spike in heart rate or panic when seeing words like "Warrant," "Arrest," or "Urgent Legal Action." These emails are psychologically engineered to bypass your logic. Taking five minutes to verify through an independent channel is a safe, responsible action that protects you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to suspicious legal or official emails. It does not provide legal defense strategy or address long-term identity theft recovery.

## Important note
Federal and state courts generally do not serve initial legal process or warrants via email. Most official legal service is conducted via the U.S. Postal Service or in person by a process server or law enforcement officer. If you believe you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
