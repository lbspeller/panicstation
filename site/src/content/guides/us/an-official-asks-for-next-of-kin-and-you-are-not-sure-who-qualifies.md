# What to do if… an official asks for next of kin and you are not sure who qualifies (USA)

## Short answer
Ask what kind of contact they need: someone to notify, an emergency contact, or a person with legal authority. Verify the requester when needed, then give only the minimum accurate contact details you can provide and say clearly that you are unsure who legally qualifies.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess a legal order of relatives.
- Don’t assume that an emergency contact, next of kin, health care agent, and personal representative are the same thing.
- Don’t share more personal information than the request requires.
- Don’t let a family disagreement pressure you into presenting one person as the confirmed legal decision-maker.
- Don’t feel that you must resolve every legal question before giving a clearly labelled contact for an urgent notification.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what the contact is needed for.**  
   Say: **“Do you need someone to notify, an emergency contact, or the name of a person with legal authority?”**
2. **Verify the requester when needed.**  
   Ask for their name, department, and callback details. If the request is unexpected or you are unsure, call back through the organization’s published main number or the hospital’s main line.
3. **Give the minimum accurate details.**  
   Say: **“The best reachable contact I know is [name, relationship, phone number]. I am not confirming that they have legal authority.”**
4. **State uncertainty plainly.**  
   Say: **“I am not sure who legally qualifies under the relevant state rules. Please tell me what category of contact or documentation you need.”**
5. **Mention known documents without guessing.**  
   If you know that a health care proxy, advance directive, court appointment, or other relevant document names or appoints someone, give that person’s contact details and say what document you believe exists. Let the organization tell you what it needs to see.
6. **For a person who has died, separate contact details from estate authority.**  
   If the request relates to the estate, records, or an authorized representative, provide an executor, administrator, or other representative only when you know that person has been named or appointed. If you are unsure, say so.
7. **For a death-certificate request, ask the vital records office what it accepts.**  
   Requirements can vary by state. Ask what relationship, reason for request, identification, or other details are required.
8. **Keep a brief note.**  
   Write down the date, time, requester’s name, what type of contact they asked for, and which details you provided.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the full state-law hierarchy immediately.
- You do not need to settle inheritance, funeral arrangements, or long-term family disagreements during this request.
- You do not need to search for every document before giving an accurate reachable contact for notification.
- You do not need to describe someone as the legal decision-maker when you are unsure.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to be unsure. In the USA, the right contact can depend on the purpose of the request, the relevant state rules, and whether applicable law or a document gives someone authority. Clear labels and an honest statement of uncertainty help reduce misunderstandings.

## Scope note
These are first steps for responding to the immediate request. Later decisions about health care authority, records, or estate matters may require documents and state-specific guidance from the relevant organization or a qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and terminology can vary by state and by organization. It is reasonable to slow down, verify an unexpected request, and provide only the minimum accurate details needed.
